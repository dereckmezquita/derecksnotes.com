# Blog Post Brainstorm

Future topics grounded in Dereck's existing interests, voice, and trajectory. Organised by theme. Generated by Claude Opus 4.6 1M after reading the full corpus (April 2026).

## Philosophy and Reflection

These follow the conversational, first-principles style of the 2018-2019 philosophical posts but push further.

- **On Randomness** -- Guy argued that randomness does not exist. You left this thread open in "Life is Suffering?" and never came back to it. Seven years later: do you still think entropy requires randomness? Has your position on suffering changed? This is the most obviously unfinished thread in your writing.

- **On Trust** -- "On Authority" ends with an unresolved question: which authorities should one trust? You now work with AI tools, depend on bioinformatics pipelines you did not write, and use packages built by strangers. How do you decide what to trust? A practical philosophy of epistemic delegation.

- **On Building as Meaning** -- The portrait post identified a tension: you believe life is suffering, yet you have built a 1,500-file website that no one asked for. The building IS your answer to the pessimism. You have never articulated this explicitly. You should.

- **On Friendship and Thinking** -- Guy, Emmanuel -- your best ideas come from conversations with specific people. A post about intellectual friendship: what makes a good thinking partner, why friction is productive, what you have learned from specific people.

- **On Bilingualism and Thought** -- You think in at least two languages. Does the language change the thought? Do you reason differently in French vs English? Your writing already carries traces of this (surnatant, "the whole was placed at..."). Make it explicit.

## Biology and Science

These extend your wet-lab and bioinformatics background into topics you haven't covered yet.

- **What I Learned from Dissecting a Human Body** -- You mentioned this in passing ("yes I've dissected human bodies") with photos but never wrote about the experience. What does it teach you about biology, about mortality, about the gap between textbook anatomy and the real thing?

- **The Microscope as a Way of Seeing** -- You own a 1960s Optico Paris microscope. You've drawn mites, hair, onions, flies, tardigrades, and your own blood through it. A post about what changes when you look at the world at 1000x. Part essay, part photo/drawing gallery.

- **Antimicrobial Resistance: The Next Pandemic** -- You wrote about copper's antimicrobial properties. Extend this: the biology of resistance mechanisms, the evolutionary arms race, why the pipeline is drying up, and what synthetic biology might offer.

- **Synthetic Biology Five Years Later** -- Your 2019 ethics post predicted a cat-and-mouse game between scientists and regulators. Were you right? What has actually happened with CRISPR regulation, gene therapy approvals, and engineered organisms since then?

- **From Wet Lab to Dry Lab: A Migration Story** -- You trained at the bench and moved to bioinformatics. Why? What was gained and what was lost? A reflection on the two cultures of modern biology.

## Technology and Engineering

These match your builder instinct and preference for understanding systems from the ground up.

- **Why I Build My Own Infrastructure** -- You run your own servers, configure your own mail, manage your own DNS. In an era of managed services this is unusual. Why do you do it? What has it cost you? What has it taught you? A defence of self-hosting as engineering practice.

- **R is Not a Programming Language (and That's Fine)** -- You write R in a way most R users would not recognise: box::use, data.table, C-style flow. A post about what R actually is, why the tidyverse is a dialect you chose not to speak, and what it means to write R like an engineer rather than a statistician.

- **How Cryptocurrency Actually Works** -- Your DigiByte posts are some of the clearest technical writing on consensus mechanisms I've read. A more general post: how proof-of-work actually functions, what "decentralisation" means mechanically, why most people's understanding of crypto is wrong. Not advocacy -- engineering explanation.

- **The Stack Behind This Website** -- You built derecksnotes.com from code. Document the architecture: Next.js, MDX, the component system, the search, the server setup. A technical post-mortem of a decade-long personal project.

## Art, Drawing, and Photography

These extend the aesthetic thread that runs through the photo dumps, notebook dumps, and art hierarchy post.

- **Why I Shoot Film in 2026** -- A deeper exploration than the portrait section allows. The economics, the process, the specific cameras and why you chose them, what film forces you to do differently, and whether digital photographers are missing something.

- **Drawing What You See Under the Microscope** -- The tradition of scientific illustration, from Hooke's Micrographia to your own notebook. Why drawing a specimen teaches you things that photographing it does not. Pair your own microscope drawings with the historical tradition you admire.

- **Architecture as Character** -- Your notebook dumps show careful studies of buildings in Angers, cathedrals, chateaux. A post about what architectural style communicates, why you are drawn to certain buildings, and what the built environment says about the people who made it.

## Cross-Cutting / Ambitious

Posts that would draw on multiple areas of your expertise.

- **A Field Guide to First Principles** -- You think from first principles in biology, in philosophy, in code, in art. What does that actually mean as a practice? How do you do it? When does it fail? A methodological post that ties together your disparate interests.

- **What Scientists Should Learn from Engineers (and Vice Versa)** -- You are both. The biographies you wrote span both categories. What does each discipline get wrong about the other? Where do they converge?

- **The Ethics of AI-Assisted Writing** -- You used an AI to help write a post on consciousness. You asked an AI to write a portrait of yourself. Where is the line between tool and author? As someone who values attribution and intellectual honesty, this is a question you are well-positioned to explore.
