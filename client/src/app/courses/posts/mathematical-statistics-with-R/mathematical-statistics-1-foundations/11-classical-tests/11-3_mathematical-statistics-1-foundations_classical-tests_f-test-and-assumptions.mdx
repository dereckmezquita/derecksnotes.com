# Chapter 11 -- Classical Tests

## Part 3: F-Test and Checking Assumptions

Every test in Parts 1 and 2 rests on assumptions: normality, independence, equal variances, or sufficiently large sample sizes for the CLT to operate. A test result is only as trustworthy as its assumptions. In this final part we derive the F-test for comparing variances and then develop the practical skills needed to assess whether the assumptions behind our tests are plausible.

---

## 11.8 The F-Test for Equality of Variances

### 11.8.1 The Problem

Given two independent samples from normal populations:

$$X_1, \ldots, X_{n_1} \sim N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2), \quad Y_1, \ldots, Y_{n_2} \sim N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2),$$

we wish to test $H_0: \sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2$ against $H_1: \sigma_1^2 \neq \sigma_2^2$.

This is relevant in its own right (quality control, for instance) and is also a preliminary step before choosing between the pooled and Welch t-tests.

### 11.8.2 Derivation from Chi-Square Ratios

**Step 1.** From Chapter 5, we know that if $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then:

$$(n - 1)S^2 / \sigma^2 \sim \chi^2_{n-1}.$$

So for our two samples:

$$U = \frac{(n_1 - 1) S_1^2}{\sigma_1^2} \sim \chi^2_{n_1 - 1}, \quad V = \frac{(n_2 - 1) S_2^2}{\sigma_2^2} \sim \chi^2_{n_2 - 1}$$

and $U, V$ are independent (because the samples are independent).

**Step 2.** The F-distribution is defined as the ratio of two independent chi-squared random variables, each divided by its degrees of freedom:

$$F = \frac{U/d_1}{V/d_2} \sim F_{d_1, d_2}.$$

**Step 3.** Substituting and noting that under $H_0$ we have $\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2$:

$$F = \frac{S_1^2 / \sigma_1^2}{S_2^2 / \sigma_2^2} = \frac{S_1^2}{S_2^2} \cdot \frac{\sigma_2^2}{\sigma_1^2} \stackrel{H_0}{=} \frac{S_1^2}{S_2^2}.$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{F = \frac{S_1^2}{S_2^2} \sim F_{n_1 - 1, \, n_2 - 1} \quad \text{under } H_0.}$$

The test statistic is simply the **ratio of the sample variances**. Values of $F$ far from 1 (in either direction) suggest unequal population variances.

### 11.8.4 A Warning About the F-Test

The F-test for equality of variances is **extremely sensitive** to departures from normality. Even mild skewness or heavy tails can inflate the Type I error rate dramatically. For this reason, Levene's test (based on the absolute deviations from the group median) or the Brown--Forsythe test are often preferred in practice. We shall not derive them here, but you should be aware that `var.test()` is fragile.

This is also why Welch's t-test is the default in R: it does not assume equal variances, so the fragility of the F-test is sidestepped entirely.

## 11.9 Checking Assumptions: The Art of Diagnostics

### 11.9.1 Why Bother?

Statistical tests are mathematical theorems: "if assumption A holds, then the test statistic has distribution D." If assumption A fails, the theorem no longer applies, and the p-value may be meaningless. Checking assumptions is not optional -- it is the bridge between mathematical theory and credible data analysis.

The three key assumptions for the tests in this chapter are:

1. **Normality** of the parent population (or large enough $n$ for the CLT).
2. **Independence** of observations.
3. **Equal variances** (for the pooled t-test and ANOVA).

Independence is usually a design issue -- it depends on how the data were collected, not on the data themselves. We focus here on normality and equal variances, which *can* be assessed from the data.

### 11.9.2 Q-Q Plots: What They Really Are

The **quantile-quantile plot** is the single most useful graphical diagnostic in statistics, yet it is widely misunderstood. Let us explain it properly.

**The idea.** If a sample $X_{(1)} \le X_{(2)} \le \cdots \le X_{(n)}$ truly comes from a distribution $F$, then the $i$-th order statistic should be "close to" the $i$-th theoretical quantile $F^{-1}(p_i)$, where $p_i$ is the plotting position (typically $p_i = (i - 0.5)/n$).

**The construction.** A Q-Q plot has:
- **x-axis:** Theoretical quantiles $F^{-1}(p_i)$ from the reference distribution.
- **y-axis:** Sample quantiles (the ordered data values $X_{(i)}$).

If the data follow the reference distribution, the points lie on a straight line. Deviations from the line reveal *how* the data depart from the reference.

**Reading the plot:**

| Pattern | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Points on the line | Data consistent with reference distribution |
| S-shape (below, above, below) | Data have **lighter tails** (less spread) |
| Reverse S-shape (above, below, above) | Data have **heavier tails** (more spread) |
| Concave upward | Data are **right-skewed** |
| Concave downward | Data are **left-skewed** |
| Individual outliers lifting off | Possible **outliers** |

### 11.9.3 Building a Q-Q Plot from Scratch



### 11.9.5 Formal Normality Tests

While Q-Q plots give qualitative insight, formal tests provide a p-value. The **Shapiro--Wilk test** is the most powerful for moderate sample sizes:


``` r
set.seed(42)

# Test normality of each distribution
normal_sample <- rnorm(100)
heavy_sample <- rt(100, df = 3)
skew_sample <- rexp(100) - 1

data.frame(
  distribution = c("Normal", "Heavy-tailed (t3)", "Right-skewed (Exp)"),
  W_statistic = c(
    round(shapiro.test(normal_sample)$statistic, 4),
    round(shapiro.test(heavy_sample)$statistic, 4),
    round(shapiro.test(skew_sample)$statistic, 4)
  ),
  p_value = c(
    round(shapiro.test(normal_sample)$p.value, 4),
    format(shapiro.test(heavy_sample)$p.value, digits = 3),
    format(shapiro.test(skew_sample)$p.value, digits = 3)
  )
)
```

```
        distribution W_statistic  p_value
1             Normal      0.9812   0.1654
2  Heavy-tailed (t3)      0.9171 9.96e-06
3 Right-skewed (Exp)      0.8387 4.63e-09
```

A word of caution: the Shapiro--Wilk test is **very powerful** for large samples, meaning it will reject normality for trivial departures that have no practical impact on the validity of a t-test. For large $n$, rely more on the Q-Q plot (which shows *how* the data depart) than on the formal test (which only tells you *whether* they depart).

## 11.10 When Tests Fail: Robustness and Alternatives

### 11.10.1 How Robust Is the t-Test?

The t-test is often described as "robust to non-normality." Let us be precise about what this means. Through simulation, we can measure the actual Type I error rate when the normality assumption is violated.


``` r
set.seed(42)
n_sims <- 10000
sample_sizes <- c(5, 10, 20, 50, 100)
alpha <- 0.05

# Distributions to test
generate_data <- list(
  Normal = function(n) rnorm(n),
  Exponential = function(n) rexp(n) - 1,
  "t(df=3)" = function(n) rt(n, df = 3),
  Uniform = function(n) runif(n, -1, 1)
)

results <- list()
for (dist_name in names(generate_data)) {
  for (nn in sample_sizes) {
    gen_fn <- generate_data[[dist_name]]
    rejections <- replicate(n_sims, {
      samp <- gen_fn(nn)
      t.test(samp, mu = 0)$p.value < alpha
    })
    results[[length(results) + 1]] <- data.frame(
      distribution = dist_name,
      n = nn,
      rejection_rate = mean(rejections)
    )
  }
}
robustness_dt <- data.table::rbindlist(results)

ggplot2$ggplot(robustness_dt, ggplot2$aes(x = n, y = rejection_rate,
    colour = distribution, group = distribution)) +
  ggplot2$geom_line(linewidth = 0.8) +
  ggplot2$geom_point(size = 2.5) +
  ggplot2$geom_hline(yintercept = 0.05, linetype = "dashed", colour = "grey40") +
  ggplot2$annotate("text", x = 100, y = 0.053, label = "Nominal \u03b1 = 0.05",
    hjust = 1, colour = "grey40", size = 4) +
  ggplot2$scale_colour_manual(values = c(
    "Normal" = "#2ecc71", "Exponential" = "#e74c3c",
    "t(df=3)" = "#3498db", "Uniform" = "#f39c12"
  )) +
  ggplot2$labs(
    x = "Sample size (n)", y = "Empirical rejection rate",
    colour = "Parent distribution",
    title = "Robustness of the One-Sample t-Test",
    subtitle = "How well does the test maintain \u03b1 = 0.05 under non-normality?"
  ) +
  ggplot2$theme_minimal(base_size = 14)
```

<Figure src="/courses/mathematical-statistics-1-foundations/robustness-sim-1.png" alt="Empirical Type I error rate of the one-sample t-test under different parent distributions. For large n, the test maintains its nominal level even for non-normal data.">
	Empirical Type I error rate of the one-sample t-test under different parent distributions. For large n, the test maintains its nominal level even for non-normal data.
</Figure>

Key observations:

- For $n \ge 30$, the t-test maintains its nominal level for symmetric distributions (uniform, t), even with heavy tails.
- For skewed distributions (exponential), larger samples ($n \ge 50$) are needed.
- For very small $n$ and skewed data, the t-test is **liberal** (rejects too often).

### 11.10.2 Diagnostic Summary: A Decision Flowchart

When you are about to apply a classical test, work through this checklist:

1. **Independence?** This is about study design, not data. Were observations collected independently? If not, you need a paired or repeated-measures approach.
2. **Normality?** Make a Q-Q plot. For $n > 30$, moderate departures are tolerable. For small $n$, consider a nonparametric alternative (Chapter 13).
3. **Equal variances?** If testing means, compare SDs visually or with the F-test. If in doubt, use Welch's test -- it is almost always appropriate.
4. **Expected counts?** For chi-square tests, ensure all $E_i \ge 5$. Otherwise, consider Fisher's exact test or combine categories.

**Report:** Male cats have substantially heavier hearts than female cats (mean 12.6 g vs 9.2 g, difference = 3.4 g). A Welch two-sample t-test confirms this is far beyond chance variation. The effect size (Cohen's d = 0.95) is large. Diagnostic plots show approximate normality in both groups, and the variance ratio is modest, supporting the validity of the test.

---

## Communicating to Stakeholders

When reporting on assumptions and diagnostics:

1. **Lead with the result**, not the diagnostics: "Male cats have heavier hearts on average."
2. **Mention that you checked**: "We verified that the data meet the assumptions of the test."
3. **If assumptions are violated, say what you did about it**: "Because the data were skewed, we used a non-parametric alternative" (Chapter 13).
4. **Never hide assumption violations**: if the diagnostics look poor, acknowledge this and temper your conclusions. Credibility comes from transparency.
5. **Visual summaries** of the data (boxplots, histograms) are more convincing than formal test output.

## Quick Reference

| Diagnostic | What it checks | Tool | Red flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| Q-Q plot | Normality | `stat_qq()` | Systematic curvature |
| Shapiro--Wilk | Normality | `shapiro.test()` | Small p-value (but sensitive for large $n$) |
| F-test | Equal variances | `var.test()` | Small p-value (but fragile) |
| Levene's test | Equal variances | `car::leveneTest()` | More robust than F-test |
| SD ratio | Equal variances | Manual | Ratio $> 2$ is concerning |
| Histogram/density | Distribution shape | `geom_histogram()` | Visible skew or multimodality |
