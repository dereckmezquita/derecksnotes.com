# Chapter 10 -- Hypothesis Testing Framework

## Part 1: Logic and P-values

In Chapters 8 and 9 we learnt to *estimate* parameters -- point estimates, confidence intervals. But estimation answers the question "what is the value of $\theta$?" In practice, we often face a sharper question: "is there evidence that $\theta$ differs from some reference value?" This is the domain of **hypothesis testing**, one of the most widely used -- and widely misunderstood -- tools in all of science.

We open with a historical episode that captures the spirit of testing beautifully: James Lind's scurvy trial of 1747.

---

## 10.1 Motivation: The Scurvy Trial

In 1747, the Scottish physician James Lind conducted what is often considered the first controlled clinical trial. Twelve sailors suffering from scurvy were divided into six pairs. Each pair received a different treatment: cider, elixir of vitriol, vinegar, seawater, oranges and lemons, or a purgative. The pair given citrus fruit recovered dramatically; the others did not.

Lind's reasoning was, in essence, a hypothesis test. His **null hypothesis** was that no treatment was effective against scurvy -- that recovery (or lack thereof) was governed by chance alone. The dramatic recovery of the citrus pair constituted evidence *against* this null. The question we formalise in this chapter is: how do we quantify such evidence rigorously?


``` r
# Lind's scurvy trial data (simplified)
scurvy <- data.frame(
  treatment = c("Cider", "Elixir of vitriol", "Vinegar",
                 "Seawater", "Citrus fruit", "Purgative"),
  n_patients = rep(2, 6),
  n_improved = c(0, 0, 0, 0, 2, 0)
)

scurvy$prop_improved <- scurvy$n_improved / scurvy$n_patients
scurvy
```

```
          treatment n_patients n_improved prop_improved
1             Cider          2          0             0
2 Elixir of vitriol          2          0             0
3           Vinegar          2          0             0
4          Seawater          2          0             0
5      Citrus fruit          2          2             1
6         Purgative          2          0             0
```

With only 2 patients per group, formal inference is limited. But the signal is unmistakable: every other group showed zero improvement. This is the archetype -- we observe something surprising under the assumption of no effect, and we must decide whether to attribute it to chance or to a real phenomenon.

## 10.2 The Logic of Hypothesis Testing

### 10.2.1 Innocent Until Proven Guilty

Hypothesis testing follows the logic of proof by contradiction, borrowing directly from the legal principle of "innocent until proven guilty."

1. **Assume the null hypothesis $H_0$ is true.** This is the "innocent" state -- the status quo, the default, the claim of no effect.
2. **Collect data and compute a test statistic.**
3. **Ask: how surprising are these data if $H_0$ were true?**
4. **If the data are sufficiently surprising, reject $H_0$.** The "burden of proof" has been met.

The defendant (null hypothesis) is presumed innocent. The prosecution (the data) must present evidence strong enough to convince us beyond a reasonable threshold. If the evidence is weak, we do not convict -- we "fail to reject $H_0$." Note carefully: we do **not** declare the defendant innocent; we merely state that the evidence was insufficient to convict.

### 10.2.2 The Asymmetry of Testing

This asymmetry is fundamental and often a source of confusion:

- **Rejecting $H_0$** is a strong conclusion: the data provide sufficient evidence against the null.
- **Failing to reject $H_0$** is a weak conclusion: the data are consistent with the null, but they might also be consistent with many alternative hypotheses.

"Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence." Failing to reject $H_0$ does *not* mean $H_0$ is true.

---

## 10.3 Null and Alternative Hypotheses

### 10.3.1 Formal Definitions

Let $\theta$ be a parameter of interest. A hypothesis test involves two competing claims:

- **Null hypothesis $H_0$:** $\theta \in \Theta_0$, where $\Theta_0$ is some subset of the parameter space.
- **Alternative hypothesis $H_1$ (or $H_a$):** $\theta \in \Theta_0^c$ (the complement of $\Theta_0$).

In the simplest and most common case, the null specifies a single value:

$$H_0: \theta = \theta_0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \theta \neq \theta_0 \quad \text{(two-sided)}$$

or a direction:

$$H_0: \theta = \theta_0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \theta > \theta_0 \quad \text{(one-sided, right)}$$

$$H_0: \theta = \theta_0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \theta < \theta_0 \quad \text{(one-sided, left)}$$

### 10.3.2 Simple vs. Composite Hypotheses

A hypothesis is **simple** if it specifies the distribution completely (i.e., a single point in the parameter space). It is **composite** if it specifies a set of distributions.

- $H_0: \mu = 5$ is simple (for a normal distribution with known $\sigma$).
- $H_1: \mu \neq 5$ is composite -- it includes all values $\mu \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{5\}$.

The distinction matters theoretically: the Neyman-Pearson lemma (Part 3) applies to simple hypotheses. For composite alternatives, we need more general tools such as likelihood ratio tests.

### 10.3.3 Choosing the Null

The null hypothesis should always represent the "conservative" claim -- the one that places the burden of proof on the researcher. In pharmaceutical trials, $H_0$ states that the drug has no effect. In quality control, $H_0$ states that the production process is operating within specification. In basic science, $H_0$ states that there is no relationship between variables.

We never set out to "prove" $H_0$. We set out to see whether the data are incompatible with it.

## 10.4 Test Statistics and Rejection Regions

### 10.4.1 Test Statistics

A **test statistic** $T = T(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ is a function of the data that measures how far the observed data deviate from what we would expect under $H_0$. Good test statistics are large (in absolute value) when the data are inconsistent with $H_0$ and small when they are consistent.

**Example.** Suppose $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known, and we test $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$ vs. $H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0$. A natural test statistic is:

$$Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}.$$

Under $H_0$, $Z \sim N(0, 1)$. Large values of $|Z|$ suggest $\mu \neq \mu_0$.

### 10.4.2 Rejection Regions

The **rejection region** (or critical region) $\mathcal{R}$ is the set of values of the test statistic for which we reject $H_0$. For a two-sided test at significance level $\alpha$:

$$\mathcal{R} = \{z : |z| > z_{\alpha/2}\}$$

where $z_{\alpha/2}$ is the upper $\alpha/2$ quantile of $N(0, 1)$. For $\alpha = 0.05$, $z_{0.025} \approx 1.96$.
