# Chapter 10 -- Hypothesis Testing Framework

## Part 1: Logic and P-values

In Chapters 8 and 9 we learnt to *estimate* parameters -- point estimates, confidence intervals. But estimation answers the question "what is the value of $\theta$?" In practice, we often face a sharper question: "is there evidence that $\theta$ differs from some reference value?" This is the domain of **hypothesis testing**, one of the most widely used -- and widely misunderstood -- tools in all of science.

We open with a historical episode that captures the spirit of testing beautifully: James Lind's scurvy trial of 1747.

---

## 10.1 Motivation: The Scurvy Trial

In 1747, the Scottish physician James Lind conducted what is often considered the first controlled clinical trial. Twelve sailors suffering from scurvy were divided into six pairs. Each pair received a different treatment: cider, elixir of vitriol, vinegar, seawater, oranges and lemons, or a purgative. The pair given citrus fruit recovered dramatically; the others did not.

Lind's reasoning was, in essence, a hypothesis test. His **null hypothesis** was that no treatment was effective against scurvy -- that recovery (or lack thereof) was governed by chance alone. The dramatic recovery of the citrus pair constituted evidence *against* this null. The question we formalise in this chapter is: how do we quantify such evidence rigorously?


``` r
# Lind's scurvy trial data (simplified)
scurvy <- data.frame(
  treatment = c("Cider", "Elixir of vitriol", "Vinegar",
                 "Seawater", "Citrus fruit", "Purgative"),
  n_patients = rep(2, 6),
  n_improved = c(0, 0, 0, 0, 2, 0)
)

scurvy$prop_improved <- scurvy$n_improved / scurvy$n_patients
scurvy
```

```
          treatment n_patients n_improved prop_improved
1             Cider          2          0             0
2 Elixir of vitriol          2          0             0
3           Vinegar          2          0             0
4          Seawater          2          0             0
5      Citrus fruit          2          2             1
6         Purgative          2          0             0
```

With only 2 patients per group, formal inference is limited. But the signal is unmistakable: every other group showed zero improvement. This is the archetype -- we observe something surprising under the assumption of no effect, and we must decide whether to attribute it to chance or to a real phenomenon.

---

## 10.2 The Logic of Hypothesis Testing

### 10.2.1 Innocent Until Proven Guilty

Hypothesis testing follows the logic of proof by contradiction, borrowing directly from the legal principle of "innocent until proven guilty."

1. **Assume the null hypothesis $H_0$ is true.** This is the "innocent" state -- the status quo, the default, the claim of no effect.
2. **Collect data and compute a test statistic.**
3. **Ask: how surprising are these data if $H_0$ were true?**
4. **If the data are sufficiently surprising, reject $H_0$.** The "burden of proof" has been met.

The defendant (null hypothesis) is presumed innocent. The prosecution (the data) must present evidence strong enough to convince us beyond a reasonable threshold. If the evidence is weak, we do not convict -- we "fail to reject $H_0$." Note carefully: we do **not** declare the defendant innocent; we merely state that the evidence was insufficient to convict.

### 10.2.2 The Asymmetry of Testing

This asymmetry is fundamental and often a source of confusion:

- **Rejecting $H_0$** is a strong conclusion: the data provide sufficient evidence against the null.
- **Failing to reject $H_0$** is a weak conclusion: the data are consistent with the null, but they might also be consistent with many alternative hypotheses.

"Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence." Failing to reject $H_0$ does *not* mean $H_0$ is true.

---

## 10.3 Null and Alternative Hypotheses

### 10.3.1 Formal Definitions

Let $\theta$ be a parameter of interest. A hypothesis test involves two competing claims:

- **Null hypothesis $H_0$:** $\theta \in \Theta_0$, where $\Theta_0$ is some subset of the parameter space.
- **Alternative hypothesis $H_1$ (or $H_a$):** $\theta \in \Theta_0^c$ (the complement of $\Theta_0$).

In the simplest and most common case, the null specifies a single value:

$$H_0: \theta = \theta_0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \theta \neq \theta_0 \quad \text{(two-sided)}$$

or a direction:

$$H_0: \theta = \theta_0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \theta > \theta_0 \quad \text{(one-sided, right)}$$

$$H_0: \theta = \theta_0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \theta < \theta_0 \quad \text{(one-sided, left)}$$

### 10.3.2 Simple vs. Composite Hypotheses

A hypothesis is **simple** if it specifies the distribution completely (i.e., a single point in the parameter space). It is **composite** if it specifies a set of distributions.

- $H_0: \mu = 5$ is simple (for a normal distribution with known $\sigma$).
- $H_1: \mu \neq 5$ is composite -- it includes all values $\mu \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{5\}$.

The distinction matters theoretically: the Neyman-Pearson lemma (Part 3) applies to simple hypotheses. For composite alternatives, we need more general tools such as likelihood ratio tests.

### 10.3.3 Choosing the Null

The null hypothesis should always represent the "conservative" claim -- the one that places the burden of proof on the researcher. In pharmaceutical trials, $H_0$ states that the drug has no effect. In quality control, $H_0$ states that the production process is operating within specification. In basic science, $H_0$ states that there is no relationship between variables.

We never set out to "prove" $H_0$. We set out to see whether the data are incompatible with it.

---

## 10.4 Test Statistics and Rejection Regions

### 10.4.1 Test Statistics

A **test statistic** $T = T(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ is a function of the data that measures how far the observed data deviate from what we would expect under $H_0$. Good test statistics are large (in absolute value) when the data are inconsistent with $H_0$ and small when they are consistent.

**Example.** Suppose $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known, and we test $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$ vs. $H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0$. A natural test statistic is:

$$Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}.$$

Under $H_0$, $Z \sim N(0, 1)$. Large values of $|Z|$ suggest $\mu \neq \mu_0$.

### 10.4.2 Rejection Regions

The **rejection region** (or critical region) $\mathcal{R}$ is the set of values of the test statistic for which we reject $H_0$. For a two-sided test at significance level $\alpha$:

$$\mathcal{R} = \{z : |z| > z_{\alpha/2}\}$$

where $z_{\alpha/2}$ is the upper $\alpha/2$ quantile of $N(0, 1)$. For $\alpha = 0.05$, $z_{0.025} \approx 1.96$.


``` r
box::use(ggplot2)
box::use(data.table)

alpha <- 0.05
z_crit <- qnorm(1 - alpha / 2)

x_seq <- seq(-4, 4, length.out = 500)
density_vals <- dnorm(x_seq)

df <- data.table$data.table(x = x_seq, y = density_vals)
df[, region := ifelse(abs(x) > z_crit, "Reject H0", "Fail to reject")]

ggplot2$ggplot(df, ggplot2$aes(x = x, y = y)) +
  ggplot2$geom_area(
    data = df[region == "Fail to reject"],

                   size = 4.5, colour = "#333333") +
```

## 10.5 P-values

### 10.5.1 Formal Definition

The **p-value** is the probability, under $H_0$, of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one actually observed.

Formally, if $T$ is the test statistic and $t_{\text{obs}}$ is the observed value:

$$\text{p-value} = P_{H_0}(T \geq t_{\text{obs}}) \quad \text{(one-sided, right)}$$

$$\text{p-value} = P_{H_0}(|T| \geq |t_{\text{obs}}|) \quad \text{(two-sided)}$$

The p-value measures the *compatibility* of the data with the null hypothesis. A small p-value means the data are surprising under $H_0$; a large p-value means they are unremarkable.

The decision rule: reject $H_0$ if $\text{p-value} \leq \alpha$.

### 10.5.2 A Concrete Example

Suppose $X_1, \ldots, X_{25} \overset{iid}{\sim} N(\mu, 4)$ (variance known to be 4). We test $H_0: \mu = 10$ vs. $H_1: \mu \neq 10$ at $\alpha = 0.05$. We observe $\bar{x} = 10.85$.


``` r
n <- 25
sigma <- 2
mu_0 <- 10
x_bar <- 10.85

z_obs <- (x_bar - mu_0) / (sigma / sqrt(n))
p_value <- 2 * (1 - pnorm(abs(z_obs)))

c(z_observed = z_obs, p_value = p_value)
```

```
z_observed    p_value 
2.12500000 0.03358661 
```

The p-value is approximately 0.034, which is less than 0.05. We reject $H_0$ and conclude there is sufficient evidence that $\mu \neq 10$.

### 10.5.3 What P-values ARE

1. The p-value is the probability of the observed data (or more extreme) **under the null hypothesis.**
2. It is a measure of the **evidence against** $H_0$.
3. It is a **continuous** measure of evidence -- not a binary yes/no.
4. Smaller p-values indicate stronger evidence against $H_0$.

### 10.5.4 What P-values are NOT -- Common Misinterpretations

This list is critical. Misinterpretation of p-values is arguably the single greatest source of confusion in applied statistics.

1. **The p-value is NOT the probability that $H_0$ is true.** This is the most common error. $P(\text{data} \mid H_0)$ is not the same as $P(H_0 \mid \text{data})$. The latter requires Bayes' theorem and a prior.

2. **The p-value is NOT the probability that the result is due to chance.** It is the probability of the data under the assumption that only chance is operating. These are logically distinct statements.

3. **A non-significant p-value does NOT mean there is no effect.** It means the data are not sufficiently surprising under $H_0$. The effect could be real but small, or the sample size could be insufficient.

4. **The p-value is NOT the probability of making a Type I error.** The significance level $\alpha$ is the *pre-specified* probability of Type I error. The p-value is a random variable that depends on the data.

5. **$p = 0.05$ does NOT mean there is a 95% chance the result is real.** This confuses the frequentist p-value with a posterior probability.

6. **Two studies, one with $p = 0.04$ and one with $p = 0.06$, do NOT necessarily show fundamentally different results.** The difference between "significant" and "not significant" is not itself necessarily significant.

## 10.6 P-values Under the Null are Uniform(0, 1)

This is one of the most elegant and important results in testing theory.

**Theorem.** If $H_0$ is true and the test statistic has a continuous distribution, then the p-value follows a $\text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ distribution.

**Proof sketch.** Let $F_0$ be the CDF of the test statistic under $H_0$. For a one-sided test (rejecting for large values of $T$):

$$\text{p-value} = 1 - F_0(T).$$

Let $U = 1 - F_0(T)$. By the probability integral transform (Chapter 5), $F_0(T) \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, and therefore $U = 1 - F_0(T) \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$. $\blacksquare$

This means that if there is no effect, p-values are equally likely to be anywhere in $[0, 1]$. In particular, there is exactly a 5% chance of obtaining $p \leq 0.05$ -- which is precisely the Type I error rate.

### 10.6.1 Simulation: P-values Under the Null




``` r
set.seed(42)
n_sim <- 10000
n <- 30
mu_0 <- 0
sigma <- 1

# Simulate data under H0: mu = 0
p_values_null <- replicate(n_sim, {
  x <- rnorm(n, mean = mu_0, sd = sigma)
  z <- (mean(x) - mu_0) / (sigma / sqrt(n))
  2 * (1 - pnorm(abs(z)))
})

pval_df <- data.table$data.table(p = p_values_null)

ggplot2$ggplot(pval_df, ggplot2$aes(x = p)) +
  ggplot2$geom_histogram(
    ggplot2$aes(y = ggplot2::after_stat(density)),
    bins = 40, fill = "#0072B2", alpha = 0.7, colour = "white"
  ) +
  ggplot2$geom_hline(yintercept = 1, linetype = "dashed",
                     colour = "#D55E00", linewidth = 1) +
  ggplot2$annotate("text", x = 0.5, y = 1.15,
                   label = "Uniform(0, 1) density", colour = "#D55E00", size = 4.5) +
  ggplot2$labs(
    x = "P-value",
    y = "Density",
    title = "P-values under the null hypothesis are Uniform(0, 1)"
  ) +
  ggplot2$theme_minimal(base_size = 13)
```

### 10.6.2 P-values Under the Alternative

When $H_0$ is false -- i.e., there is a real effect -- p-values are no longer uniform. They are skewed towards 0. The stronger the effect (or the larger the sample), the more concentrated the p-values become near 0.


``` r
set.seed(42)
n <- 30
sigma <- 1
mu_0 <- 0
n_sim <- 10000

mu_values <- c(0, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0)

pval_list <- lapply(mu_values, function(mu_true) {
  pvals <- replicate(n_sim, {
    x <- rnorm(n, mean = mu_true, sd = sigma)
    z <- (mean(x) - mu_0) / (sigma / sqrt(n))
    2 * (1 - pnorm(abs(z)))
  })
  data.table$data.table(
    p = pvals,
    scenario = sprintf("mu = %.1f", mu_true)
  )
})

pval_all <- data.table$rbindlist(pval_list)
pval_all[, scenario := factor(scenario, levels = sprintf("mu = %.1f", mu_values))]

ggplot2$ggplot(pval_all, ggplot2$aes(x = p)) +
  ggplot2$geom_histogram(
    ggplot2$aes(y = ggplot2::after_stat(density)),
    bins = 40, fill = "#0072B2", alpha = 0.7, colour = "white"
  ) +
  ggplot2$facet_wrap(~scenario, ncol = 2, scales = "free_y") +
  ggplot2$geom_hline(yintercept = 1, linetype = "dashed",
                     colour = "#D55E00", linewidth = 0.7) +
  ggplot2$labs(
    x = "P-value",
    y = "Density",
    title = "P-value distributions under null vs. alternative hypotheses"
  ) +
  ggplot2$theme_minimal(base_size = 13) +
  ggplot2$theme(strip.text = ggplot2$element_text(face = "bold"))
```

When $\mu = 0$ (the null is true), p-values are flat. As the true $\mu$ moves away from 0, the histogram piles up near 0 -- most tests correctly detect the effect. This pile-up is the visual signature of **power**, which we formalise in Part 2.

## 10.7 The Testing Procedure: A Summary

To conduct a hypothesis test:

1. **State the hypotheses:** $H_0$ and $H_1$.
2. **Choose the significance level** $\alpha$ (typically 0.05, but context-dependent).
3. **Choose a test statistic** $T$ and determine its distribution under $H_0$.
4. **Compute the observed test statistic** $t_{\text{obs}}$ from the data.
5. **Compute the p-value** or compare $t_{\text{obs}}$ to the critical value.
6. **Make a decision:** reject $H_0$ if $p \leq \alpha$ (or $t_{\text{obs}} \in \mathcal{R}$).
7. **Interpret in context.**


``` r
# Complete example: one-sample z-test
# H0: mu = 50, H1: mu != 50, sigma = 10
set.seed(42)
x <- rnorm(36, mean = 53, sd = 10)

mu_0 <- 50
sigma <- 10
n <- length(x)
x_bar <- mean(x)

z_obs <- (x_bar - mu_0) / (sigma / sqrt(n))
p_val <- 2 * (1 - pnorm(abs(z_obs)))

data.frame(
  quantity = c("Sample mean", "z-statistic", "p-value", "Decision (alpha=0.05)"),
  value = c(
    sprintf("%.3f", x_bar),
    sprintf("%.3f", z_obs),
    sprintf("%.4f", p_val),
    ifelse(p_val <= 0.05, "Reject H0", "Fail to reject H0")
  )
)
```

```
               quantity     value
1           Sample mean    53.676
2           z-statistic     2.205
3               p-value    0.0274
4 Decision (alpha=0.05) Reject H0
```

## 10.8 One-sided vs. Two-sided Tests

The choice between one-sided and two-sided depends on the scientific question, and should be decided **before** looking at the data.


``` r
alpha <- 0.05
x_seq <- seq(-4, 4, length.out = 500)
y_seq <- dnorm(x_seq)

df_base <- data.table$data.table(x = x_seq, y = y_seq)

# Two-sided
z_two <- qnorm(1 - alpha / 2)
df_base[, two_sided := ifelse(abs(x) > z_two, "Reject", "Fail to reject")]

# One-sided (right)
z_one <- qnorm(1 - alpha)
df_base[, one_sided := ifelse(x > z_one, "Reject", "Fail to reject")]

p1 <- ggplot2$ggplot(df_base, ggplot2$aes(x = x, y = y)) +
  ggplot2$geom_area(
    data = df_base[two_sided == "Reject"],
    fill = "#D55E00", alpha = 0.5
  ) +
  ggplot2$geom_area(
    data = df_base[two_sided == "Fail to reject"],
    fill = "#999999", alpha = 0.3
  ) +
  ggplot2$geom_line(linewidth = 0.7) +
  ggplot2$labs(title = sprintf("Two-sided (z_crit = +/-%.2f)", z_two),
               x = "z", y = "Density") +
  ggplot2$theme_minimal(base_size = 12)

p2 <- ggplot2$ggplot(df_base, ggplot2$aes(x = x, y = y)) +
  ggplot2$geom_area(
    data = df_base[one_sided == "Reject"],
    fill = "#D55E00", alpha = 0.5
  ) +
  ggplot2$geom_area(
    data = df_base[one_sided == "Fail to reject"],
    fill = "#999999", alpha = 0.3
  ) +
  ggplot2$geom_line(linewidth = 0.7) +
  ggplot2$labs(title = sprintf("One-sided right (z_crit = %.2f)", z_one),
               x = "z", y = "Density") +
  ggplot2$theme_minimal(base_size = 12)

gridExtra::grid.arrange(p1, p2, ncol = 2)
```

The one-sided test places all of alpha in one tail, making it more sensitive to effects in that direction but blind to effects in the opposite direction.">
	Comparison of one-sided (right) and two-sided rejection regions at alpha = 0.05. The one-sided test places all of alpha in one tail, making it more sensitive to effects in that direction but blind to effects in the opposite direction.
</Figure>

**When to use one-sided tests:**

- You have a strong *a priori* reason to expect an effect in one direction.
- A result in the opposite direction is scientifically irrelevant (e.g., a drug cannot be worse than placebo by design).
- The choice was made before data collection.

**When in doubt:** use a two-sided test. It is more conservative and less susceptible to accusations of p-hacking.

## Communicating to Stakeholders

When presenting hypothesis test results to a non-technical audience:

- Avoid jargon like "reject the null." Instead say: "We found statistically significant evidence that the treatment affects the outcome" or "We did not find sufficient evidence of a difference."
- Never say "the p-value proves there is an effect." Say: "The data are unlikely under the assumption of no effect (p = 0.03)."
- Always report effect sizes alongside p-values. A result can be statistically significant but practically meaningless.
- Use the Lind scurvy analogy: "We assumed the treatment does nothing, then checked whether the data are consistent with that assumption. They were not."
- Be honest about limitations. A p-value of 0.048 is not qualitatively different from 0.052.

## Quick Reference

| Concept | Definition |
|:--------|:-----------|
| **$H_0$ (null)** | Default claim; the hypothesis of no effect or no difference |
| **$H_1$ (alternative)** | The claim we seek evidence for |
| **Test statistic** | Function of data measuring deviation from $H_0$ |
| **Rejection region** | Values of test statistic leading to rejection of $H_0$ |
| **P-value** | $P_{H_0}(\text{data as extreme or more extreme than observed})$ |
| **Significance level $\alpha$** | Pre-specified threshold for rejection (typically 0.05) |
| **Two-sided test** | $H_1: \theta \neq \theta_0$; rejects for large $\lvert T\rvert$ |
| **One-sided test** | $H_1: \theta > \theta_0$ or $H_1: \theta < \theta_0$; rejects in one tail |
| **P-value under $H_0$** | $\sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ for continuous test statistics |
