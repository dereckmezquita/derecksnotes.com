# Chapter 7 -- Convergence and Limit Theorems

## Part 1: Inequalities and Convergence Types

Statistics works because of a remarkable fact: averages of random quantities become predictable when the sample is large. This chapter makes that claim precise. We begin with inequalities that bound how far a random variable can stray from its expected value, and then formalise exactly what it means for a sequence of random variables to "converge."

These tools form the bridge between probability theory and statistical inference. Every confidence interval, every hypothesis test, and every asymptotic result in later chapters rests on the foundations we lay here.

---

## 7.1 Why Convergence Matters

Suppose you flip a fair coin many times and compute the running proportion of heads. Intuitively, this proportion should settle near 0.5. But what does "settle" mean mathematically? How quickly? With what guarantees?

These are not idle questions. The entire edifice of frequentist statistics -- $p$-values, confidence intervals, maximum likelihood -- depends on the behaviour of estimators as the sample size grows. If sample averages did *not* converge to population means, none of our standard methods would work.

We need three things:

1. **Inequalities** that bound tail probabilities without knowing the full distribution.
2. **Modes of convergence** that formalise "a sequence of random variables approaches a limit."
3. **Limit theorems** (the LLN and CLT, covered in Parts 2 and 3) that tell us *what* the limits are.

Let us start with the inequalities.

---

## 7.2 Markov's Inequality

### 7.2.1 Statement

**Markov's inequality.** Let $X$ be a non-negative random variable with finite expectation. Then for any $a > 0$:

$$P(X \ge a) \le \frac{E[X]}{a}.$$

This is astonishingly general: we need know *nothing* about the distribution of $X$ beyond its mean and the fact that it is non-negative.

### 7.2.2 Proof

The proof is short and elegant. We use the indicator trick.

Define the indicator variable $I = \mathbf{1}(X \ge a)$. Since $X \ge 0$, whenever $X \ge a$ we certainly have $X \ge a \cdot 1 = a \cdot I$. And when $X < a$, we have $a \cdot I = 0 \le X$. Therefore, in all cases:

$$a \cdot I \le X.$$

Taking expectations on both sides (expectation preserves inequalities):

$$a \cdot E[I] \le E[X].$$

But $E[I] = P(X \ge a)$ (the expectation of an indicator is the probability of the event). Dividing both sides by $a > 0$:

$$P(X \ge a) \le \frac{E[X]}{a}. \quad \blacksquare$$

### 7.2.3 Simulation: Markov's Bound is Conservative but Valid

## 7.3 Chebyshev's Inequality

### 7.3.1 Deriving Chebyshev from Markov

Chebyshev's inequality sharpens Markov by incorporating the variance. The derivation is a clever application of Markov's inequality.

**Theorem (Chebyshev).** Let $X$ be a random variable with finite mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2$. Then for any $k > 0$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \ge k\sigma) \le \frac{1}{k^2}.$$

**Proof.** Consider the random variable $Y = (X - \mu)^2$. Note that $Y \ge 0$ and $E[Y] = \text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$.

The event $|X - \mu| \ge k\sigma$ is the same as $(X - \mu)^2 \ge k^2\sigma^2$, i.e., $Y \ge k^2\sigma^2$.

Applying **Markov's inequality** to $Y$ with $a = k^2\sigma^2$:

$$P(Y \ge k^2\sigma^2) \le \frac{E[Y]}{k^2\sigma^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2\sigma^2} = \frac{1}{k^2}. \quad \blacksquare$$

**Equivalent form.** Setting $\epsilon = k\sigma$ (so $k = \epsilon / \sigma$):

$$P(|X - \mu| \ge \epsilon) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{\epsilon^2}.$$

This form is often more convenient in proofs.

### 7.3.2 What Does Chebyshev Tell Us?

| $k$ (number of std deviations) | Upper bound on $P(\lvert X - \mu\rvert \ge k\sigma)$ |
|:---:|:---:|
| 1 | 1.000 (trivial) |
| 2 | 0.250 |
| 3 | 0.111 |
| 4 | 0.063 |
| 5 | 0.040 |

For any distribution with finite variance, at most 25% of the probability lies more than 2 standard deviations from the mean. At most 11.1% lies beyond 3 standard deviations. Compare this with the normal distribution, where only about 4.6% lies beyond 2$\sigma$ and 0.3% beyond 3$\sigma$. Chebyshev's bound is conservative because it applies to *every* distribution.

## 7.4 Jensen's Inequality

### 7.4.1 Statement

**Jensen's inequality.** If $g$ is a **convex** function and $X$ is a random variable with finite expectation, then:

$$g(E[X]) \le E[g(X)].$$

If $g$ is **concave**, the inequality reverses: $g(E[X]) \ge E[g(X)]$.

A function $g$ is convex if for all $x, y$ and $\lambda \in [0,1]$:
$$g(\lambda x + (1-\lambda)y) \le \lambda g(x) + (1-\lambda) g(y).$$

Informally, a convex function "curves upward" -- its graph lies below any secant line.

### 7.4.2 Proof for the Discrete Case

Let $X$ take values $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_m$ with probabilities $p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_m$. We prove Jensen's inequality by induction on $m$.

**Base case ($m = 1$).** Then $X = x_1$ with probability 1, so $E[X] = x_1$ and $E[g(X)] = g(x_1) = g(E[X])$. The inequality holds with equality.

**Base case ($m = 2$).** We have $E[X] = p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2$ where $p_1 + p_2 = 1$. By the definition of convexity (setting $\lambda = p_1$):

$$g(p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2) \le p_1 g(x_1) + p_2 g(x_2) = E[g(X)].$$

The left-hand side is $g(E[X])$. Done.

**Inductive step.** Assume the result holds for any discrete distribution on $m - 1$ points. Consider $X$ on $m$ points. Write $\alpha = p_1$ and $\beta = 1 - p_1$. Then:

$$E[X] = \alpha x_1 + \beta \underbrace{\left(\frac{p_2}{\beta} x_2 + \cdots + \frac{p_m}{\beta} x_m\right)}_{\equiv \mu^*}.$$

By convexity of $g$:

$$g(E[X]) = g(\alpha x_1 + \beta \mu^*) \le \alpha g(x_1) + \beta g(\mu^*).$$

By the induction hypothesis applied to the $(m-1)$-point distribution on $x_2, \ldots, x_m$ with probabilities $p_2/\beta, \ldots, p_m/\beta$:

$$g(\mu^*) \le \frac{p_2}{\beta} g(x_2) + \cdots + \frac{p_m}{\beta} g(x_m).$$

Combining:

$$g(E[X]) \le \alpha g(x_1) + \beta \left(\frac{p_2}{\beta} g(x_2) + \cdots + \frac{p_m}{\beta} g(x_m)\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{m} p_i g(x_i) = E[g(X)]. \quad \blacksquare$$

### 7.4.3 Example: $E[X^2] \ge (E[X])^2$

Since $g(x) = x^2$ is convex, Jensen's inequality immediately gives:

$$E[X^2] \ge (E[X])^2.$$

This is equivalent to $\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 \ge 0$, which we already knew. Jensen's inequality provides a one-line proof.

## 7.5 Types of Convergence

We now address the central question of this chapter: what does it mean for a sequence of random variables $X_1, X_2, X_3, \ldots$ to "converge" to a random variable $X$?

Unlike sequences of real numbers, where convergence has a single definition, random variables live in a richer world. There are several notions of convergence, each useful in different contexts.

### 7.5.1 Intuition First: Three Simulations

Before the formal definitions, let us build intuition by watching convergence in action.

Now let us define these notions precisely.

### 7.5.2 Convergence in Probability

**Definition.** A sequence $\{X_n\}$ converges **in probability** to $X$ -- written $X_n \xrightarrow{P} X$ -- if for every $\epsilon > 0$:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} P(|X_n - X| > \epsilon) = 0.$$

In words: the probability that $X_n$ differs from $X$ by more than any fixed tolerance $\epsilon$ goes to zero.

This is the mode of convergence used in the **Weak Law of Large Numbers** (Part 2).

### 7.5.3 Almost Sure Convergence

**Definition.** A sequence $\{X_n\}$ converges **almost surely** (a.s.) to $X$ -- written $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} X$ -- if:

$$P\!\left(\lim_{n \to \infty} X_n = X\right) = 1.$$

In words: with probability 1, the individual sample paths converge. This is a stronger statement than convergence in probability -- it says that for (almost) every outcome $\omega$ in the sample space, the numerical sequence $X_1(\omega), X_2(\omega), \ldots$ converges to $X(\omega)$.

**Almost sure implies in probability.** If $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} X$, then $X_n \xrightarrow{P} X$. The converse is false in general.

This is the mode of convergence used in the **Strong Law of Large Numbers**.

### 7.5.4 Convergence in Distribution

**Definition.** A sequence $\{X_n\}$ converges **in distribution** to $X$ -- written $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$ -- if:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} F_{X_n}(x) = F_X(x)$$

at every point $x$ where $F_X$ is continuous.

This is the **weakest** mode of convergence. It says only that the CDFs converge pointwise, not that the random variables themselves are close on any particular outcome. This is the mode of convergence used in the **Central Limit Theorem** (Part 3).

### 7.5.5 The Hierarchy

$$\text{Almost sure} \implies \text{In probability} \implies \text{In distribution}.$$

Neither implication reverses in general. However, convergence in distribution to a **constant** $c$ does imply convergence in probability to $c$.

### 7.5.7 A Counterexample: In Probability but Not Almost Surely

The classic "typewriter sequence" provides the counterexample. On $[0,1]$ with uniform measure, define $X_n = \mathbf{1}_{[a_n, b_n]}$ where the intervals sweep repeatedly across $[0,1]$ with decreasing length.

We can simulate a simpler variant. Consider $X_n = \mathbf{1}(U_n \le 1/n)$ where $U_n$ are independent Uniform(0,1). Then $P(X_n = 1) = 1/n \to 0$, so $X_n \xrightarrow{P} 0$.

But since $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(X_n = 1) = \sum 1/n = \infty$, by the second Borel--Cantelli lemma (the $X_n$ are independent), infinitely many of the $X_n$ equal 1 with probability 1. Thus $X_n$ does *not* converge to 0 almost surely.


``` r
set.seed(42)

# X_n = 1 if U_n <= 1/n
x_n <- as.integer(u <= 1 / seq_len(n_max))

# Count how many "spikes" (X_n = 1) we see up to each n
cumulative_spikes <- cumsum(x_n)

spike_data <- data.table$data.table(
  n = seq_len(n_max),
  spikes = cumulative_spikes
)
```

## 7.6 Continuous Mapping Theorem

We state without proof a result that is used constantly in asymptotic theory.

**Continuous Mapping Theorem.** If $X_n \xrightarrow{P} X$ and $g$ is a continuous function, then $g(X_n) \xrightarrow{P} g(X)$.

The same holds for convergence in distribution and almost sure convergence.

This is why, for instance, if $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu$, then $\bar{X}_n^2 \xrightarrow{P} \mu^2$.

---

## 7.7 Slutsky's Theorem

Another indispensable tool:

**Slutsky's Theorem.** If $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$ and $Y_n \xrightarrow{P} c$ (a constant), then:

- $X_n + Y_n \xrightarrow{d} X + c$
- $X_n Y_n \xrightarrow{d} cX$
- $X_n / Y_n \xrightarrow{d} X / c$ (provided $c \ne 0$)

We will use Slutsky's theorem heavily in Chapter 8 when working with plug-in estimators for asymptotic variances.

## Communicating to Stakeholders

When explaining these ideas to a non-technical audience:

- **Markov and Chebyshev** are useful for the *idea* they represent: "unlikely to be far from the average." You rarely need to quote the bounds themselves -- instead say: "With any distribution, the probability of an extreme outcome decreases predictably."
- **Convergence** can be explained with a simple metaphor: "As we gather more data, our estimate zooms in on the truth -- like a camera lens gradually coming into focus."
- Avoid mentioning "almost sure" vs. "in probability" to non-statisticians. Simply say the estimate "converges" or "stabilises."
- The real punchline comes in Parts 2 and 3: sample averages converge (LLN) and their fluctuations are approximately normal (CLT). These are the results that matter for practice.

## Quick Reference

| Inequality/Concept | Statement | Conditions |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Markov** | $P(X \ge a) \le E[X]/a$ | $X \ge 0$, $a > 0$ |
| **Chebyshev** | $P(\lvert X - \mu\rvert \ge k\sigma) \le 1/k^2$ | Finite variance |
| **Jensen** | $g(E[X]) \le E[g(X)]$ | $g$ convex |
| **Conv. in prob.** | $P(\lvert X_n - X\rvert > \epsilon) \to 0$ | For all $\epsilon > 0$ |
| **Almost sure** | $P(\lim X_n = X) = 1$ | Strongest mode |
| **Conv. in dist.** | $F_{X_n}(x) \to F_X(x)$ | At continuity points of $F_X$ |
| **Hierarchy** | a.s. $\Rightarrow$ in prob. $\Rightarrow$ in dist. | |
