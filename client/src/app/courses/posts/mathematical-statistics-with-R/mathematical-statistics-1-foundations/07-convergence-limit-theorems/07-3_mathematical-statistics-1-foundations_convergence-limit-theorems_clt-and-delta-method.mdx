# Chapter 7 -- Convergence and Limit Theorems

## Part 3: The Central Limit Theorem and the Delta Method

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is arguably the single most important result in statistics. It explains why the normal distribution appears so often in practice: **the sum (or average) of many independent random variables is approximately normal, regardless of the underlying distribution**. This is the theorem that makes confidence intervals work, that justifies the $z$-test, and that underpins the entire asymptotic theory of maximum likelihood.

In this part we prove the CLT using moment-generating functions, demonstrate it with extensive simulations (including real NHANES data), and then derive the **delta method** -- the tool that extends the CLT to functions of sample means.

---

## 7.8 The Central Limit Theorem

### 7.8.1 Statement

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be iid random variables with mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2 > 0$. Define the **standardised sample mean**:

$$Z_n = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n X_i - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}.$$

**Theorem (Central Limit Theorem).** As $n \to \infty$:

$$Z_n \xrightarrow{d} Z \sim N(0, 1).$$

Equivalently:

$$\bar{X}_n \stackrel{\cdot}{\sim} N\!\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$$

for large $n$.

### 7.8.2 Proof via Moment-Generating Functions

We prove the CLT by showing that the MGF of $Z_n$ converges to the MGF of a standard normal. By the MGF convergence theorem (Lévy's continuity theorem for MGFs), this implies convergence in distribution.

**Step 1: Define centred variables.**

Let $Y_i = X_i - \mu$, so $E[Y_i] = 0$ and $\text{Var}(Y_i) = \sigma^2$. Then:

$$Z_n = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \sum_{i=1}^{n} Y_i.$$

**Step 2: Write the MGF of $Z_n$.**

Since the $Y_i$ are iid, the MGF of their sum is the product of the individual MGFs. Let $M_Y(t) = E[e^{tY_i}]$ denote the common MGF. Then:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = E\!\left[\exp\!\left(t \cdot \frac{\sum Y_i}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\right)\right] = \prod_{i=1}^{n} E\!\left[\exp\!\left(\frac{t}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} Y_i\right)\right] = \left[M_Y\!\left(\frac{t}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n.$$

**Step 3: Taylor-expand the MGF around 0.**

Since $E[Y_i] = 0$ and $E[Y_i^2] = \sigma^2$, the Taylor expansion of $M_Y(s)$ around $s = 0$ is:

$$M_Y(s) = 1 + sE[Y_i] + \frac{s^2}{2}E[Y_i^2] + O(s^3) = 1 + \frac{s^2 \sigma^2}{2} + O(s^3).$$

Substituting $s = t / (\sigma\sqrt{n})$:

$$M_Y\!\left(\frac{t}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\right) = 1 + \frac{t^2/(n\sigma^2) \cdot \sigma^2}{2} + O(n^{-3/2}) = 1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2}).$$

**Step 4: Raise to the power $n$ and take the limit.**

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = \left[1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})\right]^n.$$

We use the fundamental limit: $\lim_{n \to \infty} (1 + a_n/n)^n = e^{a}$ whenever $a_n \to a$. Here $a_n = t^2/2 + O(n^{-1/2}) \to t^2/2$. Therefore:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} M_{Z_n}(t) = e^{t^2/2}.$$

**Step 5: Recognise the limit.**

The function $e^{t^2/2}$ is the MGF of the standard normal distribution $N(0,1)$.

By the **MGF convergence theorem** (if $M_{Z_n}(t) \to M_Z(t)$ for all $t$ in a neighbourhood of 0, and $M_Z$ is the MGF of some distribution, then $Z_n \xrightarrow{d} Z$), we conclude:

$$Z_n \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1). \quad \blacksquare$$

### 7.8.3 What the CLT Does and Does Not Say

**Does say:**
- The *sampling distribution of the mean* is approximately normal for large $n$.
- The approximation improves as $n$ increases.
- It works regardless of the shape of the original distribution (provided $\sigma^2 < \infty$).

**Does not say:**
- The data themselves are normal.
- The approximation is good for any particular small $n$.
- It applies to distributions without finite variance (e.g., Cauchy).

## 7.9 The Definitive CLT Demonstration

### 7.9.1 Four Source Distributions

We sample from four very different distributions and show that the sampling distribution of $\bar{X}_n$ becomes approximately normal in each case.


``` r
box::use(ggplot2)
box::use(data.table)

set.seed(42)

n_reps <- 5000

# Define four source distributions
source_dists <- list(
  "Exponential(1)" = list(
    rfun = function(n) rexp(n, 1),
    mu = 1, sigma = 1
  ),
  "Uniform(0, 1)" = list(
    rfun = function(n) runif(n),
    mu = 0.5, sigma = 1 / sqrt(12)
  ),

    mu = 2, sigma = sqrt(2)

  "Bimodal" = list(
    rfun = function(n) {
      # Mixture of N(-2, 0.5^2) and N(2, 0.5^2) with equal weight
      ifelse(runif(n) < 0.5, rnorm(n, -2, 0.5), rnorm(n, 2, 0.5))

    mu = 0, sigma = sqrt(0.5 * (0.25 + 4) + 0.5 * (0.25 + 4))
```

## 7.12 The Delta Method

### 7.12.1 Motivation

The CLT tells us that $\bar{X}_n$ is approximately normal. But what if we are interested in a *function* of the mean -- say $g(\bar{X}_n) = \log(\bar{X}_n)$ or $g(\bar{X}_n) = 1/\bar{X}_n$? What is the asymptotic distribution of $g(\bar{X}_n)$?

The **delta method** answers this question using a first-order Taylor expansion.

### 7.12.2 Crash Course: First-Order Taylor Expansion

Recall from calculus that if $g$ is a differentiable function, then near a point $a$:

$$g(x) \approx g(a) + g'(a)(x - a).$$

This is the tangent-line approximation. The error is $O((x-a)^2)$, which becomes negligible when $x$ is close to $a$.

**Example.** $g(x) = \log(x)$, $a = 1$. Then $g'(x) = 1/x$, so $g'(1) = 1$. The approximation is:

$$\log(x) \approx \log(1) + 1 \cdot (x - 1) = x - 1.$$

This is accurate for $x$ near 1.

### 7.12.3 Derivation of the Delta Method

**Theorem (Delta Method).** If $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)$ and $g$ is a function with $g'(\mu) \ne 0$, then:

$$\sqrt{n}\left(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)\right) \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(0, \, [g'(\mu)]^2 \sigma^2\right).$$

Equivalently:

$$g(\bar{X}_n) \stackrel{\cdot}{\sim} N\!\left(g(\mu), \, \frac{[g'(\mu)]^2 \sigma^2}{n}\right).$$

**Proof.** Apply a first-order Taylor expansion of $g$ around $\mu$:

$$g(\bar{X}_n) \approx g(\mu) + g'(\mu)(\bar{X}_n - \mu).$$

Rearranging:

$$g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu) \approx g'(\mu)(\bar{X}_n - \mu).$$

Multiplying both sides by $\sqrt{n}$:

$$\sqrt{n}\left(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)\right) \approx g'(\mu) \cdot \sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu).$$

By the CLT, $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)$.

Since multiplication by the constant $g'(\mu)$ preserves convergence in distribution:

$$\sqrt{n}\left(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)\right) \xrightarrow{d} g'(\mu) \cdot N(0, \sigma^2) = N\!\left(0, [g'(\mu)]^2 \sigma^2\right). \quad \blacksquare$$

The approximation error vanishes in the limit because $\bar{X}_n - \mu = O_P(1/\sqrt{n})$, so the second-order Taylor term is $O_P(1/n)$, which vanishes after multiplication by $\sqrt{n}$.

### 7.12.4 Summary

$$\text{If } \bar{X}_n \stackrel{\cdot}{\sim} N\!\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right), \quad \text{then } g(\bar{X}_n) \stackrel{\cdot}{\sim} N\!\left(g(\mu), \frac{[g'(\mu)]^2 \sigma^2}{n}\right).$$

The key insight: **the asymptotic variance of** $g(\bar{X}_n)$ **is** $[g'(\mu)]^2 \sigma^2 / n$.

## 7.13 Delta Method Examples

### 7.13.1 Example: The Log Transformation

Let $X_i \sim \text{Exp}(1)$, so $\mu = 1$, $\sigma^2 = 1$. Consider $g(x) = \log(x)$, so $g'(x) = 1/x$ and $g'(\mu) = 1$.

By the delta method:

$$\log(\bar{X}_n) \stackrel{\cdot}{\sim} N\!\left(\log(1), \frac{1^2 \cdot 1}{n}\right) = N\!\left(0, \frac{1}{n}\right).$$


``` r
set.seed(42)

n_values <- c(10, 50, 200, 1000)
n_reps <- 5000

delta_log <- do.call(rbind, lapply(n_values, function(n) {
  means <- replicate(n_reps, mean(rexp(n, 1)))
  g_means <- log(means)
  # By delta method: sqrt(n) * (g(Xbar) - g(mu)) ~ N(0, [g'(mu)]^2 * sigma^2)
  # g(mu) = log(1) = 0, g'(mu) = 1, sigma^2 = 1
  z <- sqrt(n) * (g_means - 0) / 1
  data.table$data.table(n = n, z = z)
```

### 7.13.2 Example: Variance-Stabilising Transformation for Poisson

For a Poisson random variable, $\text{Var}(X) = \mu$. The asymptotic variance of $\bar{X}_n$ is $\mu/n$, which depends on the unknown $\mu$.

Can we find a transformation $g$ such that the asymptotic variance of $g(\bar{X}_n)$ does **not** depend on $\mu$?

By the delta method, $\text{Var}(g(\bar{X}_n)) \approx [g'(\mu)]^2 \mu / n$. We want this to be a constant divided by $n$:

$$[g'(\mu)]^2 \mu = c \implies g'(\mu) = \frac{c'}{\sqrt{\mu}} \implies g(\mu) = c' \cdot 2\sqrt{\mu}.$$

So $g(x) = \sqrt{x}$ is the **variance-stabilising transformation** for the Poisson.

After the transformation:

$$\sqrt{\bar{X}_n} \stackrel{\cdot}{\sim} N\!\left(\sqrt{\mu}, \frac{1}{4n}\right).$$

The asymptotic variance $1/(4n)$ is free of $\mu$ -- mission accomplished.

### 7.13.3 Common Delta Method Results

| Statistic | $g(x)$ | $g'(x)$ | Asymptotic variance of $g(\bar{X}_n)$ |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Log mean | $\log(x)$ | $1/x$ | $\sigma^2 / (n\mu^2)$ |
| Reciprocal | $1/x$ | $-1/x^2$ | $\sigma^2 / (n\mu^4)$ |
| Square root | $\sqrt{x}$ | $1/(2\sqrt{x})$ | $\sigma^2 / (4n\mu)$ |
| Proportion odds | $\log(p/(1-p))$ | $1/(p(1-p))$ | $1/(np(1-p))$ |

## 7.14 Rate of CLT Convergence: Berry--Esseen

How fast does the CLT approximation improve? The **Berry--Esseen theorem** provides a bound.

**Theorem (Berry--Esseen).** If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are iid with $E[X_i] = 0$, $E[X_i^2] = \sigma^2$, and $E[|X_i|^3] = \rho < \infty$, then:

$$\sup_z \left|P(Z_n \le z) - \Phi(z)\right| \le \frac{C \rho}{\sigma^3 \sqrt{n}}$$

where $C \le 0.4748$ (the best known constant as of 2012) and $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.

The key message: the CLT error is $O(1/\sqrt{n})$. Distributions with large third moments (skewness) converge more slowly.


``` r
set.seed(42)

# Compare convergence rate for symmetric (Uniform) vs. skewed (Exp) distributions
n_vals <- c(5, 10, 30, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1000)
n_reps <- 20000

sup_error <- do.call(rbind, lapply(n_vals, function(n) {
  # Uniform(0,1): mu=0.5, sigma^2=1/12, rho = E[|X-mu|^3]
  z_unif <- replicate(n_reps, {
    x <- runif(n)
    (mean(x) - 0.5) / (1/sqrt(12) / sqrt(n))
  })

  # Exp(1): mu=1, sigma^2=1, rho = E[|X-1|^3]
  z_exp <- replicate(n_reps, {
    x <- rexp(n, 1)
    (mean(x) - 1) / (1 / sqrt(n))
  })

  z_grid <- seq(-4, 4, length.out = 500)

  err_unif <- max(abs(ecdf(z_unif)(z_grid) - pnorm(z_grid)))
  err_exp <- max(abs(ecdf(z_exp)(z_grid) - pnorm(z_grid)))

  data.table$data.table(
    n = c(n, n),
    sup_error = c(err_unif, err_exp),
    distribution = c("Uniform(0,1)", "Exponential(1)")
  )
}))
```


``` r
ggplot2$ggplot(sup_error, ggplot2$aes(x = n, y = sup_error, colour = distribution)) +
```

## Communicating to Stakeholders

When explaining the CLT and delta method to a non-technical audience:

- The CLT in one sentence: "When you average enough observations, the result follows a bell curve -- no matter what the individual data look like."
- A vivid analogy: "Imagine rolling a single die -- the outcome is equally likely to be 1 through 6 (flat, not bell-shaped). But if you roll 100 dice and average them, the average will be very close to 3.5 almost every time, following a bell curve. That is the CLT."
- For the delta method: "Once we know the average follows a bell curve, we can work out the bell curve for any smooth function of the average -- like the logarithm or the reciprocal."
- The practical punchline: "This is why confidence intervals work. The CLT guarantees that our estimate plus-or-minus two standard errors covers the truth about 95% of the time."
- Variance stabilisation: "Sometimes we transform the data (e.g., take the square root) so that the uncertainty is the same regardless of the true value. This makes fair comparisons possible."

## Quick Reference

| Result | Statement | Conditions |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **CLT** | $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$ | iid, $\sigma^2 < \infty$ |
| **Practical form** | $\bar{X}_n \stackrel{\cdot}{\sim} N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$ | Large $n$ |
| **Delta method** | $g(\bar{X}_n) \stackrel{\cdot}{\sim} N(g(\mu), [g'(\mu)]^2 \sigma^2/n)$ | $g'(\mu) \ne 0$ |
| **Berry--Esseen** | CDF error $\le C\rho/(\sigma^3\sqrt{n})$ | $E[\|X\|^3] < \infty$ |
| **Var-stab (Poisson)** | $\sqrt{\bar{X}} \stackrel{\cdot}{\sim} N(\sqrt{\mu}, 1/(4n))$ | Poisson data |
