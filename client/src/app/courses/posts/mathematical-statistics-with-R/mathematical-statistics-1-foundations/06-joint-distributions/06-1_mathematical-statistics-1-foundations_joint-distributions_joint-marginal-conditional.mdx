# Chapter 6 -- Joint Distributions and Multivariate Thinking

## Part 1: Joint, Marginal, and Conditional Distributions

Until now we have studied random variables one at a time. In practice, data almost always arrive as *collections* of measurements: a patient's height *and* Weight, a country's GDP *and* life expectancy, a penguin's bill length *and* flipper length. To model the relationship between two or more quantities we need the machinery of **joint distributions**.

This chapter extends everything we know about single random variables -- PMFs, PDFs, CDFs, expectations -- to the multivariate setting. The payoff is enormous: once we can describe how two variables behave *together*, we can define dependence, derive conditional expectations, and build the foundations for regression.

---

## 6.1 Joint Distributions

### 6.1.1 The Discrete Case: Joint PMF

Let $X$ and $Y$ be two discrete random variables defined on the same probability space. The **joint probability mass function** is:

$$p_{X,Y}(x, y) = P(X = x \text{ and } Y = y).$$

This function must satisfy two properties:

1. **Non-negativity:** $p_{X,Y}(x, y) \ge 0$ for all $(x, y)$.
2. **Normalisation:** $\sum_x \sum_y p_{X,Y}(x, y) = 1$.

The joint PMF contains *all* the probabilistic information about $X$ and $Y$ considered together. It tells us the probability of every possible pair of outcomes.

**Example.** Suppose we roll two fair dice. Let $X$ be the value on the first die and $Y$ the value on the second. Then each of the 36 pairs $(x, y)$ where $x, y \in \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$ has joint probability:

$$p_{X,Y}(x, y) = \frac{1}{36}.$$

### 6.1.2 Constructing a Joint PMF Table

``` r
dice[, prob := 1 / 36]

dice[, S := X + Y]

# Distribution of S
```

### 6.1.3 The Continuous Case: Joint PDF

For continuous random variables $X$ and $Y$, the **joint probability density function** $f_{X,Y}(x, y)$ is defined by the property:

$$P\bigl((X, Y) \in A\bigr) = \iint_A f_{X,Y}(x, y) \, dx \, dy$$

for any region $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$. The density must satisfy:

1. **Non-negativity:** $f_{X,Y}(x, y) \ge 0$ for all $(x, y)$.
2. **Normalisation:** $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y) \, dx \, dy = 1$.

Unlike the discrete case, $f_{X,Y}(x, y)$ is *not* a probability -- it is a density. Only when we integrate over a region do we obtain a probability.

### 6.1.4 Example: Uniform Distribution on a Triangle

Consider the joint density:

$$f_{X,Y}(x, y) = 2, \quad 0 \le x \le 1, \; 0 \le y \le x$$

and $f_{X,Y}(x, y) = 0$ elsewhere.

**Verify normalisation:**

$$\int_0^1 \int_0^x 2 \, dy \, dx = \int_0^1 2x \, dx = \left[x^2\right]_0^1 = 1. \quad \checkmark$$

The support is the triangle below the line $y = x$ in the unit square.

### 6.1.5 The Joint CDF

The **joint cumulative distribution function** is defined for both discrete and continuous cases as:

$$F_{X,Y}(x, y) = P(X \le x, \; Y \le y).$$

For the continuous case, the joint PDF can be recovered from the joint CDF by differentiation:

$$f_{X,Y}(x, y) = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x \, \partial y} F_{X,Y}(x, y).$$

## 6.2 Marginal Distributions

### 6.2.1 The Key Idea

If we know the joint distribution of $(X, Y)$, we can always recover the distribution of $X$ alone (or $Y$ alone) by "integrating out" (or summing out) the other variable. These recovered distributions are called **marginal distributions**.

### 6.2.2 Discrete Case

The **marginal PMF** of $X$ is obtained by summing the joint PMF over all possible values of $Y$:

$$p_X(x) = \sum_y p_{X,Y}(x, y).$$

Similarly, the marginal PMF of $Y$ is:

$$p_Y(y) = \sum_x p_{X,Y}(x, y).$$

### 6.2.3 Derivation from First Principles

Why does this work? By the law of total probability:

$$P(X = x) = \sum_y P(X = x, \; Y = y)$$

because the events $\{Y = y\}$ for all possible $y$ form a partition of the sample space. We are simply saying: the probability that $X$ takes a particular value equals the sum over all the ways it can happen, considering every possible companion value for $Y$.

### 6.2.4 Example: Two Dice Marginals


``` r
# Marginal distribution of X (first die)
marginal_X <- dice[, .(marginal_prob = sum(prob)), by = X]
marginal_X

# Marginal distribution of Y (second die)
marginal_Y <- dice[, .(marginal_prob = sum(prob)), by = Y]
```

### 6.2.5 Continuous Case

The **marginal PDF** of $X$ is obtained by integrating the joint density over $y$:

$$f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y) \, dy.$$

**Example:** For our triangular density $f_{X,Y}(x, y) = 2$ on $0 \le y \le x \le 1$:

$$f_X(x) = \int_0^x 2 \, dy = 2x, \quad 0 \le x \le 1.$$

*Verify:* $\int_0^1 2x \, dx = 1$. $\checkmark$ So $X$ has a linearly increasing density on $[0, 1]$ -- values near 1 are more likely than values near 0.

For $Y$:

$$f_Y(y) = \int_y^1 2 \, dx = 2(1 - y), \quad 0 \le y \le 1.$$

*Verify:* $\int_0^1 2(1-y) \, dy = 1$. $\checkmark$ The marginal of $Y$ is linearly *decreasing*.

## 6.3 Conditional Distributions

### 6.3.1 Definition

Given the joint distribution of $(X, Y)$, the **conditional distribution** of $Y$ given $X = x$ describes how $Y$ behaves once we know the value of $X$.

**Discrete case:**

$$p_{Y|X}(y | x) = \frac{p_{X,Y}(x, y)}{p_X(x)}, \quad \text{provided } p_X(x) > 0.$$

**Continuous case:**

$$f_{Y|X}(y | x) = \frac{f_{X,Y}(x, y)}{f_X(x)}, \quad \text{provided } f_X(x) > 0.$$

This is the multivariate analogue of Bayes' theorem: the conditional equals the joint divided by the marginal.

### 6.3.2 Derivation

The formula follows directly from the definition of conditional probability. For the discrete case:

$$P(Y = y | X = x) = \frac{P(X = x, Y = y)}{P(X = x)} = \frac{p_{X,Y}(x, y)}{p_X(x)}.$$

The continuous case requires more care (we are conditioning on an event of probability zero), but the intuition is the same: we "slice" the joint density at $X = x$ and renormalise so that the conditional density integrates to 1 over $y$.

### 6.3.3 Verification: Conditional Integrates to 1

For the continuous case:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{Y|X}(y | x) \, dy = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{f_{X,Y}(x, y)}{f_X(x)} \, dy = \frac{1}{f_X(x)} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y) \, dy = \frac{f_X(x)}{f_X(x)} = 1. \quad \checkmark$$

### 6.3.4 Example: Triangular Density

For the triangular density with $f_{X,Y}(x, y) = 2$ on $0 \le y \le x \le 1$ and $f_X(x) = 2x$:

$$f_{Y|X}(y | x) = \frac{2}{2x} = \frac{1}{x}, \quad 0 \le y \le x.$$

So given $X = x$, the variable $Y$ is **uniformly distributed** on $[0, x]$. This makes geometric sense: if we slice the triangle at a particular $x$ value, we get a horizontal segment from $y = 0$ to $y = x$, and the density is constant along that segment.

### 6.3.5 Conditional Expectation

The **conditional expectation** of $Y$ given $X = x$ is:

$$E[Y | X = x] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} y \, f_{Y|X}(y | x) \, dy.$$

For our triangular example:

$$E[Y | X = x] = \int_0^x y \cdot \frac{1}{x} \, dy = \frac{1}{x} \cdot \frac{x^2}{2} = \frac{x}{2}.$$

So the expected value of $Y$ given $X = x$ is $x/2$ -- the midpoint of the interval $[0, x]$. As $X$ increases, the conditional mean of $Y$ increases proportionally.

## 6.4 Independence

### 6.4.1 Definition

Two random variables $X$ and $Y$ are **independent** if and only if their joint distribution factors into the product of the marginals:

$$f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y) \quad \text{for all } (x, y).$$

(For the discrete case, replace $f$ with $p$.)

### 6.4.2 Equivalent Formulations

The following statements are all equivalent for two random variables $X$ and $Y$:

1. $f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y)$ for all $(x, y)$.
2. $f_{Y|X}(y|x) = f_Y(y)$ for all $x, y$ (knowing $X$ tells us nothing about $Y$).
3. $F_{X,Y}(x, y) = F_X(x) \cdot F_Y(y)$ for all $(x, y)$.
4. $E[g(X) h(Y)] = E[g(X)] \cdot E[h(Y)]$ for all functions $g, h$.

**Proof of 1 $\Leftrightarrow$ 2:** If $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y)$, then:

$$f_{Y|X}(y|x) = \frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_X(x)} = \frac{f_X(x) f_Y(y)}{f_X(x)} = f_Y(y).$$

Conversely, if $f_{Y|X}(y|x) = f_Y(y)$, then $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_{Y|X}(y|x) \cdot f_X(x) = f_Y(y) \cdot f_X(x)$. $\square$

### 6.4.3 Testing Independence: The Triangular Example

For our triangular density, $f_{X,Y}(x, y) = 2$ but $f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y) = 2x \cdot 2(1-y) = 4x(1-y)$. Since $2 \neq 4x(1-y)$ in general, $X$ and $Y$ are **not independent** in this example. This agrees with our intuition: $Y$ is constrained to lie below $X$, so knowing $X$ gives information about $Y$.

### 6.4.4 When Variables Are Independent


``` r
set.seed(42)

  X = rnorm(n_sim),
```

## Communicating to Stakeholders

When explaining joint distributions to a non-technical audience:

- Avoid saying "the joint PMF." Instead say: "Here is the probability of observing each combination of outcomes."
- Marginals are intuitive: "If we ignore one measurement and focus only on the other, here is what we see."
- Conditional distributions answer the most natural question: "Given that we know X, what can we expect for Y?"
- Use 2D histograms and contour plots freely -- most people understand heat maps. Label the axes carefully and explain that darker colours mean higher frequency.
- The independence concept translates well: "Knowing one variable tells us nothing new about the other." Dependence means "they move together (or against each other) in some way."

## Quick Reference

| Concept | Discrete | Continuous |
|:--------|:---------|:-----------|
| **Joint** | $p_{X,Y}(x,y) = P(X=x, Y=y)$ | $P(A) = \iint_A f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dx\,dy$ |
| **Marginal of X** | $p_X(x) = \sum_y p_{X,Y}(x,y)$ | $f_X(x) = \int f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy$ |
| **Conditional of Y given X** | $p_{Y\mid X}(y\mid x) = \frac{p_{X,Y}(x,y)}{p_X(x)}$ | $f_{Y\mid X}(y\mid x) = \frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_X(x)}$ |
| **Independence** | $p_{X,Y}(x,y) = p_X(x)\,p_Y(y)$ | $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_X(x)\,f_Y(y)$ |
| **Conditional expectation** | $E[Y\mid X=x] = \sum_y y\,p_{Y\mid X}(y\mid x)$ | $E[Y\mid X=x] = \int y\,f_{Y\mid X}(y\mid x)\,dy$ |
