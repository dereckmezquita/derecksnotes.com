# Chapter 17 -- Regression Diagnostics

## Part 2: Influence and Remedies

Part 1 introduced the four diagnostic plots and showed how to detect assumption violations visually. In this part we quantify influence precisely -- deriving leverage and Cook's distance from first principles -- and then develop remedies: robust (sandwich) standard errors for heteroscedasticity, the Box-Cox transformation for non-normality, and strategies for non-linearity. We return to the Boston housing and NHANES BMI models to apply these tools.

## 17.11 Leverage -- Derivation and Interpretation

### 17.11.1 The Hat Matrix Revisited

From Chapter 16, the hat matrix is:

$$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$$

The fitted values are $\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y}$, so each $\hat{Y}_i$ is a weighted sum of all observed $Y_j$:

$$\hat{Y}_i = \sum_{j=1}^n h_{ij} Y_j$$

The leverage $h_{ii}$ is the weight that observation $i$'s response places on its own fitted value. High leverage means the observation "pulls" the fitted surface towards itself.

### 17.11.2 Derivation of the Leverage Formula

For observation $i$ with predictor vector $\mathbf{x}_i$ (the $i$th row of $\mathbf{X}$, written as a column):

$$h_{ii} = \mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i$$

**Proof.** $h_{ij}$ is the $(i,j)$ element of $\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$. The $i$th row of $\mathbf{X}$ is $\mathbf{x}_i'$, so $h_{ii} = \mathbf{x}_i'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_i$. $\square$

This is a **Mahalanobis-type distance**: it measures how far $\mathbf{x}_i$ is from the centroid of the predictor space, scaled by the covariance structure.

### 17.11.3 Properties

- $1/n \le h_{ii} \le 1$ (the lower bound holds when the design includes an intercept).
- $\sum_{i=1}^n h_{ii} = \text{tr}(\mathbf{H}) = p$.
- Average leverage: $\bar{h} = p/n$.
- Rule of thumb: $h_{ii} > 2p/n$ flags high leverage.
