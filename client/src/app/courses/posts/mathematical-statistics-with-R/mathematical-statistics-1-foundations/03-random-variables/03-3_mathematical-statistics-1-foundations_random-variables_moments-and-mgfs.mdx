## 3.8 Moments

The mean and variance are the first two in a family of summary quantities called **moments**. Higher moments capture additional features of a distribution's shape — in particular, asymmetry and tail heaviness.

### Raw Moments

The **$k$-th (raw) moment** of a random variable $X$ is

$$
\mu_k' = E[X^k]
$$

For the discrete case: $\mu_k' = \sum_x x^k \, p(x)$. For the continuous case: $\mu_k' = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x^k f(x)\,dx$.

The first raw moment is the mean: $\mu_1' = E[X] = \mu$.

### Central Moments

The **$k$-th central moment** centres the calculation at the mean:

$$
\mu_k = E\left[(X - \mu)^k\right]
$$

- $\mu_1 = E[X - \mu] = 0$ (always, by linearity of expectation).
- $\mu_2 = E[(X - \mu)^2] = \text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$.

Central moments measure the shape of the distribution *relative to its centre*.

### Skewness: The Third Standardised Moment

The **skewness** of $X$ is the third central moment, standardised by $\sigma^3$:

$$
\gamma_1 = E\left[\left(\frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\right)^3\right] = \frac{\mu_3}{\sigma^3} = \frac{E[(X - \mu)^3]}{(\text{Var}(X))^{3/2}}
$$

Standardising by $\sigma^3$ makes skewness dimensionless — it does not depend on the scale of $X$.

**Interpretation:**

- $\gamma_1 > 0$: **positive skew** (right skew). The right tail is longer; the distribution leans left with a tail stretching to the right.
- $\gamma_1 < 0$: **negative skew** (left skew). The left tail is longer; the distribution leans right with a tail stretching to the left.
- $\gamma_1 = 0$: the distribution is symmetric about the mean (necessary but not sufficient condition for symmetry).

In Chapter 1 we computed sample skewness from data. Now we have the formal definition: skewness is a property of the *distribution*, and sample skewness is its estimator.

### Kurtosis: The Fourth Standardised Moment

The **kurtosis** of $X$ is the fourth central moment, standardised by $\sigma^4$:

$$
\gamma_2 = E\left[\left(\frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\right)^4\right] = \frac{\mu_4}{\sigma^4} = \frac{E[(X - \mu)^4]}{(\text{Var}(X))^2}
$$

The normal distribution has kurtosis exactly 3. To make comparisons easier, we often report **excess kurtosis**:

$$
\text{excess kurtosis} = \gamma_2 - 3
$$

**Interpretation:**

- $\gamma_2 - 3 > 0$ (**leptokurtic**): heavier tails than the normal distribution. Extreme values are more likely.
- $\gamma_2 - 3 = 0$ (**mesokurtic**): tails like the normal distribution.
- $\gamma_2 - 3 < 0$ (**platykurtic**): lighter tails than the normal. Extreme values are less likely.

A common misconception is that kurtosis measures "peakedness." It does not — it primarily measures the weight of the tails and the probability of extreme values.

### Computing Moments from Simulation


``` r
set.seed(42)

#' Compute the k-th central moment from a sample
#'
#' @param x Numeric vector of observations
#' @param k The order of the moment
#' @return The k-th sample central moment
my_central_moment <- function(x, k) {
  mean((x - mean(x))^k)
}

#' Compute skewness from a sample
my_skewness <- function(x) {
  m3 <- my_central_moment(x, 3)
  m2 <- my_central_moment(x, 2)
  m3 / m2^(3/2)
}

#' Compute kurtosis from a sample
my_kurtosis <- function(x) {
  m4 <- my_central_moment(x, 4)
  m2 <- my_central_moment(x, 2)
  m4 / m2^2
}

n <- 100000

# Normal distribution: skewness = 0, kurtosis = 3
x_norm <- rnorm(n)
# Normal distribution: skewness = 0, kurtosis = 3
data.frame(
  distribution = c("Normal", "Normal", "Normal"),

  theoretical = c(0, 3, 0)
)
```

## 3.9 Moment Generating Functions

### Motivation

The **moment generating function** (MGF) is one of the most powerful tools in theoretical statistics. It can feel abstract on first encounter, but it pays enormous dividends in later chapters. Here is why:

1. The MGF encodes *all* the moments of a distribution in a single function.
2. Differentiating the MGF and evaluating at zero extracts moments effortlessly.
3. The MGF of a sum of independent random variables is the *product* of their individual MGFs — this turns convolutions into simple multiplications.
4. If two distributions have the same MGF, they are the same distribution.

We will use MGFs to prove the Central Limit Theorem in Chapter 7.

### Definition

The **moment generating function** of a random variable $X$ is

$$
M_X(t) = E\left[e^{tX}\right]
$$

provided this expectation exists (is finite) for all $t$ in some open interval containing zero.

For a discrete random variable: $M_X(t) = \sum_x e^{tx} p(x)$.

For a continuous random variable: $M_X(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{tx} f(x)\,dx$.

In plain English: $M_X(t)$ is the expected value of $e^{tX}$, viewed as a function of the auxiliary variable $t$. The variable $t$ is not random — it is a free parameter that we will differentiate with respect to.

### Why "Moment Generating"?

**Theorem.** If the MGF exists in an open interval around zero, then

$$
M_X^{(k)}(0) = E[X^k]
$$

That is, the $k$-th derivative of the MGF evaluated at $t = 0$ gives the $k$-th raw moment.

*Proof.* Recall the Taylor expansion of the exponential function:

$$
e^{tX} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(tX)^k}{k!} = 1 + tX + \frac{t^2 X^2}{2!} + \frac{t^3 X^3}{3!} + \cdots
$$

Taking expectations term by term (justified by the convergence assumption):

$$
M_X(t) = E\left[e^{tX}\right] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{t^k E[X^k]}{k!} = 1 + t E[X] + \frac{t^2 E[X^2]}{2!} + \frac{t^3 E[X^3]}{3!} + \cdots
$$

Now differentiate with respect to $t$:

$$
M_X'(t) = E[X] + t E[X^2] + \frac{t^2 E[X^3]}{2!} + \cdots
$$

Setting $t = 0$: $M_X'(0) = E[X]$.

Differentiating again:

$$
M_X''(t) = E[X^2] + t E[X^3] + \cdots
$$

Setting $t = 0$: $M_X''(0) = E[X^2]$.

In general, the $k$-th derivative at $t = 0$ picks out $E[X^k]$ because all terms with $t$ vanish. $\square$

This is why the function is called "moment generating" — differentiation and evaluation at zero generates moments one by one.

### The Uniqueness Theorem

**Theorem (Uniqueness of MGFs).** If $M_X(t) = M_Y(t)$ for all $t$ in an open interval containing zero, then $X$ and $Y$ have the same distribution.

We state this theorem without proof (the proof uses complex analysis and Laplace transforms). Its power lies in applications: to show that a random variable has a particular distribution, it suffices to show that its MGF matches.

### Example 1: MGF of a Bernoulli Random Variable

Let $X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$, so $P(X = 1) = p$ and $P(X = 0) = 1 - p$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
M_X(t) &= E[e^{tX}] \\
&= e^{t \cdot 0} P(X = 0) + e^{t \cdot 1} P(X = 1) \\
&= (1-p) \cdot 1 + p \cdot e^t \\
&= 1 - p + pe^t
\end{aligned}
$$

Let us verify by extracting moments:

$$
M_X'(t) = pe^t \implies M_X'(0) = p = E[X] \quad \checkmark
$$

$$
M_X''(t) = pe^t \implies M_X''(0) = p = E[X^2]
$$

$$
\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = p - p^2 = p(1-p) \quad \checkmark
$$

### Example 2: MGF of a Binomial Random Variable

Let $X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$. Since $X = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n$ where the $X_i$ are independent $\text{Bernoulli}(p)$ random variables, and the MGF of a sum of independent RVs is the product of their MGFs (we prove this below), we have:

$$
M_X(t) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} M_{X_i}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} (1 - p + pe^t) = (1 - p + pe^t)^n
$$

Alternatively, we can derive this directly from the definition:

$$
\begin{aligned}
M_X(t) &= E[e^{tX}] = \sum_{k=0}^{n} e^{tk} \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \\
&= \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} (pe^t)^k (1-p)^{n-k} \\
&= (pe^t + 1 - p)^n
\end{aligned}
$$

where the last step uses the binomial theorem: $\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^k b^{n-k} = (a + b)^n$ with $a = pe^t$ and $b = 1-p$.

### Recovering E[X] and Var(X) from the Binomial MGF

Let us differentiate $M_X(t) = (1 - p + pe^t)^n$.

**First derivative** (using the chain rule):

$$
M_X'(t) = n(1 - p + pe^t)^{n-1} \cdot pe^t
$$

Evaluating at $t = 0$:

$$
M_X'(0) = n(1 - p + p)^{n-1} \cdot p = n \cdot 1 \cdot p = np = E[X] \quad \checkmark
$$

**Second derivative** (product rule on $n(1-p+pe^t)^{n-1} \cdot pe^t$):

$$
\begin{aligned}
M_X''(t) &= n(n-1)(1-p+pe^t)^{n-2}(pe^t)^2 + n(1-p+pe^t)^{n-1}(pe^t)
\end{aligned}
$$

Evaluating at $t = 0$:

$$
M_X''(0) = n(n-1)p^2 + np = E[X^2]
$$

Therefore:

$$
\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = n(n-1)p^2 + np - (np)^2 = n^2p^2 - np^2 + np - n^2p^2 = np - np^2 = np(1-p) \quad \checkmark
$$

This is the standard result: for a Binomial$(n, p)$ random variable, $E[X] = np$ and $\text{Var}(X) = np(1-p)$.


``` r
# Verify numerically for Binomial(10, 0.3)
n <- 10; p <- 0.3

# Via numerical differentiation of the MGF
mgf_binom <- function(t) (1 - p + p * exp(t))^n

h <- 1e-6
e_x_num <- (mgf_binom(h) - mgf_binom(-h)) / (2 * h)
e_x2_num <- (mgf_binom(h) - 2 * mgf_binom(0) + mgf_binom(-h)) / h^2
var_num <- e_x2_num - e_x_num^2

data.frame(
  quantity = c("E[X]", "Var(X)"),
  theoretical = round(c(n * p, n * p * (1 - p)), 4),
  via_MGF = round(c(e_x_num, var_num), 4)
)
```

### Key Property: MGF of a Sum of Independent RVs

**Theorem.** If $X$ and $Y$ are **independent** random variables, then

$$
M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t) \cdot M_Y(t)
$$

*Proof.*

$$
\begin{aligned}
M_{X+Y}(t) &= E\left[e^{t(X+Y)}\right] \\
&= E\left[e^{tX} \cdot e^{tY}\right] \\
&= E\left[e^{tX}\right] \cdot E\left[e^{tY}\right] \quad \text{(by independence)} \\
&= M_X(t) \cdot M_Y(t) \qquad \square
\end{aligned}
$$

The critical step is the third line: for independent random variables, the expectation of a product equals the product of expectations. This is proved in Chapter 6.

**Why this is so powerful:** Finding the distribution of $X + Y$ normally requires a convolution — a potentially difficult integral. The MGF converts this convolution into simple multiplication. Combined with the uniqueness theorem, we can identify the distribution of a sum by recognising the form of the product of MGFs.

This extends to any finite number of independent random variables:

$$
M_{X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} M_{X_i}(t)
$$

### Numerical Approximation of the MGF

When a closed-form MGF is not available, we can approximate $M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ by simulation: draw many samples from $X$, compute $e^{tX}$ for each, and take the average.


``` r
set.seed(42)

# Approximate the MGF of Binomial(10, 0.3) by simulation
n_binom <- 10; p_binom <- 0.3
n_sim <- 100000

samples <- rbinom(n_sim, size = n_binom, prob = p_binom)

# Evaluate MGF at several values of t
t_vals <- c(-1, -0.5, 0, 0.5, 1)

data.frame(
  t = t_vals,
  theoretical = vapply(t_vals, function(t) (1 - p_binom + p_binom * exp(t))^n_binom, numeric(1)),
  simulated = vapply(t_vals, function(t) mean(exp(t * samples)), numeric(1))
)
```

### A Note on Existence

Not all distributions have moment generating functions. The MGF exists only if $E[e^{tX}]$ is finite for $t$ in an open interval around zero. Distributions with very heavy tails — such as the Cauchy distribution or the log-normal distribution — fail this condition. When the MGF does not exist, an alternative called the **characteristic function** $\varphi_X(t) = E[e^{itX}]$ (using complex exponentials) always exists, but its study requires complex analysis and is beyond our scope.

### Preview: The Central Limit Theorem

The MGF machinery we have developed here will be essential in Chapter 7, where we prove the **Central Limit Theorem** — arguably the most important result in all of statistics. The proof strategy is:

1. Write the MGF of the standardised sample mean $\bar{X}_n$.
2. Use the product rule for independent MGFs.
3. Show that as $n \to \infty$, this MGF converges to the MGF of a standard normal distribution.
4. Apply the uniqueness theorem to conclude convergence in distribution.

The fact that sums become products is what makes this proof tractable.

## Communicating to Stakeholders

Moments beyond mean and variance rarely appear in stakeholder-facing reports. However, skewness and kurtosis can be communicated effectively:

- **Skewness**: "The distribution of customer spending is positively skewed — most customers spend a moderate amount, but a small number of high spenders pull the average up." This explains why the median might be more representative than the mean.

- **Kurtosis**: "The distribution of daily returns has heavy tails — extreme market movements (both gains and losses) occur more often than a normal distribution would predict." This is directly relevant to risk management.

Moment generating functions are strictly a theoretical tool and should never appear in stakeholder communications. Their value is in proving results that *do* have practical implications.

**Exercise 3.14.** Let $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$ with PMF $P(X = k) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k / k!$ for $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$. (a) Derive the MGF $M_X(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$. (b) Use the MGF to find $E[X]$ and $\text{Var}(X)$. (c) If $X_1 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1)$ and $X_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_2)$ are independent, use MGFs to show that $X_1 + X_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$.

**Exercise 3.15.** Write an R function `mgf_numerical(samples, t)` that estimates the MGF of a distribution from a sample at a given value of $t$. Use it to estimate the MGF of the standard normal at $t = 0.5$ and compare to the known result $M_X(t) = e^{t^2/2}$.

**Exercise 3.16.** Prove that $M_X(0) = 1$ for any random variable whose MGF exists. Explain why this must be the case.

**Exercise 3.17.** Let $X$ have MGF $M_X(t) = (0.4e^t + 0.6)^8$. Without identifying the distribution, compute $E[X]$ and $\text{Var}(X)$ by differentiating the MGF.

**Exercise 3.18 (Challenge).** Prove that if $X$ is symmetric about its mean $\mu$ (i.e., $X - \mu$ and $\mu - X$ have the same distribution), then all odd central moments are zero. In particular, $\gamma_1 = 0$. *Hint:* use the substitution $u = -(x - \mu)$.
