# Chapter 5 -- Continuous Distributions

## Part 2: Normal and Derived Distributions

The normal (Gaussian) distribution is the most important distribution in all of statistics. It appears everywhere -- not by coincidence, but because of deep mathematical theorems (the central limit theorem, the maximum entropy principle) that force it to arise in a vast range of settings. In this part, we derive its properties from scratch, then construct the three distributions that flow from it: the chi-square, Student's $t$, and Fisher's $F$. These "derived" distributions are the backbone of classical statistical inference.

---

## 5.4 The Normal (Gaussian) Distribution

### 5.4.1 Why the Normal?

Three independent lines of reasoning all point to the same distribution:

1. **The Central Limit Theorem (preview).** The sum (or average) of many independent random variables, regardless of their individual distributions, tends toward a normal distribution. We prove this rigorously in Chapter 7, but the intuition is simple: aggregation washes out the idiosyncrasies of individual variables, leaving a universal bell shape.

2. **Maximum entropy.** Among all distributions on $(-\infty, \infty)$ with a given mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the normal has the *highest entropy* -- it is the distribution that makes the fewest assumptions beyond specifying the first two moments. If all you know is the mean and variance, the normal is the most honest guess.

3. **Stability under addition.** The normal is *closed* under linear combinations: if $X$ and $Y$ are independent normals, then $aX + bY$ is also normal. This algebraic convenience makes the normal uniquely tractable.

### 5.4.2 The PDF

A random variable $X$ has a **normal distribution** with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ -- written $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ -- if its PDF is:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \quad -\infty < x < \infty.$$

The special case $\mu = 0$, $\sigma = 1$ is the **standard normal**, denoted $Z \sim N(0, 1)$:

$$\phi(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}.$$

**Verifying it integrates to 1.** This is the famous Gaussian integral. The trick is to square the integral and convert to polar coordinates:

$$\left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2/2}\,dz\right)^2 = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x^2 + y^2)/2}\,dx\,dy = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty r\,e^{-r^2/2}\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi.$$

Therefore $\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2/2}\,dz = \sqrt{2\pi}$, confirming that $\phi(z)$ integrates to 1.

### 5.4.3 Deriving the Moments

**Mean of the standard normal.** The function $z \cdot e^{-z^2/2}$ is odd (antisymmetric about zero), so $E[Z] = 0$.

**Variance of the standard normal.** Integration by parts with $u = z$ and $dv = z e^{-z^2/2}\,dz$:

$$E[Z^2] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty z^2 e^{-z^2/2}\,dz = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \left[-z e^{-z^2/2}\Big|_{-\infty}^\infty + \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2/2}\,dz\right] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot \sqrt{2\pi} = 1.$$

So $\text{Var}(Z) = E[Z^2] - (E[Z])^2 = 1 - 0 = 1$. $\checkmark$

**General normal.** If $X = \mu + \sigma Z$, then $E[X] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$, confirming the parameters.

### 5.4.4 The Moment-Generating Function

The MGF of $Z \sim N(0, 1)$ is:

$$M_Z(t) = E[e^{tZ}] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tz} e^{-z^2/2}\,dz.$$

Complete the square in the exponent: $tz - z^2/2 = -(z - t)^2/2 + t^2/2$. So:

$$M_Z(t) = e^{t^2/2} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(z-t)^2/2}\,dz = e^{t^2/2}.$$

For $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$: $M_X(t) = e^{\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2}$.

### 5.4.5 Linear Combinations of Normals

**Theorem.** If $X_1 \sim N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$ and $X_2 \sim N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$ are independent, then for constants $a, b$:

$$aX_1 + bX_2 \sim N(a\mu_1 + b\mu_2,\; a^2\sigma_1^2 + b^2\sigma_2^2).$$

**Proof via MGF.**

$$M_{aX_1 + bX_2}(t) = M_{X_1}(at) \cdot M_{X_2}(bt) = e^{a\mu_1 t + a^2\sigma_1^2 t^2/2} \cdot e^{b\mu_2 t + b^2\sigma_2^2 t^2/2} = e^{(a\mu_1 + b\mu_2)t + (a^2\sigma_1^2 + b^2\sigma_2^2)t^2/2}.$$

This is the MGF of $N(a\mu_1 + b\mu_2, a^2\sigma_1^2 + b^2\sigma_2^2)$. Since the MGF uniquely determines the distribution, the result follows. $\square$

### 5.4.6 Z-Scores: Standardisation

If $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, the **Z-score** or **standardised value** is:

$$Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \sim N(0, 1).$$

This allows us to convert any normal probability into a standard normal probability. In R, the four standard functions handle this:

- `dnorm(x, mean, sd)` -- density
- `pnorm(x, mean, sd)` -- CDF (probability to the left)
- `qnorm(p, mean, sd)` -- quantile (inverse CDF)
- `rnorm(n, mean, sd)` -- random generation

## 5.5 Distributions Derived from the Normal

The normal distribution is a *mother* distribution. Three children flow from it: the chi-square, the $t$, and the $F$. Each is defined as a specific algebraic transformation of normal random variables, and each plays a starring role in classical inference.

### 5.5.1 The Chi-Square Distribution

**Definition.** If $Z_1, Z_2, \ldots, Z_k$ are independent $N(0, 1)$ random variables, then

$$Q = Z_1^2 + Z_2^2 + \cdots + Z_k^2 \sim \chi^2_k.$$

The parameter $k$ is called the **degrees of freedom** (df).

**Deriving the PDF for $k = 1$.** When $k = 1$, $Q = Z^2$ where $Z \sim N(0, 1)$. We use the CDF method.

For $q > 0$:

$$F_Q(q) = P(Z^2 \le q) = P(-\sqrt{q} \le Z \le \sqrt{q}) = 2\Phi(\sqrt{q}) - 1,$$

where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. Differentiating:

$$f_Q(q) = 2\phi(\sqrt{q}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{q}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{q}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-q/2} = \frac{1}{2^{1/2} \Gamma(1/2)} q^{1/2 - 1} e^{-q/2}.$$

This is a $\text{Gamma}(1/2, 1/2)$ density. Recall from Part 1 that $\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$.

**General $k$: using the Gamma connection.** Since $Z_i^2 \sim \text{Gamma}(1/2, 1/2)$ and the sum of independent Gamma variables with the same rate is Gamma:

$$Q = \sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2 \sim \text{Gamma}(k/2, 1/2) = \chi^2_k.$$

The PDF is therefore:

$$f(q; k) = \frac{1}{2^{k/2}\,\Gamma(k/2)}\, q^{k/2 - 1}\, e^{-q/2}, \quad q > 0.$$

**Moments:**

$$E[Q] = k, \qquad \text{Var}(Q) = 2k.$$

These follow directly from the Gamma moments: $E = \alpha/\lambda = (k/2)/(1/2) = k$.

### 5.5.2 Student's $t$-Distribution

**Definition.** If $Z \sim N(0, 1)$ and $V \sim \chi^2_k$ are independent, then

$$T = \frac{Z}{\sqrt{V/k}} \sim t_k.$$

**Deriving the PDF.** This derivation is dense but important. We use the joint density and integrate out the chi-square variable.

The joint density of $(Z, V)$ is:

$$f_{Z,V}(z, v) = \phi(z) \cdot f_{\chi^2_k}(v) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2} \cdot \frac{v^{k/2-1} e^{-v/2}}{2^{k/2}\Gamma(k/2)}.$$

We want the density of $T = Z / \sqrt{V/k}$. Substitute $z = t\sqrt{v/k}$ (so $dz = \sqrt{v/k}\,dt$) and integrate over $v$:

$$f_T(t) = \int_0^\infty \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-t^2 v/(2k)} \cdot \frac{v^{k/2-1} e^{-v/2}}{2^{k/2}\Gamma(k/2)} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{v}{k}}\,dv.$$

Collect powers of $v$ and exponentials:

$$f_T(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi k}\,2^{k/2}\Gamma(k/2)} \int_0^\infty v^{(k+1)/2 - 1} \exp\left(-\frac{v}{2}\left(1 + \frac{t^2}{k}\right)\right)dv.$$

The integral is a Gamma integral. With $\beta = \frac{1}{2}(1 + t^2/k)$:

$$\int_0^\infty v^{(k+1)/2 - 1} e^{-\beta v}\,dv = \frac{\Gamma((k+1)/2)}{\beta^{(k+1)/2}}.$$

Substituting and simplifying yields:

$$\boxed{f_T(t) = \frac{\Gamma\!\left(\frac{k+1}{2}\right)}{\sqrt{k\pi}\;\Gamma\!\left(\frac{k}{2}\right)} \left(1 + \frac{t^2}{k}\right)^{-(k+1)/2}, \quad -\infty < t < \infty.}$$

**Key properties:**

- $E[T] = 0$ (for $k > 1$); the distribution is symmetric.
- $\text{Var}(T) = k/(k - 2)$ for $k > 2$; heavier tails than the normal.
- As $k \to \infty$, $t_k \to N(0, 1)$.

### 5.5.3 Fisher's $F$-Distribution

**Definition.** If $U \sim \chi^2_{d_1}$ and $V \sim \chi^2_{d_2}$ are independent, then

$$F = \frac{U/d_1}{V/d_2} \sim F_{d_1, d_2}.$$

**Deriving the PDF.** The approach mirrors the $t$-derivation. Write $F = \frac{d_2}{d_1} \cdot \frac{U}{V}$ and use the ratio distribution formula with Gamma densities.

Starting from independent Gamma variables $U \sim \text{Gamma}(d_1/2, 1/2)$ and $V \sim \text{Gamma}(d_2/2, 1/2)$, and setting $W = U/V$, the density of $W$ involves a Beta function:

$$f_W(w) = \frac{\Gamma((d_1+d_2)/2)}{\Gamma(d_1/2)\Gamma(d_2/2)} \cdot \frac{w^{d_1/2-1}}{(1+w)^{(d_1+d_2)/2}}, \quad w > 0.$$

Applying the linear transformation $F = (d_2/d_1)W$ yields:

$$\boxed{f_F(x) = \frac{\Gamma\!\left(\frac{d_1+d_2}{2}\right)}{\Gamma\!\left(\frac{d_1}{2}\right)\Gamma\!\left(\frac{d_2}{2}\right)} \left(\frac{d_1}{d_2}\right)^{d_1/2} \frac{x^{d_1/2-1}}{\left(1 + \frac{d_1}{d_2}x\right)^{(d_1+d_2)/2}}, \quad x > 0.}$$

**Key properties:**

- $E[F] = d_2/(d_2 - 2)$ for $d_2 > 2$.
- The $F$-distribution is always right-skewed.
- If $T \sim t_k$, then $T^2 \sim F_{1, k}$.

### 5.5.5 R: Verifying the Definitions by Simulation

We construct each derived distribution from scratch using only `rnorm()`, confirming the definitions.


``` r
set.seed(42)
n_sim <- 50000

# Chi-square(5): sum of 5 squared standard normals
k <- 5
z_mat <- matrix(rnorm(n_sim * k), nrow = n_sim, ncol = k)
chisq_sim <- rowSums(z_mat^2)

# t(10): Z / sqrt(V/k)
k_t <- 10
z_t <- rnorm(n_sim)
z_mat_t <- matrix(rnorm(n_sim * k_t), nrow = n_sim, ncol = k_t)
v_t <- rowSums(z_mat_t^2)
t_sim <- z_t / sqrt(v_t / k_t)

# F(5, 10): (U/d1) / (V/d2)
d1 <- 5; d2 <- 10
z_u <- matrix(rnorm(n_sim * d1), nrow = n_sim, ncol = d1)
z_v <- matrix(rnorm(n_sim * d2), nrow = n_sim, ncol = d2)
u_sim <- rowSums(z_u^2)
v_sim <- rowSums(z_v^2)
f_sim <- (u_sim / d1) / (v_sim / d2)

df_sims <- data.table$rbindlist(list(
  data.table$data.table(x = chisq_sim, dist = "chi-square(5)"),

  data.table$data.table(x = f_sim, dist = "F(5, 10)")
```

## Communicating to Stakeholders

When discussing normal-family distributions with a non-technical audience:

- For the **normal**, say "bell-shaped curve" and use the 68-95-99.7 rule: "About 68% of values fall within one standard deviation of the mean, 95% within two, and 99.7% within three."
- For the **chi-square**, frame it in terms of its application: "This measures how far our observed counts deviate from what we expected."
- For the **$t$-distribution**, explain: "It's like the normal curve but with heavier tails -- it accounts for the extra uncertainty when we estimate the standard deviation from the data rather than knowing it in advance. With large samples, it becomes indistinguishable from the normal."
- For the **$F$-distribution**, use the ANOVA story: "It compares the variability *between* groups to the variability *within* groups. A large $F$ means the group differences are large relative to the noise."

## Quick Reference

| Property | $\chi^2_k$ | $t_k$ | $F_{d_1, d_2}$ |
|:---------|:-----------|:------|:----------------|
| **Definition** | $\sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2$ | $Z / \sqrt{V_k/k}$ | $(U/d_1)/(V/d_2)$ |

| **Connection to Gamma** | $\text{Gamma}(k/2, 1/2)$ | — | — |
| **Convergence** | — | $t_k \to N(0,1)$ as $k \to \infty$ | — |
| **R functions** | `dchisq`, `pchisq`, `rchisq` | `dt`, `pt`, `rt` | `df`, `pf`, `rf` |
