## 5.6 The Beta Distribution

### 5.6.1 Motivation and Definition

The Beta distribution lives on the interval $[0, 1]$, making it a natural model for **proportions**, **probabilities**, and **percentages**. It is astonishingly flexible: by varying two shape parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$, it can be uniform, U-shaped, J-shaped, left-skewed, right-skewed, or symmetric.

**Definition.** A random variable $X$ has a **Beta distribution** with parameters $\alpha > 0$ and $\beta > 0$ -- written $X \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ -- if its PDF is:

$$f(x; \alpha, \beta) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha, \beta)} x^{\alpha - 1}(1 - x)^{\beta - 1}, \quad 0 < x < 1,$$

where $B(\alpha, \beta)$ is the **Beta function**:

$$B(\alpha, \beta) = \int_0^1 t^{\alpha-1}(1-t)^{\beta-1}\,dt = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha + \beta)}.$$

The second equality -- connecting the Beta function to the Gamma function -- is a standard result proved by a double-integral argument.

### 5.6.2 Moments

**Mean:**

$$E[X] = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}.$$

**Variance:**

$$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha + \beta)^2(\alpha + \beta + 1)}.$$

**Intuition.** The mean is the "balance point" $\alpha / (\alpha + \beta)$ -- if $\alpha > \beta$, the distribution is shifted towards 1; if $\alpha < \beta$, towards 0. The variance shrinks as the total "concentration" $\alpha + \beta$ increases.

### 5.6.3 Special Cases

| Parameters | Shape | Distribution |
|:-----------|:------|:-------------|
| $\alpha = 1, \beta = 1$ | Flat | Uniform(0, 1) |
| $\alpha = \beta$ | Symmetric | Centred at 0.5 |
| $\alpha < 1, \beta < 1$ | U-shaped | Bimodal at boundaries |
| $\alpha > 1, \beta > 1$ | Unimodal | Bell-shaped on $[0, 1]$ |
| $\alpha = 1, \beta > 1$ | J-shaped (left) | Decreasing |
| $\alpha > 1, \beta = 1$ | J-shaped (right) | Increasing |

### 5.6.4 Connection to Order Statistics

The Beta distribution has a deep connection to order statistics. If $U_1, U_2, \ldots, U_n$ are independent $\text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ random variables and we sort them to get $U_{(1)} \le U_{(2)} \le \cdots \le U_{(n)}$, then:

$$U_{(k)} \sim \text{Beta}(k, n - k + 1).$$

This result explains why the Beta appears in nonparametric statistics and reliability theory.

### 5.6.5 Bayesian Preview

The Beta distribution is the **conjugate prior** for the Bernoulli and Binomial likelihoods. If our prior belief about a probability $p$ is $p \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$, and we observe $k$ successes in $n$ Bernoulli trials, then the posterior is:

$$p \mid \text{data} \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + k,\; \beta + n - k).$$

The parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ can be interpreted as "prior pseudo-counts" -- $\alpha$ prior successes and $\beta$ prior failures. This elegant updating rule is explored in depth in Chapter 22.

### 5.6.8 Bayesian Updating: A Quick Demonstration

## 5.7 The Log-Normal Distribution

### 5.7.1 Crash Course: Change of Variables (Jacobian Method)

When we transform a continuous random variable $X$ with known PDF $f_X(x)$ via a monotone function $Y = g(X)$, the PDF of $Y$ is:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X\!\left(g^{-1}(y)\right) \cdot \left|\frac{d}{dy} g^{-1}(y)\right|.$$

The absolute value of the derivative of the inverse transformation is the **Jacobian**. It accounts for the stretching or compressing of the probability scale under the transformation.

**Why the Jacobian?** Probabilities must be preserved:

$$P(a < X < b) = P(g(a) < Y < g(b)).$$

In terms of densities: $f_X(x)\,dx = f_Y(y)\,dy$, so $f_Y(y) = f_X(x) \cdot |dx/dy|$.

### 5.7.2 Deriving the Log-Normal PDF

**Definition.** If $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $Y = e^X$ has a **log-normal distribution**, written $Y \sim \text{LogNormal}(\mu, \sigma^2)$.

Equivalently: $Y$ is log-normal if $\ln(Y) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$.

**Applying the Jacobian method.** The transformation is $Y = g(X) = e^X$, so $X = g^{-1}(Y) = \ln(Y)$ and:

$$\frac{dX}{dY} = \frac{d}{dY}\ln(Y) = \frac{1}{Y}.$$

The PDF of $X$ is the normal density $f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(x - \mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}$. Substituting $x = \ln(y)$:

$$\boxed{f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{y\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(\ln y - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \quad y > 0.}$$

### 5.7.3 Log-Normal Moments

$$E[Y] = e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}, \qquad \text{Var}(Y) = \left(e^{\sigma^2} - 1\right)e^{2\mu + \sigma^2}.$$

These follow from the normal MGF: $E[Y] = E[e^X] = M_X(1) = e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}$.

Note that $E[Y] \ne e^\mu$ -- a common error. The "extra" factor $e^{\sigma^2/2}$ is Jensen's inequality in action: for a convex function ($e^x$), $E[g(X)] > g(E[X])$.

### 5.7.4 Why Log-Normal?

Many real-world quantities are log-normal:

- **Incomes and wealth** -- multiplicative growth processes produce log-normal distributions.
- **Stock prices** -- the Black-Scholes model assumes log-normal prices.
- **Particle sizes**, **city populations**, **biological measurements** that cannot be negative and are right-skewed.

The mechanism is multiplicative: if a quantity grows by a sequence of random percentage changes, the *logarithm* of the quantity is a sum of independent terms, which by the CLT tends to normal. Hence the quantity itself tends to log-normal.

## 5.8 The Distribution Family Tree

### 5.8.1 Relationships Summary

Every distribution we have studied is connected to the others through limiting cases, transformations, or sums. The diagram below traces the major relationships.

**Transformations and special cases:**

| From | To | Relationship |
|:-----|:---|:-------------|
| Uniform(0, 1) | Exp($\lambda$) | Inverse transform: $X = -\ln(U)/\lambda$ |
| Exp($\lambda$) | Gamma($\alpha, \lambda$) | Sum of $\alpha$ independent copies |
| Gamma($k/2, 1/2$) | $\chi^2_k$ | Definition |
| Gamma($1, \lambda$) | Exp($\lambda$) | Special case $\alpha = 1$ |
| Beta($1, 1$) | Uniform(0, 1) | Special case |
| $N(0,1)$ squared | $\chi^2_1$ | $Z^2 \sim \chi^2_1$ |
| $\chi^2_k$ (sum) | $\chi^2_{k_1 + k_2}$ | Sum of independent chi-squares |
| $N(0,1) / \sqrt{\chi^2_k/k}$ | $t_k$ | Definition |
| $\chi^2_{d_1}/d_1 \div \chi^2_{d_2}/d_2$ | $F_{d_1, d_2}$ | Definition |
| $t_k^2$ | $F_{1,k}$ | Squaring a $t$ |
| $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ exponentiated | LogNormal($\mu, \sigma^2$) | $Y = e^X$ |
| Binomial$(n, \lambda/n)$, $n \to \infty$ | Poisson($\lambda$) | Limit |
| Geometric($p$) | Exp($\lambda$) | Continuous analogue |

**Limit theorems:**

| From | To | Condition |
|:-----|:---|:----------|
| Binomial($n, p$) | $N(np, np(1-p))$ | $n \to \infty$ (CLT) |
| Poisson($\lambda$) | $N(\lambda, \lambda)$ | $\lambda \to \infty$ (CLT) |
| Gamma($\alpha, \lambda$) | $N(\alpha/\lambda, \alpha/\lambda^2)$ | $\alpha \to \infty$ (CLT) |
| $t_k$ | $N(0, 1)$ | $k \to \infty$ |
| $\chi^2_k / k$ | 1 | $k \to \infty$ (LLN) |

### 5.8.2 Comparison Table: All Continuous Distributions

| Distribution | Support | PDF | E[X] | Var(X) | Key use |
|:-------------|:--------|:----|:-----|:-------|:--------|
| Uniform($a,b$) | $[a,b]$ | $1/(b-a)$ | $(a+b)/2$ | $(b-a)^2/12$ | Simulation engine |
| Exp($\lambda$) | $[0, \infty)$ | $\lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ | $1/\lambda$ | $1/\lambda^2$ | Waiting times |
| Gamma($\alpha, \lambda$) | $(0, \infty)$ | $\frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)}x^{\alpha-1}e^{-\lambda x}$ | $\alpha/\lambda$ | $\alpha/\lambda^2$ | Sum of waiting times |
| Normal($\mu, \sigma^2$) | $(-\infty, \infty)$ | $\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}$ | $\mu$ | $\sigma^2$ | CLT, measurement error |
| $\chi^2_k$ | $(0, \infty)$ | Gamma$(k/2, 1/2)$ | $k$ | $2k$ | Goodness of fit, variance |
| $t_k$ | $(-\infty, \infty)$ | See Part 2 | 0 | $k/(k-2)$ | Small-sample means |
| $F_{d_1,d_2}$ | $(0, \infty)$ | See Part 2 | $d_2/(d_2-2)$ | — | ANOVA, variance ratios |
| Beta($\alpha, \beta$) | $(0, 1)$ | $\frac{x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha,\beta)}$ | $\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}$ | $\frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)}$ | Proportions, Bayesian |
| LogNormal($\mu, \sigma^2$) | $(0, \infty)$ | $\frac{1}{y\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(\ln y - \mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}$ | $e^{\mu+\sigma^2/2}$ | $(e^{\sigma^2}-1)e^{2\mu+\sigma^2}$ | Multiplicative processes |

## Communicating to Stakeholders

When presenting these distributions to a non-technical audience:

- For the **Beta**, say "a flexible model for proportions between 0 and 1." Use the Bayesian story: "We started believing the probability was around 50-50. After seeing 7 out of 10 successes, we updated our belief to around 64%."
- For the **log-normal**, explain: "When data can only be positive and is right-skewed, taking the logarithm often makes it look normal. This is common for incomes, prices, and environmental concentrations."
- For the **change of variables**, you rarely need to explain the Jacobian itself. Instead, say: "Transforming data (like taking the log) changes the shape of the distribution in a predictable way."
- The **family tree** is a powerful visual. Show it to emphasise that these distributions are not isolated facts but a connected system.

## Quick Reference

| Property | Beta($\alpha, \beta$) | LogNormal($\mu, \sigma^2$) |
|:---------|:---------------------|:--------------------------|
| **Support** | $(0, 1)$ | $(0, \infty)$ |
| **PDF** | $\frac{x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha,\beta)}$ | $\frac{1}{y\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(\ln y - \mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}$ |
| **E[X]** | $\frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}$ | $e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}$ |
| **Var(X)** | $\frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)}$ | $(e^{\sigma^2} - 1)e^{2\mu + \sigma^2}$ |
| **Key property** | Conjugate prior for Binomial | $\ln(Y) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ |
| **R functions** | `dbeta`, `pbeta`, `rbeta` | `dlnorm`, `plnorm`, `rlnorm` |

**Key transformation rules:**

| Transformation | If $X$ has distribution... | Then $Y = g(X)$ has... |
|:---------------|:--------------------------|:----------------------|
| $Y = aX + b$ | $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ | $N(a\mu + b,\; a^2\sigma^2)$ |
| $Y = e^X$ | $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ | LogNormal($\mu, \sigma^2$) |
| $Y = X^2$ | $N(0, 1)$ | $\chi^2_1$ |
| $Y = -\ln(X)/\lambda$ | Uniform(0, 1) | Exp($\lambda$) |
| $Y = F^{-1}(X)$ | Uniform(0, 1) | CDF $= F$ |

### Spot-the-Error Exercises

**Exercise 5.31.** A student writes: "If $Y \sim \text{LogNormal}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $E[Y] = e^\mu$."

*What is the error?* *(The correct formula is $E[Y] = e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}$. The quantity $e^\mu$ is the median, not the mean. Jensen's inequality guarantees $E[e^X] > e^{E[X]}$ for any non-degenerate $X$.)*

**Exercise 5.32.** A colleague says: "The Beta distribution is useless because it's defined on $[0, 1]$. Real data is rarely on $[0, 1]$."

*What is the flaw?* *(Many quantities of interest are naturally on $[0, 1]$: probabilities, proportions, percentages (after dividing by 100), and rates. Moreover, any bounded quantity $X \in [a, b]$ can be linearly transformed to $[0, 1]$ via $(X - a)/(b - a)$.)*
