# Chapter 4 -- Discrete Distributions

## Part 3: The Poisson Distribution and Relationships

The Poisson distribution is one of the most important in all of statistics. It arises as the natural model for counting **rare events** in a fixed interval of time, space, or some other continuum. In this part we derive it as a *limit* of the binomial distribution -- a derivation that is both beautiful and illuminating -- and then survey the rich web of relationships connecting all the discrete distributions we have studied.

---

## 4.6 The Poisson Distribution

### 4.6.1 The Setup

Imagine counting events that occur randomly and independently in a fixed interval:

- The number of emails arriving in your inbox in one hour.
- The number of radioactive decays detected by a Geiger counter in one second.
- The number of typographical errors on a printed page.
- The number of car accidents at an intersection in one week.

These situations share a common structure:

1. Events occur **independently** of one another.
2. The average rate of events, $\lambda$, is constant.
3. Two events cannot occur at exactly the same instant.
4. The probability of an event in a tiny interval $\Delta t$ is approximately $\lambda \Delta t$.

Under these conditions, the number of events $X$ in the interval follows a **Poisson distribution** with parameter $\lambda > 0$, written $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$.

But where does the Poisson PMF come from? We shall derive it from first principles by taking a limit of the binomial.

### 4.6.2 Deriving the Poisson as a Limit of the Binomial

This is a highlight of the chapter. Read slowly and savour each step.

**The idea.** Divide the interval into $n$ tiny sub-intervals. If $n$ is large enough, each sub-interval contains at most one event. The probability of an event in any one sub-interval is $p = \lambda/n$ (so that the expected number of events across all $n$ sub-intervals is $np = \lambda$). Each sub-interval is a Bernoulli trial. The total number of events is therefore $\text{Bin}(n, \lambda/n)$.

Now let $n \to \infty$ (make the sub-intervals infinitesimally small) while keeping $\lambda = np$ fixed.

**The derivation.** Start with the binomial PMF:

$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} \left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^k \left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-k}.$$

We take the limit as $n \to \infty$ term by term.

**Step 1: The binomial coefficient.** Write:

$$\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} = \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-k+1)}{k!}.$$

This is a product of $k$ terms in the numerator:

$$\frac{n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-k+1)}{n^k} = \frac{n}{n} \cdot \frac{n-1}{n} \cdot \frac{n-2}{n} \cdots \frac{n-k+1}{n}.$$

As $n \to \infty$ with $k$ fixed, each factor $\frac{n-j}{n} = 1 - \frac{j}{n} \to 1$. Therefore:

$$\frac{\binom{n}{k}}{n^k} \to \frac{1}{k!}.$$

Equivalently, $\binom{n}{k} \left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^k = \frac{\binom{n}{k}}{n^k} \cdot \lambda^k \to \frac{\lambda^k}{k!}$.

**Step 2: The main exponential term.** We need:

$$\left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^n \to e^{-\lambda} \quad \text{as } n \to \infty.$$

This is one of the most fundamental limits in mathematics. One way to see it: take the logarithm:

$$n \ln\left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n}\right) = n \left(-\frac{\lambda}{n} - \frac{\lambda^2}{2n^2} - \cdots\right) = -\lambda - \frac{\lambda^2}{2n} - \cdots \to -\lambda.$$

Exponentiating: $\left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^n \to e^{-\lambda}$.

**Step 3: The remaining factor.** We also have:

$$\left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{-k} \to 1 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty.$$

This is because $k$ is fixed and $\left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n}\right) \to 1$.

**Putting it together.** Combining Steps 1--3:

$$P(X = k) = \underbrace{\binom{n}{k}\left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^k}_{\to\;\lambda^k/k!} \cdot \underbrace{\left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n}}_{\to\;e^{-\lambda}} \cdot \underbrace{\left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{-k}}_{\to\;1} \;\;\to\;\; \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}.$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{P(X = k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots}$$

This is the **Poisson PMF**. It has a single parameter $\lambda > 0$, which represents the average rate of events.

### 4.6.3 Verifying the PMF Sums to 1

We need $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} = 1$. Factor out $e^{-\lambda}$:

$$e^{-\lambda} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{\lambda^k}{k!} = e^{-\lambda} \cdot e^{\lambda} = 1. \quad \checkmark$$

We used the Taylor expansion $e^{\lambda} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{\lambda^k}{k!}$.

### 4.6.4 Deriving E[X] -- Direct Method

$$E[X] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k \cdot \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}.$$

The $k = 0$ term vanishes. For $k \ge 1$, note that $k/k! = 1/(k-1)!$:

$$E[X] = e^{-\lambda} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{\lambda^k}{(k-1)!} = e^{-\lambda} \cdot \lambda \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{\lambda^{k-1}}{(k-1)!}.$$

Let $j = k - 1$:

$$E[X] = \lambda e^{-\lambda} \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \frac{\lambda^j}{j!} = \lambda e^{-\lambda} \cdot e^{\lambda} = \lambda.$$

The expected number of events equals the rate parameter. This is intuitive and serves as a useful sanity check.

### 4.6.5 Deriving the MGF

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} e^{tk} \cdot \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} = e^{-\lambda} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(\lambda e^t)^k}{k!} = e^{-\lambda} \cdot e^{\lambda e^t} = e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}.$$

$$\boxed{M_X(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}.}$$

### 4.6.6 Deriving Var(X) via the MGF

**First derivative:**

$$M_X'(t) = \lambda e^t \cdot e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}.$$

At $t = 0$: $E[X] = M_X'(0) = \lambda \cdot 1 = \lambda$. $\checkmark$

**Second derivative** (product rule):

$$M_X''(t) = (\lambda e^t)^2 e^{\lambda(e^t-1)} + \lambda e^t \cdot e^{\lambda(e^t-1)} = e^{\lambda(e^t-1)}\left[\lambda^2 e^{2t} + \lambda e^t\right].$$

At $t = 0$:

$$E[X^2] = M_X''(0) = \lambda^2 + \lambda.$$

Therefore:

$$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = \lambda^2 + \lambda - \lambda^2 = \lambda.$$

This is one of the most distinctive features of the Poisson distribution: **the mean equals the variance.** If you observe count data where the sample mean is approximately equal to the sample variance, a Poisson model is a natural candidate. (When the variance exceeds the mean -- *overdispersion* -- the negative binomial is often a better choice; we return to this in the count models chapter.)

### 4.6.7 Implementation from Scratch


``` r
#' Poisson PMF -- implemented from scratch
#' @param k integer vector of non-negative values
#' @param lambda rate parameter (> 0)
#' @return vector of probabilities
my_dpois <- function(k, lambda) {
  stopifnot(all(k >= 0), lambda > 0)
  (lambda^k * exp(-lambda)) / factorial(k)
}

# Verify against R's built-in
k_vals <- 0:20
lambda <- 5

ours <- my_dpois(k_vals, lambda)
theirs <- dpois(k_vals, lambda)

cat("Maximum absolute difference:", max(abs(ours - theirs)), "\n")
```

### 4.6.8 The Binomial-to-Poisson Convergence: Numerical Demonstration


``` r
box::use(data.table[data.table, rbindlist])

lambda <- 3
k_vals <- 0:10

results <- rbindlist(lapply(c(10, 30, 100, 500, 2000), function(nn) {
  pp <- lambda / nn
  data.table(
    k = k_vals,
    prob = dbinom(k_vals, nn, pp),
    n = nn,
    label = sprintf("Bin(%d, %.4f)", nn, pp)
  )
}))

# Add the Poisson limit
pois <- data.table(
  k = k_vals,
  prob = dpois(k_vals, lambda),
  n = Inf,
  label = sprintf("Pois(%.0f)", lambda)
)

results <- rbind(results, pois)

cat("Binomial(n, lambda/n) converging to Poisson(lambda) for lambda =", lambda, "\n\n")
cat(sprintf("%-5s", "k"))
for (lab in unique(results$label)) cat(sprintf("%16s", lab))
cat("\n")

for (kk in k_vals) {
  cat(sprintf("%-5d", kk))
  row <- results[k == kk]
  for (i in seq_len(nrow(row))) {
    cat(sprintf("%16.6f", row$prob[i]))
  }
  cat("\n")
}
```

## 4.7 Relationships Between Discrete Distributions

### 4.7.1 The Family Tree

The discrete distributions we have studied are not isolated -- they form an interconnected family. Understanding these connections deepens our understanding of each individual distribution.

**Key relationships:**

1. **Bernoulli $\to$ Binomial.** A Binomial($n, p$) random variable is the sum of $n$ independent Bernoulli($p$) random variables. Equivalently, Bernoulli($p$) = Binomial(1, $p$).

2. **Geometric = Negative Binomial with $r = 1$.** The geometric distribution is a special case of the negative binomial.

3. **Binomial $\to$ Poisson.** As $n \to \infty$ and $p \to 0$ with $np = \lambda$ held constant, Binomial($n, p$) converges to Poisson($\lambda$). The Poisson is the "rare events" limit of the binomial.

4. **Hypergeometric $\approx$ Binomial.** When the population size $N$ is much larger than the sample size $n$, the hypergeometric distribution is well-approximated by the binomial with $p = K/N$.

5. **Sum of Poissons.** If $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda_1)$ and $Y \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda_2)$ are independent, then $X + Y \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$. This follows from the MGFs: $e^{\lambda_1(e^t-1)} \cdot e^{\lambda_2(e^t-1)} = e^{(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)(e^t-1)}$.

### 4.7.2 Summary Comparison Table

| Distribution | Support | PMF | $E[X]$ | $\text{Var}(X)$ | MGF |
|:-------------|:--------|:----|:-------|:----------------|:----|
| Bernoulli($p$) | $\{0,1\}$ | $p^x(1-p)^{1-x}$ | $p$ | $p(1-p)$ | $(1-p)+pe^t$ |
| Binomial($n,p$) | $\{0,\ldots,n\}$ | $\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$ | $np$ | $np(1-p)$ | $[(1-p)+pe^t]^n$ |
| Geometric($p$) | $\{1,2,\ldots\}$ | $(1-p)^{k-1}p$ | $1/p$ | $(1-p)/p^2$ | $\frac{pe^t}{1-(1-p)e^t}$ |
| NegBin($r,p$) | $\{r,r+1,\ldots\}$ | $\binom{k-1}{r-1}p^r(1-p)^{k-r}$ | $r/p$ | $r(1-p)/p^2$ | $\left(\frac{pe^t}{1-(1-p)e^t}\right)^r$ |
| Hypergeometric($N,K,n$) | see text | $\frac{\binom{K}{k}\binom{N-K}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}}$ | $nK/N$ | $n\frac{K}{N}\frac{N-K}{N}\frac{N-n}{N-1}$ | (no simple form) |
| Poisson($\lambda$) | $\{0,1,2,\ldots\}$ | $\frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}$ | $\lambda$ | $\lambda$ | $e^{\lambda(e^t-1)}$ |

### 4.7.4 A Utility Function: Discrete Distribution Calculator

Let us build a utility function that, given a distribution name and parameters, returns a `data.table` with the PMF and CDF values.


``` r
box::use(data.table[data.table])

#' Compute PMF and CDF for a named discrete distribution
#' @param dist character: one of "bernoulli", "binomial", "geometric",
#'   "negbin", "hypergeometric", "poisson"
#' @param params named list of parameters
#' @param k_range optional integer vector of k values (auto-determined if NULL)
#' @return data.table with columns: k, pmf, cdf
discrete_dist <- function(dist, params, k_range = NULL) {
  dist <- tolower(dist)

  if (is.null(k_range)) {
    k_range <- switch(dist,
      bernoulli = 0:1,
      binomial = 0:params$n,
      geometric = 1:(ceiling(5 / params$p)),
      negbin = params$r:(params$r + ceiling(5 * params$r / params$p)),
      hypergeometric = {
        lo <- max(0, params$n - (params$N - params$K))
        hi <- min(params$n, params$K)
        lo:hi
      },
      poisson = 0:qpois(0.999, params$lambda),
      stop("Unknown distribution: ", dist)
    )
  }

  pmf <- switch(dist,
    bernoulli = dbinom(k_range, 1, params$p),
    binomial = dbinom(k_range, params$n, params$p),
    geometric = dgeom(k_range - 1, params$p),
    negbin = dnbinom(k_range - params$r, size = params$r, prob = params$p),
    hypergeometric = dhyper(k_range, m = params$K, n = params$N - params$K, k = params$n),
    poisson = dpois(k_range, params$lambda)
  )

  data.table(
    k = k_range,
    pmf = pmf,
    cdf = cumsum(pmf)
  )
}

# Examples
cat("Binomial(10, 0.3):\n")
print(discrete_dist("binomial", list(n = 10, p = 0.3)))

cat("\nPoisson(4):\n")
print(discrete_dist("poisson", list(lambda = 4)))
```

## Communicating to Stakeholders

When presenting Poisson models to a non-technical audience:

- Say "an average of 5 events per hour" rather than "$\lambda = 5$."
- Use the Poisson's "mean = variance" property as a diagnostic: "If the spread of our data is much larger than the average, the simple Poisson model doesn't capture the full picture, and we should use a more flexible model."
- For the binomial-to-Poisson approximation, explain: "When we have very many trials but each has a tiny probability, the total count of successes follows a Poisson pattern."
- Visualisations are essential. A bar chart comparing observed frequencies to the Poisson model immediately reveals whether the model fits or whether there is overdispersion (excess variability).

## Quick Reference

| Property | Poisson($\lambda$) |
|:---------|:-------------------|
| **Setup** | Count of rare events in a fixed interval |
| **Support** | $\{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ |
| **PMF** | $\frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}$ |
| **E[X]** | $\lambda$ |
| **Var(X)** | $\lambda$ (mean = variance) |
| **MGF** | $e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$ |
| **R functions** | `dpois`, `ppois`, `qpois`, `rpois` |
| **Key property** | Limit of Bin($n, \lambda/n$) as $n \to \infty$ |
| **Overdispersion signal** | $\text{Var}(X) > E[X]$ $\Rightarrow$ consider negative binomial |

**Key relationships across all distributions:**

| From | To | Relationship |
|:-----|:---|:-------------|
| Bernoulli($p$) | Binomial($n, p$) | Sum of $n$ independent copies |
| Geometric($p$) | NegBin($r, p$) | Special case $r = 1$ |
| Binomial($n, \lambda/n$) | Poisson($\lambda$) | Limit as $n \to \infty$ |
| Hypergeometric($N, K, n$) | Binomial($n, K/N$) | Approximation when $N \gg n$ |
| Poisson($\lambda_1$) + Poisson($\lambda_2$) | Poisson($\lambda_1 + \lambda_2$) | Sum of independent Poissons |
