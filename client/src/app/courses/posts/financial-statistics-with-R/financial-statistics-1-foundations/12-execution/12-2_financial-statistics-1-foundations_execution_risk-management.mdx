## 1. Prose: Risk Architecture and Position Management

> "Risk management is not about eliminating risk—it's about ensuring that the risks you take are the ones you intended to take, sized appropriately for your edge."

Moving from execution to risk management, we shift focus from *how* to enter positions to *how much* to risk and *when* to exit. This distinction is fundamental: execution quality determines transaction costs, but risk management determines survival.

### The Risk Hierarchy

Professional risk management operates on multiple levels:

**Position Level**: Individual trade sizing based on conviction, volatility, and portfolio context. A high-conviction trade in a low-volatility asset might warrant larger sizing than a speculative position in a volatile name.

**Strategy Level**: Aggregate exposure to a particular alpha source. Even if each position is appropriately sized, excessive concentration in one strategy creates hidden correlation risk.

**Portfolio Level**: Total capital at risk, considering cross-strategy correlation and tail dependencies. Strategies that appear uncorrelated in normal markets may become highly correlated during stress.

**Enterprise Level**: Firm-wide risk aggregation across all portfolios, accounting for operational, liquidity, and counterparty risks beyond market risk.

### Why Position Limits Matter

Consider a simple thought experiment: You have a strategy with 60% win rate and 1:1 risk-reward. Kelly criterion suggests betting 20% of capital per trade. But Kelly assumes:

1. Accurate probability estimates
2. No correlation between trades
3. Infinite time horizon
4. No psychological constraints

In practice, all four assumptions fail. Position limits provide the guardrails that allow a strategy to survive the inevitable periods when these assumptions break down.

## 3. Mathematical Derivation: Risk Metrics and Position Sizing

### Value at Risk (VaR)

VaR answers: "What is the maximum loss over a given time horizon at a given confidence level?"

For a portfolio with value $V$ and return distribution $R$:

$$\text{VaR}_\alpha = -V \cdot \inf\{r : P(R \leq r) \geq 1 - \alpha\}$$

Under normality assumption (parametric VaR):

$$\text{VaR}_\alpha = V \cdot (\mu \cdot t - z_\alpha \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{t})$$

Where:
- $\mu$ = expected return
- $\sigma$ = volatility
- $t$ = time horizon
- $z_\alpha$ = quantile of standard normal (1.645 for 95%, 2.326 for 99%)

**Limitations of VaR**:
1. Says nothing about magnitude of tail losses
2. Not subadditive (portfolio VaR can exceed sum of component VaRs)
3. Assumes stable distributions

### Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR / Expected Shortfall)

CVaR addresses VaR's first limitation:

$$\text{CVaR}_\alpha = -E[R | R \leq -\text{VaR}_\alpha] = -\frac{1}{1-\alpha}\int_0^{1-\alpha} \text{VaR}_u \, du$$

CVaR is:
1. **Coherent**: Satisfies subadditivity (diversification reduces risk)
2. **Tail-sensitive**: Captures severity of extreme losses
3. **Convex**: Enables optimization

### Kelly Criterion and Fractional Kelly

The Kelly criterion maximizes long-term growth rate:

$$f^* = \frac{p \cdot b - q}{b} = \frac{\text{Edge}}{\text{Odds}}$$

Where:
- $p$ = probability of win
- $q = 1-p$ = probability of loss
- $b$ = win/loss ratio

For continuous returns with expected excess return $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$:

$$f^* = \frac{\mu}{\sigma^2}$$

**Fractional Kelly** uses $f = \lambda \cdot f^*$ where $\lambda \in (0.25, 0.5)$ typically:

| Fraction | Growth Rate | Drawdown | Recovery Time |
|----------|-------------|----------|---------------|
| Full Kelly | Maximum | Severe | Long |
| Half Kelly | 75% of max | Moderate | Medium |
| Quarter Kelly | 44% of max | Mild | Short |

### Position Sizing with Volatility Targeting

Volatility targeting adjusts position size inversely to volatility:

$$w_t = \frac{\sigma_{\text{target}}}{\sigma_t} \cdot w_{\text{base}}$$

Where $\sigma_t$ is estimated from recent returns. This creates constant risk per trade despite varying market conditions.

**Variance Drain**: High volatility strategies suffer from the arithmetic-geometric mean inequality:

$$\text{Geometric Mean} \approx \mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2}$$

A strategy with 20% annual return and 40% volatility has expected growth of only 12%.

``` r
# Comprehensive risk management implementation

#' Calculate multiple risk metrics
#' @param returns numeric vector of returns
#' @param confidence confidence level for VaR/CVaR
#' @return data.table of risk metrics
calculate_risk_metrics <- function(returns, confidence = 0.95) {
    returns <- na.omit(returns)
    n <- length(returns)

    # Basic statistics
    mu <- mean(returns)
    sigma <- sd(returns)
    skew <- mean(((returns - mu) / sigma)^3)
    kurt <- mean(((returns - mu) / sigma)^4) - 3  # Excess kurtosis

    # VaR calculations
    alpha <- 1 - confidence

    # Historical VaR
    var_hist <- -quantile(returns, alpha)

    # Parametric VaR (assumes normality)
    var_param <- -(mu + qnorm(alpha) * sigma)

    # Cornish-Fisher VaR (adjusts for skew and kurtosis)
    z <- qnorm(alpha)
    cf_adjustment <- z + (z^2 - 1) * skew / 6 +
                     (z^3 - 3*z) * kurt / 24 -
                     (2*z^3 - 5*z) * skew^2 / 36
    var_cf <- -(mu + cf_adjustment * sigma)

    # CVaR (Expected Shortfall)
    var_threshold <- quantile(returns, alpha)
    cvar <- -mean(returns[returns <= var_threshold])

    # Drawdown analysis
    cum_returns <- cumprod(1 + returns)
    running_max <- cummax(cum_returns)
    drawdowns <- (cum_returns - running_max) / running_max
    max_dd <- min(drawdowns)

    # Calmar ratio (return / max drawdown)
    ann_return <- prod(1 + returns)^(252/n) - 1
    calmar <- ann_return / abs(max_dd)

    data.table(
        n_obs = n,
        mean_daily = mu,
        volatility_daily = sigma,
        volatility_annual = sigma * sqrt(252),
        skewness = skew,
        excess_kurtosis = kurt,
        var_historical = var_hist,
        var_parametric = var_param,
        var_cornish_fisher = var_cf,
        cvar = cvar,
        cvar_var_ratio = cvar / var_hist,
        max_drawdown = max_dd,
        calmar_ratio = calmar
    )
}

print(risk_metrics)
```

``` r
#' Kelly-based position sizing with constraints
#' @param expected_return expected return of trade
#' @param volatility volatility estimate
#' @param kelly_fraction fraction of Kelly to use
#' @param max_position maximum position size
#' @return optimal position size
kelly_position_size <- function(
    expected_return,
    volatility,
    kelly_fraction = 0.25,
    max_position = 0.20
) {
    # Full Kelly
    full_kelly <- expected_return / (volatility^2)

    # Apply fraction
    fractional_kelly <- full_kelly * kelly_fraction

    # Constrain
    position <- pmin(pmax(fractional_kelly, -max_position), max_position)

    data.table(
        expected_return = expected_return,
        volatility = volatility,
        full_kelly = full_kelly,
        fractional_kelly = fractional_kelly,
        constrained_position = position
    )
}

# Example: position sizing across different opportunity sets

)

sizing_results <- opportunities[, kelly_position_size(
    expected_return, volatility
), by = 1:nrow(opportunities)]
```

``` r
#' Volatility targeting position adjustment
#' @param base_weight base position weight
#' @param current_vol current volatility estimate
#' @param target_vol target volatility
#' @param max_leverage maximum leverage allowed
#' @param min_weight minimum weight (can be 0 for de-risking)
#' @return adjusted position weight
vol_target_adjustment <- function(
    base_weight,
    current_vol,
    target_vol = 0.10,
    max_leverage = 1.5,
    min_weight = 0.1
) {
    # Calculate adjustment ratio
    vol_ratio <- target_vol / current_vol

    # Apply to base weight
    adjusted <- base_weight * vol_ratio

    # Constrain
    constrained <- pmin(pmax(adjusted, min_weight), max_leverage)

    data.table(
        base_weight = base_weight,
        current_vol = current_vol,
        target_vol = target_vol,
        vol_ratio = vol_ratio,
        adjusted_weight = adjusted,
        final_weight = constrained
    )
}
```

``` r
#' Drawdown-based position scaling
#' @param current_dd current drawdown (negative number)
#' @param dd_threshold_1 first threshold for scaling
#' @param dd_threshold_2 second threshold for further scaling
#' @param dd_max maximum tolerable drawdown (triggers stop)
#' @return position multiplier (0 to 1)
drawdown_position_scale <- function(
    current_dd,
    dd_threshold_1 = -0.05,
    dd_threshold_2 = -0.10,
    dd_max = -0.20
) {
    scale <- ifelse(
        current_dd >= dd_threshold_1, 1.0,
        ifelse(
            current_dd >= dd_threshold_2, 0.75,
            ifelse(
                current_dd >= dd_max, 0.50,
                0.0  # Stop trading
            )
        )
    )

    data.table(
        current_dd = current_dd,
        position_scale = scale,
        status = ifelse(scale == 0, "STOPPED",
                       ifelse(scale < 1, "REDUCED", "NORMAL"))
    )
}
```

``` r
#' Comprehensive position sizing with multiple risk constraints
#' @param signal trading signal strength (-1 to 1)
#' @param expected_return expected return estimate
#' @param volatility current volatility estimate
#' @param current_dd current portfolio drawdown
#' @param correlation correlation to existing portfolio
#' @return final position size with all adjustments
comprehensive_position_size <- function(
    signal,
    expected_return,
    volatility,
    current_dd = 0,
    correlation = 0,
    # Risk parameters
    kelly_fraction = 0.25,
    target_vol = 0.15,
    max_position = 0.10,
    max_correlation = 0.7,
    dd_threshold = -0.10
) {
    # Start with signal-based direction
    direction <- sign(signal)
    signal_strength <- abs(signal)

    # Kelly sizing
    if (expected_return * direction > 0) {
        kelly_size <- abs(expected_return) / (volatility^2) * kelly_fraction
    } else {
        kelly_size <- 0  # Don't trade against our expected return
    }

    # Vol targeting adjustment
    vol_adjustment <- target_vol / volatility
    vol_adjusted_size <- kelly_size * vol_adjustment

    # Signal strength scaling
    signal_scaled <- vol_adjusted_size * signal_strength

    # Drawdown adjustment
    dd_scale <- ifelse(current_dd >= -0.05, 1.0,
                       ifelse(current_dd >= dd_threshold, 0.5, 0.25))
    dd_adjusted <- signal_scaled * dd_scale

    # Correlation penalty (reduce size for correlated positions)
    corr_penalty <- 1 - abs(correlation) * 0.5
    corr_adjusted <- dd_adjusted * corr_penalty

    # Final constraint
    final_size <- pmin(pmax(corr_adjusted, 0), max_position) * direction

    data.table(
        signal = signal,
        direction = direction,
        kelly_raw = kelly_size,
        vol_adjusted = vol_adjusted_size,
        signal_scaled = signal_scaled,
        dd_adjusted = dd_adjusted,
        corr_adjusted = corr_adjusted,
        final_position = final_size,
        rejection_reason = ifelse(
            expected_return * direction <= 0, "SIGNAL_CONFLICT",
            ifelse(abs(correlation) > max_correlation, "HIGH_CORRELATION",
                   ifelse(current_dd < dd_threshold, "DRAWDOWN_LIMIT", "ACCEPTED"))
        )
    )
}

# Test across various scenarios
```

## Summary

Risk management transforms a trading strategy from a gamble into a sustainable business:

**Key Metrics**:
- VaR tells you daily risk at a confidence level, but misses tail severity
- CVaR captures expected loss in the tail—use this for position limits
- Drawdown is the lived experience of risk—the metric clients care about
- Calmar ratio (return/drawdown) measures pain-adjusted performance

**Position Sizing Principles**:
1. Start with Kelly (edge/variance) for theoretical optimal
2. Apply fractional Kelly (25-50%) for real-world robustness
3. Adjust for volatility to maintain consistent risk
4. Scale down during drawdowns to preserve capital
5. Penalize correlated positions to avoid concentration

**Risk Hierarchy**:
- Position risk < Strategy risk < Portfolio risk < Enterprise risk
- Each level needs its own limits and monitoring
- Correlations matter more as you aggregate up

The goal is not to eliminate risk but to ensure every unit of risk taken earns adequate return. In the next section, we'll see how these risk management tools integrate into complete portfolio construction.
