## 10.1 Volatility as an Asset Class

### 10.1.1 Prose & Intuition

Volatility is not just a risk measure—it's a tradeable asset class with its own unique characteristics. Unlike traditional assets that derive value from cash flows or ownership claims, volatility represents uncertainty itself. This distinction creates fascinating properties that sophisticated traders exploit.

The key insight is that **implied volatility is almost always higher than realised volatility**. This gap—the Volatility Risk Premium (VRP)—exists because investors are willing to pay extra for protection against uncertainty. Just as insurance companies profit from the gap between premiums charged and claims paid, volatility sellers harvest this premium.

However, volatility trading carries significant tail risk. When markets crash, volatility explodes, and positions that appeared profitable can face catastrophic losses. The asymmetry between slow premium collection and sudden, violent drawdowns defines the volatility trader's challenge.

### 10.1.3 Mathematical Derivation

**Implied vs Realised Volatility**

Implied volatility ($\sigma_{IV}$) is extracted from option prices using the Black-Scholes formula. It represents the market's expectation of future volatility.

Realised volatility ($\sigma_{RV}$) is computed from historical returns:

$$
\sigma_{RV} = \sqrt{\frac{252}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} r_i^2}
$$

assuming zero mean (which is approximately true for daily returns).

**Volatility Risk Premium**

$$
VRP = \sigma_{IV} - E[\sigma_{RV}]
$$

Empirically, $VRP > 0$ on average, meaning implied volatility consistently overstates realised volatility.

**VIX Calculation (Simplified)**

The VIX approximates the 30-day expected variance:

$$
VIX^2 = \frac{2}{T} \sum_i \frac{\Delta K_i}{K_i^2} e^{rT} Q(K_i)
$$

where $K_i$ are strike prices, $Q(K_i)$ are OTM option prices, and $T$ is time to expiration.

This model-free approach aggregates information from all available strikes.

## 10.2 VIX Mechanics and Term Structure

### 10.2.1 Prose & Intuition

The VIX isn't directly tradeable—it's a calculated index. To gain volatility exposure, traders use VIX futures, options, or ETPs. This creates a crucial distinction between spot VIX and tradeable instruments.

The VIX term structure—the relationship between futures prices at different expirations—reveals market expectations and creates trading opportunities. When the curve is in **contango** (upward sloping), longer-dated futures trade above spot VIX. This is the normal state, reflecting the time value embedded in volatility exposure.

When markets stress, the term structure inverts to **backwardation** (downward sloping). Spot VIX spikes above futures prices as near-term fear exceeds longer-term expectations. These inversions are rare but violent, and represent regime changes that volatility traders must navigate.

### 10.2.3 Mathematical Derivation

**Term Structure and Roll Yield**

For VIX futures, the relationship between spot and futures follows:

$$
F_T = VIX_t \cdot e^{(r - y)(T-t)}
$$

where $y$ is the convenience yield. In practice, VIX futures don't follow cost-of-carry because VIX isn't holdable.

**Contango Roll Cost**

When futures trade at premium to spot (contango), rolling positions costs:

$$
\text{Roll Cost} = \frac{F_2 - F_1}{F_1}
$$

This drag can be substantial—historically 4-6% monthly during contango.

**Term Structure Slope**

The slope measures term structure steepness:

$$
\text{Slope} = \frac{VIX_{\text{long}} - VIX_{\text{short}}}{VIX_{\text{short}}}
$$

High slope suggests elevated roll cost; inverted slope signals near-term stress.

### 10.2.4 Implementation & Application

``` r
# Average contango assumptions
monthly_roll_cost <- 0.05  # 5% monthly in contango

vix_start <- 15

spot_path <- numeric(n_months * days_in_month)
spot_path[1] <- vix_start
for (i in 2:length(spot_path)) {
    mean_revert <- 0.02 * (18 - spot_path[i-1])  # Pull toward 18
```

## 10.3 Volatility Mean Reversion

### 10.3.1 Prose & Intuition

Volatility mean-reverts more reliably than prices. This fundamental property makes volatility trading distinct from directional asset trading. When VIX spikes to 40, we're highly confident it will return to normal levels—we just don't know when or how.

The mean-reversion process follows different speeds depending on the direction. VIX rises fast (in sudden spikes) but falls slowly (grinding back to normal). This asymmetry creates strategic opportunities: selling volatility after spikes captures mean reversion, while the initial spike can devastate short positions.

Understanding the half-life of volatility shocks—how long they persist—is crucial for timing entries and sizing positions. Shorter half-lives suggest faster mean reversion and shorter holding periods.

### 10.3.3 Mathematical Derivation

**Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process for Volatility**

Volatility is often modelled as mean-reverting:

$$
d\sigma = \kappa(\theta - \sigma)dt + \eta dW
$$

where:
- $\kappa$ = speed of mean reversion
- $\theta$ = long-run mean volatility
- $\eta$ = volatility of volatility

**Half-Life Calculation**

For an AR(1) process $X_t = \phi X_{t-1} + \epsilon_t$:

$$
\text{Half-life} = \frac{-\ln(2)}{\ln(\phi)}
$$

For log VIX with $\phi \approx 0.97$:

$$
\text{Half-life} \approx \frac{0.693}{0.03} \approx 23 \text{ days}
$$

**Expected Future VIX**

Given current VIX, expected future value:

$$
E[VIX_{t+h}] = \theta + (VIX_t - \theta) \cdot e^{-\kappa h}
$$

High VIX today implies decay toward the long-run mean.

### 10.3.4 Implementation & Application


``` r
# Define mean reversion thresholds
vix_mean <- mean(combined$vix, na.rm = TRUE)
vix_sd <- sd(combined$vix, na.rm = TRUE)

combined[, z_score := (vix - vix_mean) / vix_sd]

# Strategy: Short when z > 1.5, Long when z < -0.5

                                 fifelse(z_score < -0.5, 1,  # Long low VIX
                                        0))]
```
