## 10.4 Volatility Risk Premium

### 10.4.1 Prose & Intuition

The Volatility Risk Premium (VRP) is one of the most persistent anomalies in financial markets. Implied volatility—embedded in option prices—consistently exceeds the volatility that actually materialises. This gap represents compensation for bearing uncertainty risk.

Why does this premium exist? Investors are willing to pay above fair value for protection against market crashes. This creates a systematic imbalance: more buyers than sellers of volatility protection. Market makers and volatility sellers earn this premium by providing insurance.

The VRP is remarkably stable across asset classes, time periods, and market conditions. However, harvesting it requires surviving occasional severe drawdowns when realised volatility explodes above implied. These tail events can wipe out years of premium collection in days.

### 10.4.2 Visual Evidence


``` r
# Load and prepare data
vix <- load_vix("vix")
spy <- load_market("SPY")

start_date <- as.Date("1993-01-01")
end_date <- as.Date("2023-12-31")

vix <- filter_dates(vix, start_date, end_date)
spy <- filter_dates(spy, start_date, end_date)

combined <- merge_by_date(spy[, .(date, adjusted)], vix[, .(date, vix = close)])
combined[, spy_ret := c(NA, diff(log(adjusted)))]

# Calculate 30-day realised volatility
combined[, rv_30 := frollapply(spy_ret^2, 30, function(x) sqrt(sum(x) * 252 / length(x)))]
combined[, rv_30_pct := rv_30 * 100]

# Forward 30-day realised (what actually happened)
combined[, fwd_rv_30 := shift(rv_30_pct, -30)]

# VRP = VIX - forward realised
combined[, vrp := vix - fwd_rv_30]
```

### 10.4.3 Mathematical Derivation

**VRP Definition**

$$
VRP_t = IV_t - E_t[RV_{t,t+\tau}]
$$

where $IV_t$ is implied volatility at time $t$ and $RV_{t,t+\tau}$ is realised volatility over period $\tau$.

**Variance Risk Premium**

In variance terms (squared volatility):

$$
VRP^{var}_t = IV_t^2 - E_t[RV_{t,t+\tau}^2]
$$

This is more directly tradeable via variance swaps.

**Sources of the Premium**

1. **Insurance Premium**: Investors pay for downside protection
2. **Leverage Constraints**: Inability to leverage forces premium
3. **Jump Risk**: Rare but severe events justify premium
4. **Stochastic Volatility Risk**: Uncertainty about future volatility

**Risk-Adjusted VRP**

$$
\text{VRP Sharpe} = \frac{E[VRP]}{\sigma[VRP]}
$$

Historical VRP Sharpe ratios often exceed 1.0, suggesting attractive risk-adjusted returns.

## 10.5 Variance Swaps and Straddles

### 10.5.1 Prose & Intuition

Variance swaps provide the cleanest exposure to volatility as an asset. Unlike options that mix delta and vega exposure, variance swaps pay the difference between realised variance and a strike agreed at inception.

The payoff is purely variance: no delta hedging required, no gamma path dependency, just realised variance vs. struck variance. This makes them ideal for expressing views on volatility levels.

Straddles (long call + long put at the same strike) provide similar exposure but require delta hedging. Their payoff depends on both the magnitude and timing of moves. A straddle profits from large moves in either direction, losing only if the underlying stays flat.

The relationship between variance swap strikes and implied volatility reveals information about vol-of-vol and jump expectations embedded in option prices.

### 10.5.2 Visual Evidence

### 10.5.3 Mathematical Derivation

**Variance Swap Payoff**

$$
\text{Payoff} = N_{var} \times (\sigma_{RV}^2 - K_{var})
$$

where $N_{var}$ is variance notional and $K_{var}$ is struck variance.

**Vega Notional Conversion**

$$
N_{vega} = N_{var} \times 2 K_{vol}
$$

This converts variance notional to volatility terms.

**Fair Strike (Replicating Portfolio)**

Under no-arbitrage, the fair variance strike equals:

$$
K_{var} = \frac{2}{T} \left[ \int_0^F \frac{P(K)}{K^2} dK + \int_F^\infty \frac{C(K)}{K^2} dK \right]
$$

This is related to VIX calculation—integrating OTM options.

**Straddle Greeks**

At inception (ATM straddle):
- **Delta**: ≈ 0 (call delta + put delta = 1 + (-1) = 0)
- **Gamma**: Maximum at ATM
- **Vega**: Maximum at ATM
- **Theta**: Maximum negative at ATM

## 10.6 VIX Products and ETPs

### 10.6.1 Prose & Intuition

VIX exchange-traded products (ETPs) revolutionised volatility trading by making it accessible to retail investors. Products like VXX (long volatility) and SVXY (short volatility) track VIX futures indices.

The critical insight is that these products don't track spot VIX—they track VIX futures. This creates persistent structural effects:

- **Long VIX ETPs** suffer from contango roll, losing value steadily in normal markets
- **Short VIX ETPs** benefit from contango roll, gaining value steadily until they get crushed in spikes

The February 2018 "Volmageddon" demonstrated the risks. XIV (inverse VIX ETP) lost 90%+ overnight when VIX doubled, forcing liquidation. This tail risk is inherent in short volatility strategies.

### 10.6.3 Mathematical Derivation

**VIX Futures Roll Return**

When rolling from near-month to next-month futures:

$$
R_{roll} = \ln\left(\frac{F_1}{F_2}\right)
$$

In contango ($F_2 > F_1$), roll return is negative.

**ETP Daily Return Model**

$$
R_{ETP} = R_{spot} - R_{roll} - \text{fees}
$$

For constant contango, long ETP loses approximately:

$$
\text{Annual Decay} \approx \frac{F_2 - F_1}{F_1} \times \frac{252}{T_2 - T_1}
$$

**Inverse ETP Mechanics**

Inverse ETPs rebalance daily to maintain -1x exposure:

$$
V_{t+1} = V_t \times (1 - R_{underlying})
$$

This creates convexity that amplifies losses during volatility spikes.

**Leverage Decay**

For leveraged/inverse products held over multiple periods:

$$
V_T = V_0 \prod_{t=1}^{T} (1 + L \times R_t)
$$

where $L$ is leverage. Variance drag reduces returns:

$$
E[\text{Variance Drag}] \approx -\frac{L^2 \sigma^2}{2}
$$
