# Single-Asset Mean Reversion

Mean reversion is the opposite philosophy from trend following. While trend followers say "the trend is your friend," mean reversion traders say "what goes up must come down." Both can be profitable—they work in different market regimes.

The key question: *does this particular asset or spread actually mean-revert?* Many assets don't—stock prices can go up indefinitely (or to zero). But spreads, oscillators, and certain market conditions exhibit genuine mean-reverting behaviour.

---

## 8.1 Mean Reversion Foundations

### 8.1.1 Prose/Intuition

Mean reversion arises from several mechanisms:

**Overreaction and correction:**
- Investors overreact to news (both good and bad)
- Prices overshoot fair value
- Subsequent correction drives prices back

**Liquidity provision:**
- Market makers provide liquidity by trading against order flow
- Large buy orders push prices up temporarily
- Market makers' subsequent selling pushes prices back

**Inventory effects:**
- Dealers accumulate inventory when buying from sellers
- To reduce inventory risk, they cut prices
- Prices revert as inventory normalises

**Fundamental constraints:**
- Valuations have bounds (can't be negative, P/E can't be infinite)
- Extreme valuations attract arbitrageurs
- Prices are pulled back toward fair value

Not all price series mean-revert. Stock prices are generally believed to follow random walks (or near-random walks). But *spreads* between related assets, *deviations* from fair value, and *oscillator readings* often do mean-revert.

### 8.1.3 Mathematical Derivation

**Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process:**

The continuous-time OU process is:

$$
dp_t = \theta(\mu - p_t)dt + \sigma dW_t
$$

where:
- $\theta > 0$ is the mean-reversion speed
- $\mu$ is the long-term mean
- $\sigma$ is the volatility
- $dW_t$ is a Wiener process (Brownian motion)

**Solution:**

The OU process has the closed-form solution:

$$
p_t = \mu + (p_0 - \mu)e^{-\theta t} + \sigma \int_0^t e^{-\theta(t-s)} dW_s
$$

The first term shows prices converge to $\mu$ exponentially. The integral term adds random noise.

**Stationary Distribution:**

As $t \to \infty$, the OU process converges to a stationary normal distribution:

$$
p_\infty \sim N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{2\theta}\right)
$$

Higher $\theta$ (faster mean reversion) means lower variance around $\mu$.

**Half-Life:**

The half-life is the expected time for the price to move halfway from $p_0$ to $\mu$:

$$
t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln(2)}{\theta}
$$

If $\theta = 0.1$ (per day), half-life = $\ln(2)/0.1 \approx 7$ days.

**Variance Ratio Test:**

The variance ratio test detects mean reversion:

$$
VR(q) = \frac{\text{Var}(r_t + r_{t+1} + ... + r_{t+q-1})}{q \times \text{Var}(r_t)}
$$

- $VR = 1$: Random walk (no predictability)
- $VR < 1$: Mean reversion (returns negatively autocorrelated)
- $VR > 1$: Momentum (returns positively autocorrelated)


``` r
# OU parameter estimation via regression
# From dp = theta*(mu - p)*dt + sigma*dW
# Discretised: p_{t+1} - p_t = theta*mu - theta*p_t + epsilon
# Regress: delta_p = a + b*p_t + epsilon
# theta = -b, mu = -a/b

estimate_ou_params <- function(prices) {
    delta_p <- diff(prices)
    p_lag <- prices[-length(prices)]

    # Regression: delta_p = a + b * p_lag
    fit <- lm(delta_p ~ p_lag)

    a <- coef(fit)[1]
    b <- coef(fit)[2]

    theta <- -b
    mu <- -a / b
    sigma <- sd(residuals(fit))
    half_life <- log(2) / theta

    list(theta = theta, mu = mu, sigma = sigma, half_life = half_life,
         r_squared = summary(fit)$r.squared, b_pvalue = summary(fit)$coefficients[2, 4])
}

# Estimate for simulated OU process
```

``` r
# Variance ratio test
calc_variance_ratio <- function(x, q_values = c(2, 5, 10, 20)) {
    n <- length(x)
    var_1 <- var(diff(x), na.rm = TRUE)

    vr <- sapply(q_values, function(q) {
        if (n < q + 1) return(NA)

        # q-period differences
        diffs_q <- x[(q + 1):n] - x[1:(n - q)]
        var_q <- var(diffs_q, na.rm = TRUE)

        var_q / (q * var_1)
    })

    names(vr) <- paste0("VR(", q_values, ")")
    vr
}

# Test RSI for mean reversion
```

## 8.2 RSI and Bollinger Band Strategies

### 8.2.1 Prose/Intuition

Mean reversion strategies buy when prices are "too low" and sell when "too high." But how do we define these extremes?

**RSI approach:** Buy when RSI < 30 (oversold), sell when RSI > 70 (overbought). RSI naturally ranges between 0-100, making threshold selection straightforward.

**Bollinger Band approach:** Buy when price touches the lower band (2σ below mean), sell at upper band. The bands adapt to volatility, widening in turbulent markets.

**Z-score approach:** Standardise deviations from a moving average. Buy when z < -2, sell when z > +2. This gives probabilistic interpretation under normality assumptions.

The key insight: these strategies work when the underlying price *actually mean-reverts*. In strong trends, they get destroyed—buying dips that keep dipping, selling rallies that keep rallying.

### 8.2.3 Mathematical Derivation

**Z-Score Calculation:**

$$
z_t = \frac{p_t - \bar{p}_t}{\hat{\sigma}_t}
$$

where $\bar{p}_t$ is the rolling mean and $\hat{\sigma}_t$ is the rolling standard deviation.

**Threshold Entry from OU Model:**

For an OU process, the optimal entry threshold depends on transaction costs and mean-reversion speed.

If the spread follows $dp = \theta(\mu - p)dt + \sigma dW$, the expected profit from entering at $z_{\text{entry}}$ and exiting at $z_{\text{exit}} = 0$ is:

$$
E[\text{Profit}] = z_{\text{entry}} \times \hat{\sigma} - 2c
$$

where $c$ is the one-way transaction cost. We need:

$$
z_{\text{entry}} > \frac{2c}{\hat{\sigma}}
$$

**Expected Holding Time:**

For an OU process starting at $z_0$, the expected time to reach $z = 0$ is:

$$
E[T] \approx \frac{|z_0|}{\theta \times \hat{\sigma}}
$$

Higher $\theta$ (faster mean reversion) means shorter holding times.

**Expected Profit per Trade:**

$$
E[\text{Profit per Trade}] = |z_{\text{entry}}| \times \hat{\sigma} - 2c
$$

The trade-off:
- Higher threshold → fewer trades but more profit per trade
- Lower threshold → more trades but less profit per trade (and higher transaction cost drag)


``` r
# Simulate mean reversion strategy with different thresholds
spy <- load_market("SPY")
spy <- filter_dates(spy, as.Date("2015-01-01"), as.Date("2023-12-31"))
spy[, returns := log(adjusted / shift(adjusted))]

spy[, ma_50 := frollmean(adjusted, 50)]
spy[, ma_std := frollapply(adjusted, 50, sd)]
spy[, z_score := (adjusted - ma_50) / ma_std]

spy <- spy[!is.na(z_score) & !is.na(returns)]

# Test strategy with different thresholds
test_mr_strategy <- function(data, entry_threshold, exit_threshold = 0, cost = 0.001) {
    dt <- copy(data)

    position <- 0
    positions <- numeric(nrow(dt))
    trades <- 0

    for (i in 2:nrow(dt)) {
        z <- dt$z_score[i - 1]

        # Entry
        if (position == 0) {
            if (z < -entry_threshold) {
                position <- 1
                trades <- trades + 1
            } else if (z > entry_threshold) {
                position <- -1
                trades <- trades + 1
            }
        }
        # Exit
        else if (position == 1 && z > exit_threshold) {
            position <- 0
            trades <- trades + 1
        }
        else if (position == -1 && z < -exit_threshold) {
            position <- 0
            trades <- trades + 1
        }

        positions[i] <- position
    }

    dt[, position := positions]
    dt[, strat_ret := position * returns - abs(position - shift(position, 1)) * cost / 2]
    dt <- dt[!is.na(strat_ret)]

    list(
        sharpe = sharpe_ratio(dt$strat_ret),
        ann_ret = annualised_return(dt$strat_ret, type = "simple"),
        trades = trades,
        trades_per_year = trades / (nrow(dt) / 252)
    )
}

# Test different thresholds
thresholds <- seq(1.0, 3.0, by = 0.25)
results <- data.table()

for (thresh in thresholds) {
    perf <- test_mr_strategy(spy, thresh, exit_threshold = 0, cost = 0.001)
    results <- rbind(results, data.table(
        Threshold = thresh,
        Sharpe = perf$sharpe,
        Ann_Return = perf$ann_ret * 100,
        Trades_Per_Year = perf$trades_per_year
    ))
}
```

## 8.3 Short-Term Reversals

### 8.3.1 Prose/Intuition

At very short time horizons (intraday to a few days), returns often exhibit *negative* autocorrelation—yesterday's winners underperform, yesterday's losers outperform.

This "short-term reversal" effect arises from:
- **Market making:** Dealers provide liquidity by buying when others sell (and vice versa), then unwind positions
- **Inventory rebalancing:** Institutions rebalance after large moves
- **Overreaction:** Traders overreact to news, then correct
- **Bid-ask bounce:** Prices oscillate between bid and ask

The effect is well-documented but often absorbed by transaction costs at retail trading frequencies. It's primarily exploited by high-frequency traders with minimal costs.

``` r
    n <- length(x)
```

### 8.3.3 Mathematical Derivation

**Return Reversal Model:**

A simple reversal model:

$$
r_t = \alpha + \beta r_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t
$$

Mean reversion implies $\beta < 0$. If $\beta = -0.1$, a 1% up day predicts a -0.1% reversal.

**Testing for Reversal:**

We test $H_0: \beta = 0$ vs $H_1: \beta < 0$ using standard regression t-tests.

The t-statistic:

$$
t = \frac{\hat{\beta}}{\text{SE}(\hat{\beta})}
$$

With thousands of observations, even tiny effects become statistically significant, so we also examine economic magnitude.

**Transaction Cost Break-Even:**

For the reversal strategy to profit:

$$
|\beta| \times \sigma_r > 2c
$$

where $c$ is the one-way cost. If $\beta = -0.05$, $\sigma_r = 1\%$, and $c = 0.1\%$:

$$
0.05 \times 1\% = 0.05\% < 2 \times 0.1\% = 0.2\%
$$

The reversal is too small to overcome costs.

## Quick Reference

### Mean Reversion Tests

| Test | Formula | Interpretation |
|------|---------|----------------|
| OU Regression | $\Delta p = a + b \cdot p_{t-1}$ | $b < 0$ indicates mean reversion |
| Variance Ratio | $\frac{\text{Var}(r_q)}{q \cdot \text{Var}(r_1)}$ | $VR < 1$ indicates mean reversion |
| Autocorrelation | $\rho_k = \text{Corr}(r_t, r_{t-k})$ | $\rho_1 < 0$ indicates short-term reversal |

### OU Process Parameters

| Parameter | Formula | Interpretation |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| Mean-reversion speed | $\theta = -b$ | Higher = faster reversion |
| Long-term mean | $\mu = -a/b$ | Equilibrium level |
| Half-life | $t_{1/2} = \ln(2)/\theta$ | Time to revert halfway |
| Stationary variance | $\sigma^2/(2\theta)$ | Long-run volatility |

### Strategy Thresholds

| Approach | Entry | Exit | Notes |
|----------|-------|------|-------|
| RSI | RSI < 30 / > 70 | RSI = 50 | Classic levels |
| Bollinger | Price < Lower / > Upper | Middle band | 2σ bands |
| Z-Score | $|z| > 2$ | $z = 0$ | Standardised |

### Key Insights

1. **Test before trading:** Not all series mean-revert; prices often don't
2. **Half-life matters:** Series with half-life > 30 days are hard to trade
3. **Transaction costs kill:** Reversal effects are often smaller than costs
4. **Regime dependence:** Mean reversion works in ranges, fails in trends
5. **Spreads vs levels:** Spreads between related assets are more likely to mean-revert
