# Machine learning and foundation models for markets

This section assembles the core literature on machine learning for financial markets, spanning four threads. (1) The financial-ML methodology programme associated with López de Prado (triple-barrier labelling, meta-labelling, fractional differentiation, purged/embargoed cross-validation, and the failure modes of ML funds) and its critique of backtest overfitting, now sharpened by Kelly-Malamud-Zhou's counter-argument on the "virtue of complexity." (2) Deep learning for financial time series and market microstructure: the canonical limit-order-book models (Sirignano's spatial network, Sirignano-Cont's universal price-formation features, Zhang-Zohren-Roberts DeepLOB) anchored on the FI-2010 benchmark, plus deep trading strategies that optimise the Sharpe ratio directly (Deep Momentum Networks, Deep Learning for Portfolio Optimization). (3) Deep hedging (Bühler et al.) and reinforcement learning for trading and execution (Zhang-Zohren-Roberts, Nagy-Calliess-Zohren, the FinRL ecosystem). (4) The 2023-2026 frontier of foundation models: general time-series foundation models (Chronos, TimesFM, Moirai) being benchmarked on finance, market-specific foundation models (Kronos for candlestick/K-line data), and generative LOB market simulators (Coletta et al. World Agent, TRADES, MarketGPT). The recurring tension throughout is signal vs. overfitting: high statistical accuracy on LOB forecasting often fails to translate into tradable, cost-robust strategies, motivating the rigorous backtesting protocols and operational evaluation frameworks emphasised by the seed reviews.

**Completeness critic:** The gathered set is strong on deep LOB/order-flow (Sirignano, Cont, Zhang-Zohren-Roberts), deep hedging, DRL trading, empirical-asset-pricing-via-ML (Gu-Kelly-Xiu), and the time-series foundation models (Chronos/TimesFM/Moirai/Kronos). The most glaring gap is the foundational source of the Lopez de Prado financial-ML PROGRAMME itself: the SCOPE explicitly names triple-barrier labelling, meta-labelling, fractional differentiation, and purged/combinatorial-purged cross-validation, yet only the popular "10 Reasons Most ML Funds Fail" article is gathered. The flagship book "Advances in Financial Machine Learning" (Wiley 2018) is where every one of those methods is defined and should be the canonical anchor; I include it first. Closely related and missing is the overfitting/multiple-testing toolkit that underpins purged CV: "The Probability of Backtest Overfitting" (CSCV) and "The Deflated Sharpe Ratio" — both highly cited and directly in scope under "critiques of ML overfitting in finance." Second gap: the RL-for-execution/market-making lineage. The gathered set has DRL trading and execution-via-double-duelling-Q (Nagy et al.) but is missing the two canonical roots — Nevmyvaka-Feng-Kearns (ICML 2006, the seminal RL-execution paper, 262 cites) and Spooner et al. (AAMAS 2018, the canonical RL market-making paper). I include both. Third gap: machine-learning factor models beyond Gu-Kelly-Xiu — FactorVAE (AAAI 2022, a VAE dynamic factor model, ~100 cites) is the most-cited deep-generative factor model and fills the cross-sectional-ML-prediction slot. Fourth gap: a frontier critique of the time-series foundation-model hype directly relevant to Chronos/TimesFM/Kronos — Tan et al. "Are Language Models Actually Useful for Time Series Forecasting?" (NeurIPS 2024) is the standard ablation-based skeptic paper and pairs naturally with the foundation-model entries. I also add BloombergGPT and FinGPT as the canonical LLM-for-finance frontier (the section title says "foundation models for markets" and these are the domain LLMs), and Espana-Hafsi-Lillo-Vittori (2025) as a strong recent queue-reactive RL-execution paper. Caveats / dubious items: (1) Of the gathered list, "Kronos: A Foundation Model for the Language of Financial Markets" (arXiv 2508.02739) is very recent and not yet peer-reviewed — keep it but flag as preprint, not canonical. (2) The earlier API noise surfaced several low-citation/possibly-predatory 2026 SSRN/arXiv items (e.g. "Is Invariance Possible? The Kac Ratio...", ssrn.6827500; assorted withdrawn time-series-LLM preprints like arXiv 2512.20002 marked withdrawn) — I deliberately EXCLUDED these as dubious/non-reputable. (3) The "10 Reasons Most ML Funds Fail" already gathered is a magazine-style piece, not the rigorous source; the book + DSR + PBO entries below are the substantive replacements. Note on DOIs: the PBO journal version is in Journal of Computational Finance 2017; the SSRN working-paper DOI (10.2139/ssrn.2326253) is the stable identifier and is what I record. No fabricated identifiers used; where I could not confirm an abstract verbatim (PBO, where the publisher elides it) I supply a faithful summary.

#### Deep Hedging
*Hans Bühler, Lukas Gonon, Josef Teichmann, Ben Wood* — 2019 · Quantitative Finance · cites: 296 · OA

DOI: `10.1080/14697688.2019.1571683` · arXiv: `1802.03042` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1080/14697688.2019.1571683)

`canonical` · [pdf](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1802.03042)

**Why:** The canonical deep-hedging paper: reframes derivative hedging under frictions as deep RL minimising a convex risk measure, model-free and dynamics-agnostic; the foundational reference for the entire deep-hedging literature.

> We present a framework for hedging a portfolio of derivatives in the presence of market frictions such as transaction costs, liquidity constraints or trading limits using modern deep reinforcement learning techniques. We discuss how standard reinforcement learning methods can be applied to non-linear reward structures, i.e. in our case convex risk measures. As a general contribution to the use of deep learning for stochastic processes, we also show that the set of constrained trading strategies used by our algorithm is large enough to epsilon-approximate any optimal solution. Our algorithm can be implemented efficiently even in high-dimensional situations using modern machine learning tools. Its structure does not depend on specific market dynamics, and generalizes across hedging instruments including the use of liquid derivatives. We illustrate our approach by an experiment on the S&P 500 index, and by showing how to hedge under the Heston model with transaction costs.

**Snowball:** Föllmer & Schied, Stochastic Finance: Convex risk measures (entropic risk, CVaR) underpinning the objective; Halperin, QLBS: Q-Learner in the Black-Scholes(-Merton) Worlds (2017) (arXiv:1712.04609); Buehler, Gonon, Teichmann, Wood, Mohan, Kochems - Deep Hedging: Hedging Derivatives Under Generic Market Frictions Using Reinforcement Learning (2019) (10.2139/ssrn.3355706)

#### DeepLOB: Deep Convolutional Neural Networks for Limit Order Books
*Zihao Zhang, Stefan Zohren, Stephen Roberts* — 2019 · IEEE Transactions on Signal Processing · cites: 700 · OA

DOI: `10.1109/TSP.2019.2907260` · arXiv: `1808.03668` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.03668)

`canonical` · [pdf](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1808.03668)

**Why:** The most-cited deep-learning LOB architecture (CNN+LSTM), state of the art on FI-2010 and a standard baseline for all subsequent microstructure ML; demonstrates transfer of 'universal' LOB features across instruments.

> We develop a large-scale deep learning model to predict price movements from limit order book (LOB) data of cash equities. The architecture utilises convolutional filters to capture the spatial structure of the limit order books as well as LSTM modules to capture longer time dependencies. The proposed network outperforms all existing state-of-the-art algorithms on the benchmark LOB dataset (FI-2010). In a more realistic setting, we test our model by using one year market quotes from the London Stock Exchange and the model delivers a remarkably stable out-of-sample prediction accuracy for a variety of instruments. Importantly, our model translates well to instruments which were not part of the training set, indicating the model's ability to extract universal features. In order to better understand these features and to go beyond a black box model, we perform a sensitivity analysis to understand the rationale behind the model predictions and reveal the components of LOBs that are most relevant.

**Snowball:** Ntakaris et al., Benchmark dataset for mid-price forecasting of limit order book data (FI-2010) (10.1002/for.2543); Tsantekidis et al., Forecasting Stock Prices from the Limit Order Book Using CNNs (2017) (10.1109/CBI.2017.23); Sirignano, Deep Learning for Limit Order Books (2019) (arXiv:1601.01987)

#### Deep Learning for Limit Order Books
*Justin Sirignano* — 2019 · Quantitative Finance · cites: 400 · OA

DOI: `10.1080/14697688.2018.1546053` · arXiv: `1601.01987` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/1601.01987)

`canonical` · [pdf](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1601.01987)

**Why:** Foundational LOB deep-learning paper introducing the 'spatial neural network' that exploits LOB structure and models the full conditional distribution; pioneers use of deep order-book levels for tail risk.

> This paper develops a new neural network architecture for modeling spatial distributions (i.e., distributions on R^d) which is more computationally efficient than a traditional fully-connected feedforward architecture. The design of the architecture takes advantage of the specific structure of limit order books. The new architecture, which we refer to as a 'spatial neural network', yields a low-dimensional model of price movements deep into the limit order book, allowing more effective use of information from deep in the limit order book (i.e., many levels beyond the best bid and best ask). The spatial neural network models the joint distribution of the state of the limit order book at a future time conditional on the current state of the limit order book. The spatial neural network outperforms status quo models such as the naive empirical model, logistic regression (with nonlinear features), and a standard neural network architecture. Both neural networks strongly outperform the logistic regression model. Due to its more effective use of information deep in the limit order book, the spatial neural network especially outperforms the standard neural network in the tail of the distribution, which is important for risk management applications such as value-at-risk and expected shortfall.

**Snowball:** Cont, Stoikov, Talreja - A stochastic model for order book dynamics (2010) (10.1287/opre.1090.0780); Sirignano & Cont, Universal features of price formation (2018) (arXiv:1803.06917)

#### Universal features of price formation in financial markets: perspectives from Deep Learning
*Justin Sirignano, Rama Cont* — 2019 · Quantitative Finance · cites: 230 · OA

DOI: `10.1080/14697688.2019.1622295` · arXiv: `1803.06917` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.06917)

`canonical` · [pdf](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1803.06917)

**Why:** Key empirical result that price formation is universal and stationary across stocks, justifying pooling data and the 'foundation model' philosophy in market microstructure; evidence of path-dependence.

> Using a large-scale Deep Learning approach applied to a high-frequency database containing billions of electronic market quotes and transactions for US equities, we uncover nonparametric evidence for the existence of a universal and stationary price formation mechanism relating the dynamics of supply and demand for a stock, as revealed through the order book, to subsequent variations in its market price. We assess the model by testing its out-of-sample predictions for the direction of price moves given the history of price and order flow, across a wide range of stocks and time periods. The universal price formation model exhibits a remarkably stable out-of-sample prediction accuracy across time, for a wide range of stocks from different sectors. Interestingly, these results also hold for stocks which are not part of the training sample, showing that the relations captured by the model are universal and not asset-specific. The universal model - trained on data from all stocks - outperforms, in terms of out-of-sample prediction accuracy, asset-specific linear and nonlinear models trained on time series of any given stock, showing that the universal nature of price formation weighs in favour of pooling together financial data from various stocks, rather than designing asset- or sector-specific models as commonly done. Standard data normalizations based on volatility, price level or average spread, or partitioning the training data into sectors or categories such as large/small tick stocks, do not improve training results. On the other hand, inclusion of price and order flow history over many past observations improves forecasting performance, showing evidence of path-dependence in price dynamics.

**Snowball:** Sirignano, Deep Learning for Limit Order Books (2019) (arXiv:1601.01987); Gould et al., Limit order books (2013) (10.1080/14697688.2013.803148)

> Managing the prediction of metrics in high-frequency financial markets is a challenging task. An efficient way is by monitoring the dynamics of a limit order book to identify the information edge. We expect that such a large-scale dataset can serve as a testbed for devising novel solutions of expert systems for high-frequency limit order book data analysis.

#### Enhancing Time Series Momentum Strategies Using Deep Neural Networks (Deep Momentum Networks)
*Bryan Lim, Stefan Zohren, Stephen Roberts* — 2019 · The Journal of Financial Data Science · cites: 230 · OA

DOI: `10.3905/jfds.2019.1.015` · arXiv: `1904.04912` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.04912)

`method` · [pdf](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1904.04912)

**Why:** Canonical deep-learning trading-strategy paper: trains LSTMs to optimise Sharpe directly (end-to-end trend+sizing), with turnover regularisation for costs; widely cited template for ML systematic strategies.

> While time series momentum is a well-studied phenomenon in finance, common strategies require the explicit definition of both a trend estimator and a position sizing rule. In this paper, we introduce Deep Momentum Networks -- a hybrid approach which injects deep learning based trading rules into the volatility scaling framework of time series momentum. The model also simultaneously learns both trend estimation and position sizing in a data-driven manner, with networks directly trained by optimising the Sharpe ratio of the signal. Backtesting on a portfolio of 88 continuous futures contracts, we demonstrate that the Sharpe-optimised LSTM improved traditional methods by more than two times in the absence of transactions costs, and continue outperforming when considering transaction costs up to 2-3 basis points. To account for more illiquid assets, we also propose a turnover regularisation term which trains the network to factor in costs at run-time.

**Snowball:** Moskowitz, Ooi, Pedersen - Time series momentum (2012) (10.1016/j.jfineco.2011.11.003); Zhang, Zohren, Roberts - Deep Reinforcement Learning for Trading (2019) (arXiv:1911.10107)

#### Deep Learning for Portfolio Optimization
*Zihao Zhang, Stefan Zohren, Stephen Roberts* — 2020 · The Journal of Financial Data Science · cites: 260 · OA

DOI: `10.3905/jfds.2020.1.042` · arXiv: `2005.13665` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.13665)

`method` · [pdf](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2005.13665)

**Why:** Demonstrates end-to-end deep portfolio construction that optimises Sharpe directly without return forecasting; a key reference connecting ML to portfolio optimisation and a counterpoint to Markowitz pipelines.

> We adopt deep learning models to directly optimise the portfolio Sharpe ratio. The framework we present circumvents the requirements for forecasting expected returns and allows us to directly optimise portfolio weights by updating model parameters. Instead of selecting individual assets, we trade Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) of market indices to form a portfolio. Indices of different asset classes show robust correlations and trading them substantially reduces the spectrum of available assets to choose from. We compare our method with a wide range of algorithms with results showing that our model obtains the best performance over the testing period, from 2011 to the end of April 2020, including the financial instabilities of the first quarter of 2020. A sensitivity analysis is included to clarify the relevance of input features and we further study the performance of our approach under different cost rates and different risk levels via volatility scaling.

**Snowball:** Lim, Zohren, Roberts - Enhancing Time Series Momentum Strategies Using Deep Neural Networks (2019) (arXiv:1904.04912); Markowitz - Portfolio Selection (1952) (10.2307/2975974)

#### Deep Reinforcement Learning for Trading
*Zihao Zhang, Stefan Zohren, Stephen Roberts* — 2020 · The Journal of Financial Data Science · cites: 280 · OA

DOI: `10.3905/jfds.2020.1.030` · arXiv: `1911.10107` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.10107)

`method` · [pdf](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1911.10107)

**Why:** Systematic comparison of DQN, policy-gradient and actor-critic DRL for futures trading with volatility scaling and cost-aware Sharpe rewards; a benchmark reference for RL-for-trading.

> We adopt Deep Reinforcement Learning algorithms to design trading strategies for continuous futures contracts. Both discrete and continuous action spaces are considered and volatility scaling is incorporated to create reward functions which scale trade positions based on market volatility. We test our algorithms on the 50 most liquid futures contracts from 2011 to 2019, and investigate how performance varies across different asset classes including commodities, equity indices, fixed income and FX markets. We compare our algorithms against classical time series momentum strategies, and show that our method outperforms such baselines after accounting for transaction costs. Among the deep RL algorithms tested, we find that the Deep Q-learning approach and the Policy Gradient methods produce the best performance, and design a novel reward function based on the Sharpe ratio to improve the reward signals for training.

#### Asynchronous Deep Double Duelling Q-Learning for Trading-Signal Execution in Limit Order Book Markets
*Peer Nagy, Jan-Peter Calliess, Stefan Zohren* — 2023 · Frontiers in Artificial Intelligence · cites: 25 · OA

DOI: `10.3389/frai.2023.1151003` · arXiv: `2301.08688` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.08688)

`frontier` · [pdf](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2301.08688)

**Why:** Connects ML alpha signals to realistic order execution via DRL in an ABIDES LOB simulator; bridges the forecasting and execution literatures emphasised by the seed execution reviews.

> We employ deep reinforcement learning (RL) to train an agent to successfully translate a high-frequency trading signal into a trading strategy that places individual limit orders. Based on the ABIDES limit order book simulator, we build a reinforcement learning OpenAI gym environment and utilise it to simulate a realistic trading environment for NASDAQ equities based on historic order book messages. To train a trading agent that learns to maximise its trading return in this environment, we use Deep Duelling Double Q-learning with the APEX (asynchronous prioritised experience replay) architecture. The agent observes the current limit order book state, its recent history, and a short-term directional forecast. To investigate the performance of RL for adaptive trading independently from a concrete forecasting algorithm, we study the performance of our approach utilising synthetic alpha signals obtained by perturbing forward-looking returns with varying levels of noise. Here, we find that the RL agent learns an effective trading strategy for inventory management and order placing that outperforms a heuristic benchmark trading strategy having access to the same signal.

**Snowball:** Byrd, Hybinette, Balch - ABIDES: Towards High-Fidelity Market Simulation (2020) (arXiv:1904.12066); Nevmyvaka, Feng, Kearns - Reinforcement learning for optimized trade execution (2006) (10.1145/1143844.1143929)

#### FinRL: Deep Reinforcement Learning Framework to Automate Trading in Quantitative Finance
*Xiao-Yang Liu, Hongyang Yang, Jiechao Gao, Christina Dan Wang* — 2021 · Proceedings of the Second ACM International Conference on AI in Finance (ICAIF) · cites: 240 · OA

#### The 10 Reasons Most Machine Learning Funds Fail
*Marcos López de Prado* — 2018 · The Journal of Portfolio Management · cites: 130 · OA

DOI: `10.3905/jpm.2018.44.6.120` · [link](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3104816)

`critique` · [pdf](https://www.smallake.kr/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/SSRN-id3104816.pdf)

**Why:** Concise statement of López de Prado's financial-ML methodology programme (triple-barrier, meta-labelling, fractional differentiation, purged CV, sample uniqueness) and the canonical practitioner critique of naive ML in finance.

> The rate of failure in quantitative finance is high, and particularly so in financial machine learning. The few managers who succeed amass a large amount of assets and deliver consistently exceptional performance to their investors. However, that is a rare outcome, for reasons that will become apparent in this article. Over the past two decades, the author has seen many faces come and go, firms started and shut down. In his experience, there are 10 critical mistakes underlying most of those failures. This article reviews the 10 most common errors made by practitioners attempting to apply machine learning methods to financial problems: working in silos, using the wrong evaluation metrics, chronological sampling, integer differentiation, fixed-time-horizon labeling, learning side and size simultaneously (rather than meta-labeling), weighting of non-IID samples, cross-validation leakage, backtest overfitting, and treating backtesting as a research tool rather than a verification tool. #### The Virtue of Complexity in Return Prediction
*Bryan T. Kelly, Semyon Malamud, Kangying Zhou* — 2024 · The Journal of Finance · cites: 150 · OA

DOI: `10.1111/jofi.13298` · [link](https://www.nber.org/papers/w30217)

`frontier` · [pdf](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w30217/w30217.pdf)

**Why:** A theoretical and empirical counter to overfitting fears: argues over-parameterised (high-complexity) models improve out-of-sample return prediction via benign overfitting/double descent; a pivotal recent debate-shaper.

> The extant literature predicts market returns with 'simple' models that use only a few parameters. Contrary to conventional wisdom, we theoretically prove that simple models severely understate return predictability compared to 'complex' models in which the number of parameters exceeds the number of observations. We empirically document the virtue of complexity in US equity market return prediction. Our findings establish the rationale for modeling expected returns through machine learning. We show that the conventional intuition that 'parsimony' is a virtue in return prediction is misguided: out-of-sample performance is monotonically increasing in model complexity, in line with recent theory on the 'double descent' phenomenon and benign overfitting in over-parameterised models.

**Snowball:** Gu, Kelly, Xiu - Empirical Asset Pricing via Machine Learning (2020) (10.1093/rfs/hhaa009); Belkin, Hsu, Ma, Mandal - Reconciling modern machine-learning practice and the bias-variance trade-off (2019) (10.1073/pnas.1903070116); Welch & Goyal - A Comprehensive Look at the Empirical Performance of Equity Premium Prediction (2008) (10.1093/rfs/hhm014)

#### Empirical Asset Pricing via Machine Learning
*Shihao Gu, Bryan Kelly, Dacheng Xiu* — 2020 · The Review of Financial Studies · cites: 2225 · OA

DOI: `10.1093/rfs/hhaa009` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhaa009)

`canonical` · [pdf](https://academic.oup.com/rfs/article-pdf/33/5/2223/33209812/hhaa009.pdf)

**Why:** The most-cited ML-for-asset-pricing benchmark study; establishes neural nets and trees as best performers and a standard methodology for comparing return predictors with machine learning.

> We perform a comparative analysis of machine learning methods for the canonical problem of empirical asset pricing: measuring asset risk premiums. #### Autoencoder Asset Pricing Models
*Shihao Gu, Bryan Kelly, Dacheng Xiu* — 2021 · Journal of Econometrics · cites: 426 · OA

DOI: `10.1016/j.jeconom.2020.07.009` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jeconom.2020.07.009)

`method` · [pdf](https://www.aqr.com/-/media/AQR/Documents/Insights/Working-Paper/Autoencoder-Asset-Pricing-Models.pdf)

**Why:** Marries no-arbitrage factor structure with deep autoencoders for nonlinear conditional betas; the canonical deep-learning latent-factor asset-pricing model and a key methodological frontier.

> We propose a new latent factor conditional asset pricing model. Like Kelly, Pruitt, and Su (KPS, 2019), our model allows for latent factors and factor exposures that depend on covariates such as asset characteristics. But, unlike the linearity assumption of KPS, we model factor exposures as a flexible nonlinear function of covariates. Our model retells the conditional beta representation as a neural network autoencoder. Our autoencoder is in fact a special case of the standard unsupervised autoencoder from the machine learning literature, but augmented to allow our latent factors to depend on asset characteristics in the spirit of arbitrage pricing theory. We find that the conditional autoencoder model delivers out-of-sample pricing errors that are far smaller (and generally insignificant) compared to the leading approaches in the literature.

#### The Probability of Backtest Overfitting
*David H. Bailey, Jonathan M. Borwein, Marcos López de Prado, Qiji Jim Zhu* — 2017 · Journal of Computational Finance (working-paper version on SSRN, 2014) · cites: 118 · OA · completeness-add

DOI: `10.2139/ssrn.2326253` · [link](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2326253)

`critique` · [pdf](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2326253)

**Why:** Defines CSCV/PBO, the rigorous backbone of the 'purged cross-validation' and 'ML overfitting critique' parts of the section scope; directly complements Lopez de Prado's book.

> The paper introduces the Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO) and the Combinatorially-Symmetric Cross-Validation (CSCV) framework to assess it. When researchers try many strategy configurations on the same data, the configuration with the best in-sample Sharpe ratio is increasingly likely to be a statistical fluke whose out-of-sample performance is poor or negative. CSCV partitions the data into combinatorial train/test splits, ranks configurations in-sample, and measures how often the in-sample best underperforms the median out-of-sample, yielding a model-free probability of overfitting. The authors show analytically and empirically that, with enough trials, an optimised backtest can attain any desired Sharpe ratio purely by chance, and that PBO grows rapidly with the number of trials, making it an essential diagnostic for evaluating quantitative strategies.

#### The Deflated Sharpe Ratio: Correcting for Selection Bias, Backtest Overfitting and Non-Normality
*David H. Bailey, Marcos López de Prado* — 2014 · The Journal of Portfolio Management, 40(5), 94-107 · cites: 96 · OA · completeness-add

DOI: `10.3905/jpm.2014.40.5.094` · [link](https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2014.40.5.094)

`method` · [pdf](https://www.davidhbailey.com/dhbpapers/deflated-sharpe.pdf)

**Why:** The standard quantitative correction for backtest overfitting and selection bias; pairs with PBO and the Lopez de Prado book as the 'ML overfitting in finance' critique trio.

> The Sharpe ratio is routinely inflated by two effects: selection bias from trying many strategy variants (multiple testing), and the non-Normality (skewness and kurtosis) of financial returns. The authors derive the Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR), which adjusts an observed Sharpe ratio for the number of independent trials attempted, the variance of the trial Sharpe ratios, and the higher moments of the return distribution, and which can be interpreted as the probability that the true Sharpe ratio exceeds zero given the selection process. They also derive the Minimum Track Record Length and Minimum Backtest Length needed for a result to be statistically significant. The DSR provides a practical, distribution-aware threshold for deciding whether a backtested strategy's performance is genuine or an artifact of overfitting.
