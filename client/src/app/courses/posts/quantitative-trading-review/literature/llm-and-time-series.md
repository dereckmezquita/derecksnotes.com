# LLMs, foundation models & methods for financial time series — base-truth review

> Output of the `llm-methods-for-financial-time-series` workflow: bull + bear + methods evidence, an adversarial cross-examination, and a weighed verdict. Gathered abstracts, not yet deep-read.

## Base-truth verdict

# Time-Series-Specific Models vs Chat-LLMs for Financial Time Series: A Base-Truth Brief

## (a) Chat-LLMs as forecasters/traders: state of evidence

The pro-LLM headline is Lopez-Lira & Tang (2023): GPT-4 sentiment on news headlines predicts next-day cross-sectional returns, with a long-short strategy earning positive returns. But the much-quoted "90%" is the *non-tradable contemporaneous* reaction, and the surviving predictability concentrates in small, illiquid, hard-to-arbitrage stocks. Kim, Muhn & Nikolaev (2024) report GPT-4 beating analysts at directional earnings forecasts on anonymised statements — their leakage defence is real but partial.

The refuting camp is decisive on mechanism. Sarkar & Vafa (2024) formalise look-ahead bias: pretrained LLMs "predict" COVID-19 from 2019 transcripts, and prompt-based date constraints do *not* fix it — only pre-period training does (DatedGPT, 2026; ChronoBERT/ChronoGPT, He et al. 2025). Wongchamcharoen & Glasserman (2025) show LLMs do not robustly understand chronology, explaining why. Chen, Green, Gulen & Zhou (2024) show LLM return forecasts over-extrapolate and are miscalibrated — reproducing human biases, not exploiting dynamics. The audit by Xia et al. (2026) is damning: of 19 LLM-trading studies, 1 models costs, 1 handles survivorship, and FinMem's +23% flips to −22% under an equally-defensible window. Nuance cuts both ways: Glasserman & Lin (2023) find a "distraction effect" can dominate look-ahead bias, and He et al. (2025) find chronologically-consistent LLMs retain *some* Sharpe — leakage is rarely fatal but never safely ignored.

## (b) Time-series foundation models: do they beat simple baselines on financial data?

Chronos (Ansari et al. 2024), TimesFM (Das et al. 2023), Moirai (Woo et al. 2024), Lag-Llama, MOMENT, TimeGPT and Time-MoE establish strong zero-/few-shot forecasting — but on GENERAL benchmarks (GIFT-Eval, Monash) dominated by energy/traffic/weather. Almost none benchmark head-to-head against naive/ARIMA/DLinear *on market price data*. Finance-specific work fills the gap and is skeptical: Kronos (Shi et al. 2026) states plainly that general TSFMs on K-line data "remain limited, often underperforming non-pretrained architectures"; Rahimikia, Ni & Wang (2025) and Marconi (2025) find off-the-shelf TSFMs are matched or beaten on real market data, closing the gap only with fine-tuning; Jetwiriyanon et al. (2025) find zero-shot parity only in stable regimes, degrading sharply under shocks. Worse, Meyer et al. (2025) and TSFMAudit (Li et al. 2026) show pretraining-corpus contamination inflates the apparent TSFM edge from ~0.3–14% to an illusory 47–184%. The only robust finance signal comes from models *trained on market data* (Kronos, FinCast, Zhu et al. 2025) — and even those rarely report cost-net, walk-forward evaluations.

## (c) Transferable METHODS — promising vs hype

- **Patching (PatchTST, Nie et al. 2022/2023): PROMISING.** Subseries tokens are the decisive design that made Transformers competitive again; Tan et al. (2024) and TSMixer attribute most "LLM-for-TS" gains to patching, not the language model.
- **Attention variants (Informer, iTransformer): MIXED→PROMISING.** DLinear (Zeng et al. 2023) showed a linear layer beats elaborate Transformers; iTransformer (Liu et al. 2023) and PatchTST rebut by re-applying attention well. Attention is useful but not magic.
- **State-space / Mamba (Gu & Dao 2023; S4): PROMISING-BUT-UNPROVEN on finance.** Wang et al. (2024) find gains dataset-dependent; on markets, SAMBA (Mehrabian et al. 2024) and CrossMamba (Shuvo et al. 2026) report only modest, dataset-specific improvements.
- **Self-supervised / masked pre-training (TST, SimMTM, TS2Vec, MOMENT): PROMISING** for low-label representation learning, well-evidenced cross-domain — though not specifically validated as a market alpha source.
- **In-context / zero-shot LLM forecasting (LLMTime, GPT4TS, Time-LLM): HYPE.** Tan et al. (2024) show ablating/replacing the frozen LLM leaves accuracy unchanged or better at a fraction of the cost. The "language knowledge transfers to numbers" thesis is largely refuted.
- **Tokenisation/quantisation + scaling laws (Chronos; Edwards et al. 2024): PROMISING** as engineering; scaling laws transfer, but scale ≠ financial skill (TTM/Time-MoE show tiny models match giants).

## (d) Where the preconception holds vs breaks

**SUPPORTED:** *Domain-specific* TS models (Kronos, FinCast, FinSrag) reliably beat generic chat-LLMs and zero-shot general TSFMs on market data — the strongest robust finance signal is from market-trained models. Generic chat-LLMs as direct forecasters are weak and leakage-confounded, so "TS-specific > chat-LLM" holds *for purpose-built TS models*.

**CHALLENGED:** The preconception fails if "TS-specific" means *generic zero-shot TSFMs*. These do not reliably beat naive/ARIMA/DLinear on financial data once leakage is controlled (Meyer et al. 2025; Rahimikia et al. 2025; Marconi 2025; TFB). And Tan et al. (2024) show much TSFM machinery (the "LLM," attention) adds little over cheap baselines — so the *category* "TS-specific" is not automatically superior; only the *finance-trained subset* is.

## Adversarial cross-examination

ADVERSARIAL VERDICT: Most of the optimism does NOT survive scrutiny when the four cross-examination tests are applied. (1) STRONG CLASSICAL BASELINES: The flagship TSFM papers (Chronos, TimesFM, Moirai, Lag-Llama, MOMENT) almost never benchmark against naive/seasonal/ARIMA/ETS/DLinear ON MARKET DATA; where independent finance-specific evaluations do this (Marconi 2507.07296; Rahimikia-Ni-Wang 2511.18578; Marcondes/Lu; Jetwiriyanon et al.; Montet et al.; TFB), zero-shot TSFMs match or beat naive/classical baselines only intermittently and usually lose or tie unless pre-trained from scratch on financial data. The Tan et al. ablation and TSMixer/TTM/DLinear results further show the "LLM" and the attention add little net value over cheap baselines. (2) DATA LEAKAGE / LOOK-AHEAD: This is the decisive blow. Multiple new papers establish that headline gains are contaminated. For TSFMs, Meyer et al. (2510.13654) and TSFMAudit (Li et al. 2605.26161) show pretraining-corpus contamination of benchmark test sets is pervasive and inflates the apparent TSFM advantage from a true 0.3-14% edge to an illusory 47-184% MSE gain on leaked datasets. For chat-LLMs, Sarkar-Vafa, Glasserman-Lin (2309.17322), Wongchamcharoen-Glasserman (2511.14214 "Do LLMs Understand Chronology?"), and the ChronoBERT/ChronoGPT and DatedGPT chronologically-consistent models all confirm pretrained LLMs leak post-period knowledge; the Ludwig-Mullainathan-Rambachan econometric framework formalizes "training leakage" as a first-order validity threat. Notably the evidence is nuanced: Glasserman-Lin find a "distraction effect" can exceed look-ahead bias, and ChronoBERT finds leakage is "model- and application-specific" with surviving Sharpe ratios — so leakage is not always fatal, but it is never safely ignorable. (3) NET OF TRANSACTION COSTS: The strongest positive ML asset-pricing results (Gu-Kelly-Xiu) and the LLM-sentiment alpha (Lopez-Lira) are concentrated in small, illiquid, hard-to-arbitrage stocks. Avramov-Cheng-Metzker show ML profitability is driven by exactly these microcaps/distressed/high-volatility states and deteriorates badly under reasonable trading costs and high turnover; the Pagliaro reassessment makes the same finance-wide point. Azevedo-Hoegner-Velikov provide the most balanced rebuttal: ~57% of gross performance is destroyed by costs/decay/liquidity, yet the most sophisticated (LSTM) strategies retain net alpha up to ~1.42%/month — so a residual, smaller-than-advertised edge can survive in some setups. (4) REPRODUCIBILITY / CHERRY-PICKING: Bailey/Borwein/Lopez de Prado and Kapoor-Narayanan are the base-truth lens; the new "Agentic Trading" audit (Xia et al. 2605.19337) is devastating for the LLM-trading-agent literature — only 1 of 19 audited studies models transaction costs, only 1 handles survivorship, 15 of 19 are at the lowest reproducibility tier, and FinMem's reported +23.26% becomes -22.04% under a slightly different equally-defensible window with costs, a full sign reversal. NET ASSESSMENT: The honest base truth is that (a) generic zero-shot TSFM superiority on financial market data is unproven and frequently negative once leakage is controlled and naive/classical baselines are included; (b) the only robust positive finance signal comes from models explicitly trained/fine-tuned on market data (Kronos, FinCast, FinSrag, and from-scratch TSFMs), and even those rarely report rigorous cost-net, leakage-free, walk-forward evaluations; (c) chat-LLM return-forecasting "skill" is heavily confounded by look-ahead leakage and concentrates in unarbitrageable stocks; (d) a modest residual edge may survive for purpose-built ML/LLM signals net of costs, but the large, splashy claims do not. Optimism surviving scrutiny: roughly the Gu-Kelly-Xiu-class supervised ML result, a discounted version of domain-pretrained financial TSFMs, and small net-of-cost LSTM/ML alphas — perhaps 20-30% of the headline claims. The remainder is artifact of leakage, missing classical baselines, ignored costs, and backtest overfitting.

## Evidence by topic

### Time-series foundation models (Chronos, TimesFM, Moirai, TimeGPT, Lag-Llama, MOMENT, TinyTimeMixer, Kronos): architectures, zero-shot benchmarks, and whether they beat strong classical baselines on financial/market data specifically

A wave of 2023-2024 time-series foundation models (TSFMs) - Chronos, TimesFM, Moirai, TimeGPT, Lag-Llama, MOMENT, Tiny Time Mixers, Time-MoE - established that LLM-style pretraining on large heterogeneous time-series corpora yields strong zero-/few-shot forecasting that approaches or beats per-dataset supervised models on GENERAL benchmarks (GIFT-Eval, Monash, etc.). Crucially, almost none of these flagship papers actually evaluate head-to-head against strong classical baselines on real market/price data; their benchmarks are dominated by energy, traffic, weather, retail and macroeconomic series. The base-truth picture on finance is far more skeptical: a wave of critical and finance-specific work (Tan et al.'s "Are Language Models Actually Useful for Time Series Forecasting?", the zero-shot economic-forecasting generalisation study, BForTFin, FinCast, and the Kronos paper itself) finds that generic TSFMs frequently fail to beat naive/ARIMA/non-pretrained baselines on noisy, low signal-to-noise financial candlestick data, degrade badly under regime shifts and shocks, and only become competitive when fine-tuned or purpose-built (FinCast, Kronos). The honest adjudication is that TSFM superiority on financial market data is not established; the strongest finance-specific evidence comes from models explicitly trained on market data, while zero-shot transfer of general TSFMs to markets remains unproven and often negative.

### Chat-LLMs applied directly to trading and return forecasting (FinGPT, BloombergGPT, LLM trading agents, "can ChatGPT/LLMs forecast stock returns", news/sentiment) — supporting and refuting evidence, with data-leakage / look-ahead flags

The literature splits sharply between optimistic in-sample/quasi-out-of-sample demonstrations and methodological critiques. The pro-LLM headline result is Lopez-Lira & Tang (2023), showing GPT-4 sentiment scores on news headlines predict next-day returns with high hit-rates, echoed by Kim-Muhn-Nikolaev's finding that GPT-4 beats analysts at directional earnings forecasting; alongside these sit domain LLMs (BloombergGPT, FinGPT, FinBERT, PIXIU) and LLM trading agents (FinMem, TradingGPT) reporting backtest alpha. The refuting/cautionary camp is led by Sarkar & Vafa (2024), who formalize look-ahead (temporal) bias — pretrained models leak future knowledge (e.g. mentioning COVID-19 in 2019 transcripts) — and by Chen-Green-Gulen-Zhou (2024), who show LLM return forecasts over-extrapolate and are miscalibrated; DatedGPT (2026) operationalizes the fix with strictly time-partitioned training. The central adjudication issue is that almost every positive result is vulnerable to data contamination, look-ahead leakage, ignored transaction costs, and short/overlapping evaluation windows, so reported "forecasting ability" is frequently an artifact rather than genuine out-of-sample skill. Context papers (Gu-Kelly-Xiu's machine-learning asset pricing, FinRL, and the LLM-in-finance surveys) frame the baseline these LLM claims must beat.

- **Can ChatGPT Forecast Stock Price Movements? Return Predictability and Large Language Models** — Alejandro Lopez-Lira, Yuehua Tang (2023, *SSRN / arXiv (working paper)*, cites 336) [pro-llm, on-fin-data] — DOI `10.2139/ssrn.4412788` · arXiv `2304.07619` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.07619)
  The canonical pro-LLM paper: GPT-4 sentiment scores on news headlines significantly predict next-day cross-sectional stock returns (a long-short ChatGPT strategy earns positive returns), with predictive power rising with model size. The much-publicized 90% figure refers to the non-tradable contemporaneous reaction, not implementable alpha, and the strongest predictability is in small, illiquid stocks where arbitrage is limited.

### The skeptic case for deep learning and Transformers in time series forecasting: DLinear, simple-baseline critiques, and reproducibility/leakage in financial ML

The skeptic literature on deep learning for time series rests on three pillars. First, the simple-baseline critique: Zeng et al.'s "Are Transformers Effective for Time Series Forecasting?" (DLinear/NLinear) showed a one-layer linear model beats elaborate Transformers (Informer, Autoformer, FEDformer) on standard long-horizon benchmarks, echoing the older Makridakis/M-competition finding that classical statistical methods (and naive/seasonal baselines) routinely dominate pure ML on accuracy and cost. Second, the reproducibility-and-leakage critique: Kapoor & Narayanan document a cross-field "leakage" crisis that inflates reported ML performance, and the López de Prado / Bailey backtest-overfitting work shows financial out-of-sample gains are frequently statistical artifacts of multiple testing and look-ahead bias. Third, fair-benchmarking work (TFB, Shao et al.) shows reported gains are highly sensitive to dataset choice, lookback length, normalization, and hyperparameter budget, often reversing rankings. The defense is not empty: PatchTST and iTransformer re-establish that properly-designed Transformers can beat DLinear, TimesNet generalizes across tasks, and Gu/Kelly/Xiu show real economic gains from ML in cross-sectional asset pricing. Crucially, almost none of the canonical TS-architecture benchmarks use market/financial data (they use ETT, electricity, traffic, weather); the strongest financial-specific evidence is mixed (Gu/Kelly/Xiu positive on premia; Pagliaro and the M5/Walmart retail results cautionary), so claims that Transformers "win" on financial series remain largely untested against rigorously-leakage-controlled naive baselines.

- **Are Transformers Effective for Time Series Forecasting?** — Ailing Zeng, Muxi Chen, Lei Zhang, Qiang Xu (2023, *AAAI (Proceedings of the AAAI Conference on Artificial Intelligence)*, cites 2610) [anti-llm, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.1609/aaai.v37i9.26317` · arXiv `2205.13504` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1609/aaai.v37i9.26317)
  A single linear layer (DLinear/NLinear) beats then-state-of-the-art LTSF Transformers across nine benchmarks, indicating that reported Transformer gains came from confounds (e.g., long lookback, instance normalization) rather than the attention mechanism. This is the canonical anti-Transformer simple-baseline paper.
- **Statistical and Machine Learning forecasting methods: Concerns and ways forward** — Spyros Makridakis, Evangelos Spiliotis, Vassilios Assimakopoulos (2018, *PLOS ONE*, cites 1390) [anti-llm, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.1371/journal.pone.0194889` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0194889)
  On a large neutral benchmark, popular ML methods were dominated by simple statistical methods (and naive baselines) on accuracy at all horizons and at far higher computational cost. Establishes the simple-baseline skeptic position years before DLinear.
- **The M4 Competition: 100,000 time series and 61 forecasting methods** — Spyros Makridakis, Evangelos Spiliotis, Vassilios Assimakopoulos (2019, *International Journal of Forecasting*, cites 887) [anti-llm, on-fin-data] — DOI `10.1016/j.ijforecast.2019.04.014` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijforecast.2019.04.014)
  Across 100,000 series, pure ML methods rarely beat well-tuned statistical methods and naive/combination benchmarks; the gains came from hybridization, not from replacing classical methods. Establishes the strength of simple/statistical baselines on a large neutral testbed.
- **M5 accuracy competition: Results, findings, and conclusions** — Spyros Makridakis, Evangelos Spiliotis, Vassilios Assimakopoulos (2022, *International Journal of Forecasting*, cites 459) [context, on-fin-data] — DOI `10.1016/j.ijforecast.2021.11.013` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijforecast.2021.11.013)
  On large-scale hierarchical commercial-sales data, tree-based ML (LightGBM) won, but deep learning did not lead and most entries failed to beat simple statistical benchmarks, showing context-dependence of when ML helps. Tempers blanket claims for deep nets.
- **Leakage and the reproducibility crisis in machine-learning-based science** — Sayash Kapoor, Arvind Narayanan (2023, *Patterns (Cell Press)*, cites 663) [anti-llm, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.1016/j.patter.2023.100804` · arXiv `2207.07048` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.patter.2023.100804)
  Systematically documents that data leakage produces overoptimistic, irreproducible ML results across 17 scientific fields, providing a taxonomy and prevention protocol. The core reference for the leakage/reproducibility critique.
- **Pseudo-Mathematics and Financial Charlatanism: The Effects of Backtest Overfitting on Out-of-Sample Performance** — David H. Bailey, Jonathan M. Borwein, Marcos Lopez de Prado, Qiji Jim Zhu (2014, *Notices of the American Mathematical Society*, cites 188) [anti-llm, on-fin-data] — DOI `10.1090/noti1105` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1090/noti1105)
  Demonstrates that multiple-testing/backtest overfitting makes high in-sample Sharpe ratios achievable on noise, so financial out-of-sample claims that ignore the number of trials are unreliable. Foundational for leakage/overfitting critiques specific to markets.
- **TFB: Towards Comprehensive and Fair Benchmarking of Time Series Forecasting Methods** — Xiangfei Qiu, Jilin Hu, Lekui Zhou, Xingjian Wu, Junyang Du (2024, *Proceedings of the VLDB Endowment (PVLDB)*, cites 119) [anti-llm, on-fin-data] — DOI `10.14778/3665844.3665863` · arXiv `2403.20150` · [link](https://doi.org/10.14778/3665844.3665863)
  A standardized, multi-domain benchmark shows that forecasting-method rankings are highly sensitive to pipeline choices and that statistical baselines remain competitive, undercutting many headline deep-learning gains. Strengthens the fair-evaluation skeptic case.
- **Exploring Progress in Multivariate Time Series Forecasting: Comprehensive Benchmarking and Heterogeneity Analysis** — Zezhi Shao, Fei Wang, Yongjun Xu, Wei Wei, Chengqing Yu (2024, *IEEE Transactions on Knowledge and Data Engineering (TKDE)*, cites 144) [anti-llm, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.1109/tkde.2024.3484454` · arXiv `2310.06119` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1109/tkde.2024.3484454)
  Under a unified pipeline, much of the claimed progress in MTS forecasting evaporates and is attributable to dataset heterogeneity and inconsistent evaluation rather than model design, with simple baselines often competitive. Reinforces the benchmarking-rigor critique.
- **Are Language Models Actually Useful for Time Series Forecasting?** — Mingtian Tan, Mike A.   Ablations show the LLM in LLM-for-time-series methods contributes nothing: removing or replacing it leaves accuracy unchanged or better at a fraction of the cost. Directly refutes the value-add of language models for forecasting.
- **Empirical Asset Pricing via Machine Learning** — Shihao Gu, Bryan Kelly, Dacheng Xiu (2020, *The Review of Financial Studies*, cites 2225) [pro-llm, on-fin-data] — DOI `10.1093/rfs/hhaa009` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhaa009)
  With rigorous out-of-sample design, ML (especially shallow neural nets and trees) delivers genuine and economically large gains in cross-sectional asset-pricing prediction, identifying robust nonlinear signals. The strongest pro-ML evidence on real financial data.
- **A Time Series is Worth 64 Words: Long-term Forecasting with Transformers (PatchTST)** — Yuqi Nie, Nam H.   By inverting the tokenization (attention over variates, not time), a Transformer regains state-of-the-art performance over linear models, arguing the problem was how Transformers were applied, not attention itself. A second major Transformer-defense rebuttal.

  A 2D-variation deep architecture generalizes across five time-series tasks and provides a widely adopted benchmark library, showing deep models retain value beyond narrow long-horizon forecasting. Provides the broader-utility counter-narrative to pure linear baselines.
- **Artificial Intelligence vs. Efficient Markets: A Critical Reassessment of Predictive Models in the Big Data Era** — Antonio Pagliaro (2025, *Electronics (MDPI)*, cites 26) [anti-llm, on-fin-data] — DOI `10.3390/electronics14091721` · [link](https://doi.org/10.3390/electronics14091721)
  Argues that headline AI stock-forecasting gains often fail to survive regime changes, transaction costs, and the statistical-significance-vs-economic-relevance gap, with ensembles outperforming deep nets. A finance-specific synthesis of the skeptic case.
- **The M4 Competition: Results, findings, conclusion and way forward** — Spyros Makridakis, Evangelos Spiliotis, Vassilios Assimakopoulos (2018, *International Journal of Forecasting*, cites 580) [anti-llm, on-fin-data] — DOI `10.1016/j.ijforecast.2018.06.001` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijforecast.2018.06.001)
  States bluntly that all six pure-ML entries in M4 failed to beat a simple statistical combination benchmark, and that hybrid/combination methods won. A sharp, often-cited articulation of the simple-baseline skeptic position.

### Architectural methods transferable from LLMs / sequence modelling to time series (patching, attention variants / long-context, state-space models, positional encodings, inverted attention)

The dominant story of 2021-2026 time-series forecasting is the wholesale transfer of LLM/sequence-modelling architecture: the same building blocks invented for language (efficient/sparse attention, FlashAttention, patch tokenization, rotary/ALiBi positional schemes, and the state-space lineage S4 -> Mamba) have been ported, with strong but contested results, to multivariate forecasting. Two innovations proved decisive on standard benchmarks: PatchTST's subseries "patch" tokens with channel-independence, and iTransformer's "inversion" of attention onto the variate axis rather than the time axis. The field is genuinely adversarial: DLinear ("Are Transformers Effective?") showed a one-layer linear model beats most Transformer forecasters, and even Mamba's first careful TS evaluations are mixed, so architecture transfer is not a free lunch. Critically, almost all canonical evidence comes from weather/electricity/traffic benchmarks; direct evaluation on noisy, low-signal financial market data is rare and recent (Graph-Mamba/SAMBA, Mamba-augmented Transformers, MambaLLM), and those few financial studies report only modest, dataset-specific gains — a key caveat for any market-forecasting claim.

- **A Time Series is Worth 64 Words: Long-term Forecasting with Transformers (PatchTST)** — Yuqi Nie, Nam H. Nguyen, Phanwadee Sinthong, Jayant Kalagnanam (2022, *ICLR 2023 (arXiv:2211.14730)*, cites 544) [method-transfer, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.48550/arXiv.2211.14730` · arXiv `2211.14730` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.14730)
  Establishes patch tokenization (the direct analogue of LLM/ViT subword/patch tokens) plus channel-independence as the decisive design that made Transformers competitive again for long-horizon forecasting. Canonical method-transfer reference for 'patching'.
- **iTransformer: Inverted Transformers Are Effective for Time Series Forecasting** — Yong Liu, Tengge Hu, Haoran Zhang, Haixu Wu, Shiyu Wang, Lintao Ma, Mingsheng Long (2023, *ICLR 2024 (arXiv:2310.06625)*, cites 364) [method-transfer, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.48550/arXiv.2310.06625` · arXiv `2310.06625` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.06625)
  Shows that simply re-purposing vanilla attention onto the variate dimension (inverted attention) yields SOTA, reframing what attention should model in TS. A pure architectural-reinterpretation transfer from the standard Transformer.
- **Mamba: Linear-Time Sequence Modeling with Selective State Spaces** — Albert Gu, Tri Dao (2023, *COLM 2024 (arXiv:2312.00752)*, cites 1003) [ts-foundation, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.48550/arXiv.2312.00752` · arXiv `2312.00752` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.00752)
  Foundational state-space-model alternative to attention; the selective-SSM that the entire Mamba-for-time-series literature builds on. Establishes SSMs as a credible linear-time Transformer replacement.
- **Efficiently Modeling Long Sequences with Structured State Spaces (S4)** — Albert Gu, Karan Goel, Christopher Re (2021, *ICLR 2022 (arXiv:2111.00396)*, cites 492) [ts-foundation, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.48550/arXiv.2111.00396` · arXiv `2111.00396` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.00396)
  The deep-SSM ancestor of Mamba; mathematically grounds long-range dependency capture in linear-time recurrences. Establishes the S4 lineage the topic asks for.
- **RoFormer: Enhanced Transformer with Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE)** — Jianlin Su, Yu Lu, Shengfeng Pan, Ahmed Murtadha, Bo Wen, Yunfeng Liu (2021, *Neurocomputing (2023); arXiv:2104.09864*, cites 1348) [method-transfer, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.1016/j.neucom.2023.127063` · arXiv `2104.09864` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.09864)
  Canonical relative/rotary positional encoding now widely transplanted into time-series Transformers to handle ordering and length generalization. Core 'positional encodings (RoPE)' reference.

### Training and representation paradigms transferable from LLMs to time series (self-supervised/masked pre-training, contrastive learning, in-context/zero-shot, tokenisation/quantisation, scaling laws, mixture-of-experts, distillation)

The field has converged on importing the LLM playbook into time series along four axes: (1) self-supervised pre-training, split between masked reconstruction (TST, SimMTM, PatchTST, MOMENT) and contrastive representation learning (Franceschi triplet loss, TS2Vec, TS-TCC, TF-C); (2) tokenisation/quantisation of continuous series so transformer LMs can be applied via next-token prediction (Chronos, LLMTime, patching in PatchTST/TimesFM); (3) generative foundation models trained for zero-shot forecasting on huge corpora (TimeGPT, Chronos, TimesFM, Lag-Llama, Moirai, plus Moirai-MoE for mixture-of-experts), with early evidence of LLM-like scaling laws (Edwards et al.); and (4) direct reuse of frozen text LLMs via reprogramming or adapter fine-tuning (GPT4TS/One-Fits-All, Time-LLM, LLMTime). Crucially, the base truth is contested: the strongest refutation (Tan et al., NeurIPS 2024) shows that ablating or replacing the LLM in three popular LLM-for-TS methods does not hurt and often improves accuracy, and pretrained LLMs do not beat from-scratch models, suggesting the gains attributed to language-knowledge transfer are largely illusory. Financial market data is conspicuously underrepresented: most canonical work evaluates on energy/traffic/weather/UCR-UEA benchmarks, and where finance is included it is one slice of a broad public corpus rather than a focused, regime-shift-aware evaluation, which is itself a caution for a financial-forecasting review.

### Generative and retrieval methods bordering the LLM world for financial time series: diffusion models, GAN/diffusion market-data generation and augmentation, retrieval-augmented forecasting, and their evaluation pitfalls (mode collapse, leakage, unrealistic backtests).

This corpus covers three adjacent threads. (1) The diffusion lineage for time series — TimeGrad, CSDI, SSSD, D3VAE, TSDiff and Latent Diffusion Transformer — established score/diffusion models as state-of-the-art probabilistic forecasters and synthetic-data generators, but almost all canonical work is benchmarked on energy/traffic/weather data, not markets, so financial transfer is asserted more than demonstrated. (2) Generative market-data and augmentation work (Quant GANs, Takahashi-GAN, Fin-GAN, Market-GAN, and the recent LOB diffusion model "Painting the Market") shows GANs/diffusion can reproduce stylized facts (heavy tails, volatility clustering, leverage effect) and even yield positive Sharpe in narrow tasks — yet a systematic comparison by Caulfield & Gleeson tempers these claims, and these papers are precisely where mode collapse and fidelity-vs-diversity trade-offs bite. (3) Retrieval-augmented forecasting (RATD, RAF, RAFT, and the financial RAG-LLM FinSrag) is a fast-moving 2024-2026 frontier that ports RAG from the LLM world into forecasting, with the financial variant being the most leakage-sensitive. Two methodological cornerstones — Bailey/Borwein/López de Prado on backtest overfitting and Kapoor & Narayanan on data leakage and the ML reproducibility crisis — are the base-truth lens against which every generative/retrieval claim of "realistic data" or "improved returns" must be adjudicated, since look-ahead leakage and backtest overfitting are the dominant failure modes here.

  Canonical first diffusion model for multivariate probabilistic forecasting; couples an RNN-encoded conditioning with a per-step denoising diffusion head to produce calibrated predictive distributions. - **Painting the market: generative diffusion models for financial limit order book simulation and forecasting** — Alfred Backhouse, Kang Li, Jakob Foerster, Anisoara Calinescu, Stefan Zohren (2025, *arXiv (q-fin.TR/cs.LG)*, cites 0) [generative, on-fin-data] — DOI `10.48550/arXiv.2509.05107` · arXiv `2509.05107` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.05107)

- **Retrieval-Augmented Diffusion Models for Time Series Forecasting (RATD)** — Jingwei Liu, Ling Yang, Hongyan Li, Shenda Hong (2024, *NeurIPS*, cites 1) [method-transfer, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.48550/arXiv.2410.18712` · arXiv `2410.18712` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.18712)

- **Retrieval Augmented Time Series Forecasting (RAF)** — Kutay Tire, Ege Onur Taga, Muhammed Emrullah Ildiz, Samet Oymak (2024, *arXiv (cs.LG)*, cites 2) [method-transfer, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.48550/arXiv.2411.08249` · arXiv `2411.08249` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.08249)

- **Retrieval Augmented Time Series Forecasting (RAFT)** — Sungwon Han, Seungeon Lee, Meeyoung Cha, Sercan O. Arik, Jinsung Yoon (2025, *arXiv (cs.LG)*, cites 1) [method-transfer, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.48550/arXiv.2505.04163` · arXiv `2505.04163` · [link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.04163)
  A simple, non-LLM retrieval scheme: retrieve similar historical windows and feed their future continuations into the forecaster, winning on 86% of comparisons across ten datasets. Shows much of RAG's benefit comes from analogue retrieval, not the LLM.

- **Pseudo-Mathematics and Financial Charlatanism: The Effects of Backtest Overfitting on Out-of-Sample Performance** — David H. Bailey, Jonathan M. - **Leakage and the reproducibility crisis in machine-learning-based science** — Sayash Kapoor, Arvind Narayanan (2023, *Patterns (Cell Press)*, cites 663) [context, not-fin-data] — DOI `10.1016/j.patter.2023.100804` · [link](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.patter.2023.100804)
  Documents that data leakage is pervasive across 17 scientific fields, provides an eight-type leakage taxonomy, and shows leakage produces over-optimistic, non-replicable results, proposing model info sheets as a remedy. The definitive base-truth reference for leakage as a systemic failure mode.
