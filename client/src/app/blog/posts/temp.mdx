This article presents notes and reflections from a 121-5 laboratory practical focused on characterising genetic constructs in *Escherichia coli* to produce carotenoid pigments such as Î²-carotene and lycopene using genetic parts freely available in the Registry of Standard Biological Parts. A key secondary goal of this lab was to familiarise students with core molecular biology techniques, including polymerase chain reaction (PCR), electrophoresis, enzymatic digestion, and DNA/pigment extraction[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6][^7][^8].

The experiments revolve around engineering *E. coli* to produce colourful pigments by introducing and modulating specific operons and regulatory parts. By carefully controlling promoters (like pBAD and pT5) and supplementing growth media with appropriate inducers (arabinose and IPTG), the production of carotenoid pigments can be influenced. This investigation helps elucidate how metabolic flux and promoter induction levels affect pigment yield.

Although the practical encountered some challenges such as variable data consistency and experimental errors the activities shed light on the interplay between genetic regulation, metabolic pathways, and environmental conditions. Observations from agarose gels, enzyme digestions, PCR amplifications, and pigment extraction measurements laid a foundation for understanding the conditions under which engineered *E. coli* cells can efficiently produce carotenoids.
