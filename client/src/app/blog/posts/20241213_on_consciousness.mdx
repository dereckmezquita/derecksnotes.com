## The question that eats itself

For thousands of years philosophers have pondered the nature of what we call consciousness. More recently, scientists have begun to probe this phenomenon with imaging, electrodes, and computational models. Some claim it to be a mysterious force that inhabits a physical body; others treat it as an emergent property of complex information systems. Billions of dollars and centuries of thought have been poured into the question. Yet none of it has produced a definitive answer as to what consciousness *is*, let alone how to measure it.

I propose that the reason we have no answer is that we are asking the wrong question. *Consciousness* does not exist as a discrete phenomenon. It is not a thing to be found, measured, or explained. It is a label we have applied to the felt experience of a brain modelling its own activity, and the label has led us astray.

This is not a claim that nothing is happening. Brains process information in staggeringly complex ways. The result of that processing feels like something to the system doing the processing. But the moment we reify that feeling into a noun — *consciousness* — and then go looking for it, we have made a category error. We have confused the map for the territory.

The question we should be asking is not *what is consciousness?* but rather: *how does the brain process information, and why does that processing produce the appearance of a unified observer?* These are tractable questions. They have answers that can be tested. The consciousness question, by contrast, is a trap.

## The hard problem is a grammatical accident

In 1995, David Chalmers formalised what he called the *hard problem of consciousness*: why does physical processing give rise to subjective experience at all[^1]? Why doesn't all this neural computation happen "in the dark," without any accompanying feeling?

[^1]: Chalmers DJ. *Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness.* Journal of Consciousness Studies. 1995;2(3):200-219. [Link](https://consc.net/papers/facing.html)

The hard problem has dominated consciousness research for three decades. It has consumed the careers of brilliant people. And I believe it is, at its core, a grammatical accident — a question that *feels* profound because of how language works, not because it points to a genuine gap in nature.

Consider an analogous question: *why is water wet?* We can describe the molecular properties of H₂O, the hydrogen bonding, the surface tension, the way liquid water interacts with other materials. At some point you've explained everything there is to explain about the behaviour of water in contact with surfaces. But someone could still ask: "Yes, but why does it *feel* wet?" — and the question would seem to demand something beyond physics. Yet there is nothing beyond physics here. The "feeling of wetness" is what happens when nerve endings in skin respond to a particular pattern of pressure, temperature, and moisture. There is no residual mystery. The question dissolves once you see that "wetness" is not a property of water molecules; it is a property of a system (skin + water + nerve endings) interacting.

Consciousness is the same kind of thing. It is not a property of neurons. It is not a property of any individual brain region. It is what the interaction of billions of neurons in a particular configuration *does*. Asking "why does it feel like something?" is like asking "why is a murmuration of starlings shapely?" — the shape IS the flock behaviour. There is no additional "shape essence" to explain.

No single starling is a murmuration. No single water molecule is wet. No single neuron is conscious. These are properties that exist only at the level of collective interaction. They are real, causally potent, and measurable — but they are not things. They are processes.

## Why the question persists: biology, not philosophy

If the hard problem is ill-posed, why does it feel so compelling? The answer, I believe, is biological rather than philosophical.

The human brain evolved to model other agents. Theory of mind — the ability to predict what another creature will do by attributing beliefs, desires, and intentions to it — is one of our most powerful cognitive adaptations[^2]. We are so good at it that we attribute agency to storms, to rivers, to stock markets. We see faces in clouds and intentions in algorithms.

[^2]: Premack D, Woodruff G. *Does the chimpanzee have a theory of mind?* Behavioral and Brain Sciences. 1978;1(4):515-526. [Link](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X00076512)

When this agent-modelling machinery turns inward, it produces the sensation of a self — a unified observer sitting behind the eyes, watching the show. This is useful. An organism that models itself as a coherent agent makes faster decisions than one that accurately represents itself as a committee of competing subsystems. Natural selection did not optimise for accurate self-knowledge; it optimised for survival[^3].

[^3]: Graziano MSA. *Consciousness and the Social Brain.* Oxford University Press. 2013.

The "hard problem" is what happens when this self-model encounters its own limits. The brain asks "what am I?" and the agent-modelling system, which was designed to model *other* agents, produces a confused answer: *you are a thing that experiences things*. It reifies the process into an entity. And then philosophers spend centuries trying to find the entity.

Michael Graziano's *attention schema theory* formalises this idea: the brain constructs a simplified model of its own attention processes, and that model is what we experience as consciousness[^4]. The model is useful but inaccurate — it attributes to itself properties (unity, continuity, ineffability) that the underlying process does not actually have. This is not a bug; it is the design specification.

[^4]: Graziano MSA, Webb TW. *The attention schema theory: a mechanistic account of subjective awareness.* Frontiers in Psychology. 2015;6:500. [Link](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2015.00500)

## What the neuroscience actually shows

If we set aside the consciousness question and look at what neuroscience has actually discovered about the brain's information processing, the picture is both clearer and more interesting.

### The adversarial collaboration

In April 2025, Nature published the results of a seven-year adversarial collaboration testing the two leading theories of consciousness: Integrated Information Theory (IIT) and Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT)[^5]. The study involved 256 participants, three measurement methods (fMRI, MEG, intracranial EEG), and over 40 co-authors including Christof Koch and Stanislas Dehaene.

[^5]: Melloni L, et al. *An adversarial collaboration to critically evaluate theories of consciousness.* Nature. 2025. [Link](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-08888-1)

Neither theory won.

IIT predicted that conscious content would be represented in posterior cortex — this was confirmed. But the sustained synchronisation *within* posterior cortex that IIT requires was absent. GNWT predicted prefrontal "ignition" — a sudden broadcast of information across a global workspace centred on frontal cortex. This did not happen cleanly either; prefrontal representation of conscious content was weaker than predicted, and the expected ignition at stimulus offset was missing.

The result is telling. Not because it falsifies either theory, but because it demonstrates that seven years of sophisticated experimentation designed to answer the consciousness question produced a shrug. The data showed that information about visual stimuli was processed across visual, temporal, and frontal cortex in a distributed fashion. This is interesting neuroscience. It tells us something about how the brain handles visual information. But it tells us nothing about "consciousness" per se, because the concept is too vague to be tested.

### The default mode network

The default mode network (DMN) — a set of brain regions active during rest, self-reflection, and mind-wandering — has been proposed as the "core of consciousness"[^6]. Its architecture is illuminating: a 2024 Nature Neuroscience paper mapped the DMN in detail and found that its core is relatively insulated from direct sensory input[^7]. It maintains an internal model of the self and world independent of what is currently happening outside.

[^6]: Luppi AI, Lyu D, Stamatakis EA. *The default mode network as the core of consciousness.* Current Opinion in Behavioral Sciences. 2025. [Link](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352154625000439)

[^7]: Paquola C, et al. *The architecture of the default mode network.* Nature Neuroscience. 2024. [Link](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41593-024-01868-0)

This is exactly what you would expect if the brain maintains a running self-model for adaptive purposes. The DMN is the machinery that produces the *narrative* of selfhood. It is not evidence of consciousness as a fundamental property; it is the mechanism by which the illusion is generated. Study the DMN, study its dynamics, study how it breaks down in psychosis or meditation — these are productive research questions. They do not require the word "consciousness" at any point.

### Psychedelics as causal evidence

Perhaps the most compelling evidence that subjective experience is a product of specific network dynamics — not a separate phenomenon — comes from psychedelic research. A 2025 Cell paper demonstrated that a single dose of psilocybin triggers activity-dependent rewiring of cortical networks, with new dendritic spines persisting for at least one month[^8].

[^8]: Bhatt D, et al. *Psilocybin triggers activity-dependent rewiring of cortical networks.* Cell. 2025. [Link](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(25)01305-4)

Psilocybin reduces functional connectivity *within* the DMN while increasing connectivity *between* normally segregated networks[^9]. This DMN dissolution correlates directly with the subjective experience of ego dissolution — the feeling of self-boundaries dissolving.

[^9]: Carhart-Harris RL, et al. *Neural correlates of the psychedelic state as determined by fMRI studies with psilocybin.* PNAS. 2012;109(6):2138-2143. [Link](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1119598109)

Notice the logic: change the network interactions, change the subjective experience. This is *causal* evidence that what we call consciousness is identical to certain patterns of neural activity — not a separate thing produced by neural activity. If consciousness were a genuine additional property of the universe, pharmacologically disrupting network topology should not predictably and reproducibly alter its character. But it does. Every time.

Robin Carhart-Harris's entropic brain hypothesis frames this elegantly: psychedelics increase brain entropy — the repertoire of available neural states — producing richer, more fluid experience[^10]. Reduced entropy characterises rigid states like depression and addiction. This is a mechanistic account of how subjective experience varies. No appeal to consciousness as a separate entity is needed.

[^10]: Carhart-Harris RL. *The entropic brain – revisited.* Neuropharmacology. 2018;142:167-178. [Link](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuropharm.2018.03.010)

## The consciousness question has produced zero actionable science

This is the claim I want to make most forcefully: decades of consciousness research have not produced a single medical treatment, a single engineering breakthrough, or a single useful prediction.

Compare this to what neuroscience has achieved by *ignoring* consciousness entirely: SSRIs for depression, deep brain stimulation for Parkinson's disease, cochlear implants for deafness, brain-computer interfaces for paralysis. All of these came from studying specific circuits, specific neurotransmitters, specific pathologies. None of them required solving — or even addressing — the hard problem.

The adversarial collaboration spent seven years, millions of dollars, and the efforts of over 40 researchers to conclude that the data are "more nuanced" than either theory predicted. What was gained? Interesting observations about visual processing — which could have been obtained without framing the study around consciousness at all.

Meanwhile, researchers who focus on specific mechanisms — cortical plasticity, network dynamics, predictive coding — make concrete, replicable progress. Karl Friston's free energy principle provides a mathematical framework for understanding how brains maintain homeostasis and learn, without needing the word "consciousness" once[^11]. It has generated testable predictions and practical applications in AI.

[^11]: Friston K. *The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?* Nature Reviews Neuroscience. 2010;11(2):127-138. [Link](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn2787)

The consciousness question is not just unproductive; it actively misdirects resources and attention. Every dollar spent debating whether IIT's Phi value correlates with subjective experience is a dollar not spent on understanding specific neural pathologies.

## AI research suffers from the question too

The same distraction operates in artificial intelligence research. A 2025 paper in Trends in Cognitive Sciences, co-authored by David Chalmers and Yoshua Bengio among others, proposed 14 theory-based indicators for assessing whether an AI system might be conscious[^12]. The framework is intellectually interesting. It has zero practical applications.

[^12]: Butlin P, Long R, et al. *Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness.* Trends in Cognitive Sciences. 2025. [Link](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2025.11.005)

No AI system has ever been improved by asking whether it is conscious. The relevant questions for AI are: what can this system do? How does it fail? Can it be made safer? Can it be made more capable? Can it be aligned with human values? These are engineering questions with testable answers. The consciousness question adds nothing.

Worse, it risks anthropomorphic confusion. When people believe an LLM might be conscious, they are more likely to attribute rights to it, more likely to trust its outputs uncritically, more likely to project emotional states onto a statistical pattern-matching engine[^13]. This is not just a philosophical curiosity; it has policy implications. Resources that should be spent on AI safety and interpretability are diverted to debates about machine sentience.

[^13]: Colombatto C, Fleming SM. *Folk psychological attributions of consciousness to large language models.* Neuroscience of Consciousness. 2024;1:niae013. [Link](https://doi.org/10.1093/nc/niae013)

Multiple analyses using IIT's framework have concluded that transformer architectures — the basis of all current large language models — cannot be conscious because they lack the recurrent, integrated causal structure that IIT requires[^14]. Once a token is generated, internal state resets. There is no temporal continuity, no deeply entangled cause-effect structure. This is the right kind of analysis: specific, mechanistic, testable. It happens to use IIT's formalism, but its value lies in the engineering insight about transformer architecture, not in any claim about consciousness.

[^14]: Kraus M, et al. *LLMs cannot possess consciousness.* Cognitive Systems Research. 2025. [Link](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2949719125000391)

## The unfalsifiability problem

Every proposed measure of consciousness ultimately bottoms out at an assumption. IIT's Phi is computed from causal integration, but the claim that Phi corresponds to subjective experience is an axiom, not a finding[^15]. GNWT identifies global broadcast, but the claim that broadcast *is* consciousness is stipulated, not derived. Predictive processing describes how hierarchical inference generates self-models, but the claim that self-models *feel like something* is an article of faith.

[^15]: Merker B, Williford K, Stevens G. *Integrated Information Theory does not satisfy its critics.* Neuroscience of Consciousness. 2022;8(1):niac007. [Link](https://doi.org/10.1093/nc/niac007)

In 2023, approximately 120 researchers signed an open letter calling IIT "pseudoscience" on precisely these grounds — that its core claim is unfalsifiable[^16]. IIT's proponents responded by listing 16 peer-reviewed empirical tests. But the tests measure information integration, not consciousness. They measure a correlate and assume it is the thing. This is the fundamental problem with all consciousness research: you cannot close the gap between third-person measurement and first-person experience without assuming what you are trying to prove.

[^16]: Fleming S, et al. *The Integrated Information Theory of Consciousness is Pseudoscience.* 2023. Signed open letter.

The hard problem is not hard. It is *undefined*. It is like asking "why is there something rather than nothing?" — a question that feels profound but may simply be a grammatically valid sentence with no answer. We should stop treating it as a research programme.

## What we should study instead

I am not arguing that we should stop studying the brain, or stop studying subjective reports, or stop building theories of neural information processing. I am arguing that the word "consciousness" should be retired from scientific discourse, the way "élan vital" was retired from biology once we understood biochemistry.

Here are the questions that actually produce knowledge:

1. **How does the brain construct a self-model?** The DMN, attention schema theory, and predictive processing all offer tractable approaches to this question. Study the mechanisms. Do not ask whether the self-model is "conscious."

2. **How do specific network dynamics produce specific cognitive states?** Psychedelic research demonstrates causal links between network topology and subjective reports. Map these links. Do not invoke consciousness as an intermediary.

3. **How do disorders of neural processing alter behaviour and self-report?** Coma, vegetative states, locked-in syndrome, anaesthesia — these are clinical categories defined by *responsiveness* and *information processing capacity*, not by consciousness. The useful clinical question is "can this brain process and respond to stimuli?" — not "is there something it is like to be this patient?"

4. **How do artificial systems process information, and where do they fail?** For AI, the productive questions are about capability, alignment, and safety. Not about machine sentience.

5. **How does the brain maintain a coherent narrative across time?** Memory consolidation, predictive coding, temporal binding — these are the mechanisms that produce the *appearance* of a continuous experiencer. Study them directly.

Each of these questions can be addressed with standard neuroscientific methods. None of them require solving — or even posing — the hard problem.

## Objections and responses

### "But I experience things!"

Yes. The experience is real in the same way wetness is real. When you put your hand in water, the feeling of wetness is a genuine sensory event produced by specific neural processes. But no individual water molecule is wet. Wetness is a macro-level property of a system interacting. Similarly, when your brain processes information in certain ways, the result feels like something. The feeling IS the process, viewed from the inside. There is no residual mystery.

### "If consciousness doesn't matter, does suffering matter?"

Yes. Pain and suffering are specific neural and behavioural phenomena that can be studied and addressed without invoking consciousness. We do not need to solve the hard problem to treat someone's chronic pain, to develop better anaesthetics, or to recognise when an animal is in distress. These are questions about specific neural circuits and their outputs. The word "consciousness" adds nothing to the clinical picture.

### "Aren't you just redefining the problem away?"

No. I am arguing that the "problem" was never well-defined in the first place. When we had no understanding of biochemistry, people posited a *vital force* — élan vital — that distinguished living matter from dead matter. The question "what is the vital force?" seemed profound and important. It turned out to be a category error. Life is what certain arrangements of matter *do*, not what they *are*. The vital force was retired, and biology flourished. I am making the same argument for consciousness.

### "What about the clinical need to assess awareness?"

Disorders of consciousness are real clinical challenges. But the useful question is always operational: does this patient respond to commands? Does their brain show differentiated responses to speech? Can information propagate across their cortical networks? Marcello Massimini's perturbational complexity index (PCI) — which measures the brain's response complexity after transcranial magnetic stimulation — is the most promising clinical tool for assessing awareness in unresponsive patients[^17]. It works by measuring *information processing capacity*, not consciousness. The word is unnecessary.

[^17]: Casali AG, et al. *A theoretically based index of consciousness independent of sensory processing and behavior.* Science Translational Medicine. 2013;5(198):198ra105. [Link](https://doi.org/10.1126/scitranslmed.3006294)

## Temperature, wetness, and the self

I want to close with an analogy that I think captures the correct way to think about what we have been calling consciousness.

No single gas molecule has a temperature. Temperature is the average kinetic energy of a population of molecules. It is statistically meaningless for one particle. Yet temperature is as real as anything — it burns you, it drives engines, it shapes weather. It is a macro-level property of a system, not a property of any component.

No single water molecule is wet. Wetness is what happens when many water molecules interact with a surface. It is real, measurable, consequential — but it does not exist at the level of individual molecules.

No single neuron is conscious. What we call consciousness is what happens when billions of neurons interact in particular configurations. It is real in the sense that the brain's self-model has genuine causal power — it drives decisions, shapes behaviour, generates the narrative of a life. But it does not exist as a separate thing. It is a process, not a substance.

We already accept that temperature, life, wetness, and economic value are real without being fundamental. They are patterns of interaction that have genuine causal power. The mistake is not thinking that subjective experience exists — it is thinking that it is a *thing* rather than a *process*. It exists the way a wave exists: not as a substance, but as a pattern propagating through a medium.

The brain asks "what am I?" and its agent-modelling subsystem, which was designed to model *other* agents, produces the best answer it can: *you are a someone who experiences things*. This answer is useful. It is also wrong. There is no someone. There is only the processing, and the processing includes a model of itself. The model is what we call consciousness. And the model, like all models, is a simplification — a useful fiction.

We should study the fiction-making machinery. We should stop looking for the fiction's protagonist.
