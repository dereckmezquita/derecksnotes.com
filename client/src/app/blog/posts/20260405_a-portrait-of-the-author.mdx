## Why This Post Exists

This is a portrait -- not in graphite or india ink, but in words. A timestamp. A fixed point in the trajectory of a person who has spent years writing about everything from the entropy of the universe to the proper way to prepare a Petri dish, from the ethics of synthetic biology to the art of centering a div. If you have read enough of what I have written, you know me already. But I wanted to make the implicit explicit -- to sit down and say: this is who Dereck is, as of now, captured in the same medium he has always used to think.

## The Thinker at the Table

The most characteristic thing about my intellectual life is that it happens in conversation. Nearly every philosophical post I have ever written begins the same way: "I had an interesting conversation with Guy the other day," or "I was speaking with Emmanuel," or "Daniel asked me about..." I do not arrive at ideas in isolation. I arrive at them across a table, in argument, in the friction between two minds that disagree.

This is not a weakness. It is how I think. I need the resistance of another perspective to sharpen my own. When Guy told me there was no reason to fear death, I did not simply agree -- I admitted that I feared it, sat with his argument, and then reconsidered. When Emmanuel presented his optimistic view of life, I pushed back with entropy and thermodynamics, not out of contrarianism but because I genuinely believe that life goes against the grain of the universe. And when Guy later asked me "Why be objective at all?" -- a question I had no answer to -- I did not fake one. I said I needed to think, and then I thought, and eventually I built an argument from evolutionary cooperation and the avoidance of suffering.

This is the pattern: someone poses a question I cannot immediately answer, and I chase it. Not to win, but to understand.

I am, by tendency, a pessimist. I believe that biology is an act of defiance against entropy -- noble, perhaps, but ultimately doomed. The universe tends towards disorder; life demands order; therefore life is suffering. I arrived at this conclusion through physics and cell biology, not through mood. It is a structural observation, not a complaint. And yet I do not stop there. I argue for trying anyway. I argue for objectivity, for shooting high, for doing something meaningful with the short time we have. If there is a contradiction in holding both positions simultaneously -- that life is suffering and that one should try to enjoy it -- then I am comfortable with that contradiction. Most honest positions contain one.

## The Scientist in the Lab

I am a biologist by training. I have built fluorescent protein constructs and watched them glow under the microscope at two in the morning because I was too excited to wait until the next day. I have extracted DNA, run PCR, poured electrophoresis gels, transformed bacteria, conjugated plasmids, and engineered *E. coli* to produce carotenoid pigments. I have dissected human bodies and examined their internal structures extensively. I have looked at mites from my own bed under a microscope and drawn what I saw with a Namiki Falcon fountain pen.

The lab taught me something that philosophy alone could not: that rigour is not the enemy of wonder. Quite the opposite. The more precisely you understand a process -- the exact temperature for heat shock, the right concentration of antibiotic, the reason you never wear gloves near a Bunsen burner -- the more you appreciate the elegance of what you are manipulating. A cell is not less beautiful for being understood; it is more so.

This carries into everything I do. When I write about the Earth Biogenome Project or gene doping or the ethics of CRISPR, I am not writing as a spectator. I have held the pipette. I know what it smells like when you autoclave LB medium. The science is not abstract to me; it is tactile, procedural, real.

## The Builder

I built this website from scratch. Not with a template, not with a CMS -- from code. I write my blog posts in MDX, manage my own servers, configure my own DNS records, set up my own mail server with Postfix and OpenDKIM. When I needed to send emails from Node.js, I did not reach for a third-party service; I requested SMTP restrictions lifted from my hosting provider and configured the whole stack myself.

This is a pattern that extends beyond programming. I build things. I built a comprehensive statistics course. I wrote over a thousand dictionary definitions spanning biology, chemistry, and mathematics. I compiled biographies of twenty-one scientists and mathematicians -- not summaries, but detailed accounts with full mathematical derivations, from Einstein's mass-energy equivalence to Shannon's information entropy to Bohr's model of the hydrogen atom. I wrote seventeen wet lab protocols from my own experience. I created interactive fractal visualisations -- Golden Spirals, Sierpinski Triangles, Koch Snowflakes, Barnsley Ferns -- and embedded them directly into blog posts as live JavaScript canvases.

I do not build these things because someone asked me to. I build them because the act of constructing something from first principles is how I learn. If I cannot build it, I do not understand it. And if I understand it, I want to make it available to others in a form that is clear, precise, and complete.

## The Aesthetic

I shoot film. Minolta X700, Minolta XD7 -- analog cameras from the 1980s. I have walked through Paris, Dublin, London, Nantes, Angers, and Moscow with a roll of film and no screen to review what I have captured. There is something about the constraint of film -- the finite number of exposures, the impossibility of immediate review, the delay between the act and the result -- that appeals to me. It forces deliberation. Every frame costs something.

I draw. Architecture studies in Faber Castel india ink markers. Portraits in BIC ballpoint on scrap paper. Scientific observations in black fountain pen. Microscopic specimens rendered by hand, the way scientists did before photography existed. I admire this tradition -- the naturalist's notebook, the careful observation translated through the hand into a permanent record.

And I hold strong opinions about art. I believe that technical mastery must precede abstraction. Picasso earned the right to simplify because he could first draw with extraordinary precision. Dali could paint the surreal because he had first mastered the real. An abstract painting by someone who cannot draw a hand is not daring; it is evasion. Classical art, as I have written before, "kicks ass" -- because it demanded skill and showed it off proudly.

This is not conservatism for its own sake. It is a belief that craft matters. That the foundation must be solid before you experiment with the structure. This principle applies equally to art, to science, and to code.

## The Voice

If you have read this far, you have already experienced my voice. But let me make it explicit.

I write in British English -- honour, behaviour, recognised -- despite being raised in the Americas. This is partly the influence of having studied in France, where British English is the standard taught, and partly preference. I find it more... considered.

I use "one" as an impersonal pronoun where most people would use "you." "One should try to enjoy life." "One must take into account." This is not affectation; it is precision. "You" implies the reader specifically; "one" implies anyone, universally. When I am making a general claim, I want the language to reflect that generality.

I structure my thinking around conversations. My philosophical posts are dialogues -- not in the formal Platonic sense, but in the sense that they record the process of two people working through an idea together. I attribute insights generously. When Guy gives me an argument that changes my thinking, I say so. When Emmanuel challenges my pessimism, I present his view faithfully before responding. I paraphrase, and I admit that I am paraphrasing.

I am honest about what I do not know. "I did not know how to answer and had to think for quiet a while." "After further reflection I do not think I continue to fear my death. I will have to reflect further upon this." These are not rhetorical hedges; they are genuine admissions. I would rather say "I do not know" than pretend to certainty I have not earned.

I can also be blunt. "Fucking bullshit CSS, always forgetting how to centre shit." This is not inconsistency; it is range. The same person who writes carefully about entropy and the human condition also gets frustrated by `margin: 0 auto` and says so without pretence. I do not maintain a single register. I write formally when the subject demands it and informally when it does not. The content dictates the tone, not the other way around.

My blurbs -- the short lines beneath each post title -- are deliberately cryptic. "Golden gray walls." "Big pipe to small pipe to big pipe." "Believe in Magic." These are not descriptions of the posts. They are fragments -- images, phrases, moods that were in my mind at the time of writing. They are a private joke with myself, a small act of poetry in an otherwise rigorous space.

I use footnotes. I cite sources. Even in personal reflections, I link to my own previous posts when they are relevant. This is not pedantry; it is the habit of a scientist. Ideas do not exist in isolation. They build on prior work -- including my own.

## The Technician and the Engineer

I am not a computer scientist by training. I am a biologist who taught himself to code because he needed tools that did not exist yet, or because the ones that did exist were not built the way he wanted. This is an important distinction. I do not approach technology as an end in itself; I approach it as a means of building things that serve a purpose -- and then I go deep enough that the means becomes a craft of its own.

I write R the way I write lab protocols: with `box::use` imports, `data.table` for everything, two-space indentation, and C-style control flow. I do not use the tidyverse. I do not use `library()`. These are not arbitrary preferences; they are decisions made after understanding the alternatives and choosing the one that offers the most control and transparency. When I needed async programming in R -- a language that was not designed for it -- I figured it out by mapping the concepts from JavaScript, where I already understood them, and wrote a tutorial translating between the two paradigms.

My technical range is broad but not shallow. I have configured Postfix and OpenDKIM from scratch to send authenticated email. I have set up Raspberry Pis headless over SSH. I have written my own DNS records, SPF policies, and DMARC configurations. I build in Next.js, write MDX components, manage my own infrastructure. I once wrote an entire interactive periodic table in raw HTML. When I need a fractal rendered in the browser, I write the JavaScript myself -- the Golden Spiral from its polar coordinate equations, the Barnsley Fern from its four affine transformations with their probability weights.

The pattern is always the same: I do not use a library when I can write the thing myself and understand what it does. I do not abstract until I have built the concrete version first. This is the same principle I apply to art -- master the fundamentals before you simplify -- applied to engineering.

My scientific interests sit at the intersection of biology and computation. I trained in synthetic biology and microbiology -- genetic engineering, metabolic pathway design, lambda-red recombination, InFusion cloning. My master's programme covered everything from rational protein design to nanobiology to mathematical modelling of biological systems. But the trajectory has moved increasingly towards bioinformatics and data science: RNA-seq pipelines, variant calling, single-cell analysis, metagenomics workflows. I wrote a comprehensive bioinformatics cheat sheet that covers nine complete analysis pipelines not as an academic exercise but because I needed the reference for my own work.

What ties the wet lab and the dry lab together is the same impulse: I want to understand biological systems at the level of mechanism, and I will use whatever tools -- pipette or Python, gel or genome browser -- get me closer to that understanding.

I am drawn to the scientists who bridged theory and practice. The biographies I chose to write are telling: Leeuwenhoek, who ground his own lenses and discovered bacteria. Hooke, who built microscopes and coined the word "cell." Newton, who invented calculus because he needed it. Shannon, who formalised information theory while building circuits. These are people who did not separate thinking from making. That is the tradition I see myself in -- not as their equal, but as someone who understands why they worked the way they did.

## The Character and the Ethics

I believe in earning things. This is perhaps the deepest thread in everything I do. Mastery must precede abstraction. Understanding must precede opinion. Construction must precede critique. I do not trust knowledge that has not been tested by building something with it. I do not trust art that has not been preceded by craft. I do not trust authority that has not been questioned.

This makes me sceptical by default -- but not cynical. I questioned authority after watching a documentary about fraud in the art world and realised I had been accepting museum displays uncritically. But I also recognised the practical limit: "How much time and energy can one spend questioning everything? How much should one question?" I did not resolve this tension by choosing a side. I held it openly. I distrust authority, and I also acknowledge that at some point I must trust some authorities. The question of which ones remains open.

I attribute ideas to the people who gave them to me. When Guy changes my thinking about death, I say "this insight was mainly by Guy." When I paraphrase, I say I am paraphrasing. When I add to someone else's argument, I say "I've even added some ideas to the central thesis." This is not mere citation practice; it is a moral position. Ideas have origins, and pretending otherwise is a kind of theft.

I am honest about my limitations and fears. I admitted to fearing death in a public post. I admitted to having difficulty concentrating. I wrote "I did not know how to answer" without embarrassment. In a culture that rewards the appearance of certainty, this is a deliberate choice. I would rather be seen as uncertain and honest than confident and wrong.

I am practical in a way that can look contradictory. I write about the heat death of the universe and then sell my furniture on the internet in French, accepting cryptocurrency. I build reference materials that no one asked for. I keep natural history posters for five years and then part with them to a named individual, recording the transaction in a blog post. I do not discard things carelessly, and I do not hold on to them sentimentally. There is a logic to what I keep and what I let go; it is just not always visible from the outside.

I think cooperation matters. My argument for objectivity -- that social creatures must find shared reference points to resolve conflict without violence -- is not just an intellectual exercise. It reflects a genuine belief that finding common ground is both practically necessary and morally important. I arrived at this position through evolutionary reasoning, not through sentiment, but the conclusion is the same: we are better together, and objectivity is the mechanism that makes that possible.

I am bilingual and bicultural in a way that shows up in small details rather than grand statements. I write in British English but grew up in the Americas. I sell furniture in French. My lab protocols carry traces of French phrasing. I have lived in Angers, Paris, Dublin, London, Nantes, and Moscow. I do not make a spectacle of this; it is simply the texture of a life lived across borders.

## The Psychology

I am systematic. I name my files with timestamps: `YEARMONTHDATE_name-of-file`. I organise my folders into logical hierarchies. I flag my academic emails with yellow and change them to green once I have truly consumed the contents -- not merely opened them. I keep reminders, calendars, and notes synchronised across devices. I believe that ten minutes of organisation can save hours of searching, and I practise what I preach.

But I am not rigid. The system exists to serve the work, not the other way around. I will dissect a human body in the morning and draw it with a fountain pen in the afternoon. I will write about the heat death of the universe and then sell my furniture on the internet -- in French, accepting Bitcoin, Monero, and DigiByte.

I am curious without being dilettantish. When I become interested in something -- DigiByte's consensus mechanism, the oligodynamic effect of copper, Japanese archery, the history of alchemy -- I go deep. I read the primary sources. I visit the Royal Society Archives. I derive the equations. I do not skim; I understand or I move on.

I value independence. I built my own infrastructure rather than relying on platforms. I question authority as a matter of principle -- "do not trust any authority, and always question everything; this is what it means to be a scientist" -- while acknowledging the practical impossibility of questioning everything. This tension -- between the ideal of total scepticism and the necessity of trusting something -- is one I have not resolved, and I am honest about that too.

I am someone who keeps things. Photo dumps. Notebook dumps. Natural history posters from the mid-1800s that I owned for five years before parting with them. Alchemy images. Microscope drawings. I archive not out of nostalgia but out of respect for the record. Things that existed should be preserved. Observations that were made should be documented. The act of recording is itself a small rebellion against entropy.

## The Timestamp

This is who I am in April 2026. A biologist who codes. A programmer who draws. A pessimist who tries anyway. A scientist who sits at tables with friends and argues about whether randomness exists, whether objectivity is a biological imperative, whether life is suffering or merely difficult. Someone who believes that mastery precedes innovation, that craft matters, that the foundation must be solid. Someone who names his files with dates and writes his notes in extended markdown and shoots film on forty-year-old cameras.

I do not know who I will be in ten years. But I know that whoever reads this then will find the thread -- the same insistence on understanding from first principles, the same respect for rigour and craft, the same willingness to sit with a question until an answer presents itself or to admit that none has.

The universe tends towards disorder. I tend towards making sense of it. That is the whole of it, really.
