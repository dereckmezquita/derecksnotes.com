• Atom - The basic unit of matter that makes up a chemical element. Atoms are composed of a nucleus, which contains protons and neutrons, and electrons that orbit the nucleus. The number of protons in the nucleus determines the atomic number and the identity of the element.
  • Molecule - A molecule is a group of atoms that are bonded together and behave as a single unit. Molecules can be composed of atoms of the same element (e.g. oxygen molecule, O2) or different elements (e.g. water molecule, H2O).
  • Chemical reaction - A chemical reaction is a process that results in the transformation of one set of chemical substances into another. Chemical reactions involve the breaking and forming of chemical bonds, and can be represented by a chemical equation.
  • Chemical equation - A chemical equation is a representation of a chemical reaction using chemical formulas and symbols. It shows the reactants (the substances undergoing the reaction) on the left side of an arrow, and the products (the substances produced by the reaction) on the right side of the arrow. The arrow represents the transformation of reactants into products.
  • Molar mass - The molar mass of a substance is the mass of one mole of that substance, expressed in grams. The mole is a unit of measurement in chemistry that represents the number of atoms or molecules in a substance. The molar mass of a substance can be calculated by adding the atomic masses of all the atoms in the molecule or formula unit of the substance.
  • Acid - An acid is a substance that donates protons (hydrogen ions, H+) to a solution when it is dissolved in water. Acids have a sour taste, and they turn blue litmus paper red. Common examples of acids include hydrochloric acid (HCl) and sulphuric acid (H2SO4).
  • Base - A base is a substance that accepts protons (hydrogen ions, H+) when it is dissolved in water. Bases have a bitter taste, and they turn red litmus paper blue. Common examples of bases include sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and potassium hydroxide (KOH).
  • pH - The pH of a solution is a measure of the concentration of hydrogen ions (H+) in the solution. The pH scale ranges from 0 to 14, with 7 being neutral. Solutions with a pH less than 7 are considered acidic, while solutions with a pH greater than 7 are considered basic. The pH of a solution can be calculated using the formula:
    $$ pH = -\log[H^+] $$
    where [H+] is the concentration of hydrogen ions in moles per liter (mol/L).
  • Enthalpy - Enthalpy is a measure of the heat content of a chemical system. It is a state function, meaning that it depends only on the state of the system and not on the path taken to reach that state. The change in enthalpy (ΔH) of a chemical reaction is defined as the heat absorbed or released during the reaction at constant pressure. The change in enthalpy can be calculated using the formula:
    $$ \Delta H = q_p $$
    where qp is the heat absorbed or released at constant pressure.
  • Thermochemistry - Thermochemistry is the study of the heat released or absorbed during chemical reactions. It involves the measurement of changes in enthalpy and the use of thermochemical equations to calculate the heat of reactions. Thermochemistry is useful for predicting the feasibility and direction of chemical reactions, as well as for understanding the energy changes that occur during reactions.
  • Kinetics - Kinetics is the study of the rates of chemical reactions. It involves the determination of the factors that affect the rates of reactions, such as the concentration of reactants, temperature, and the presence of a catalyst. Kinetics is useful for understanding the mechanisms of chemical reactions, as well as for predicting the rates of reactions. The rate of a chemical reaction can be expressed using the formula:
    $$ rate = \frac{[A]^m[B]^n}{[C]^p[D]^q} $$
    where [A] and [B] are the concentrations of reactants A and B, and [C] and [D] are the concentrations of products C and D. The exponents m and n are the stoichiometric coefficients of reactants A and B, and p and q are the stoichiometric coefficients of products C and D.
  • Rate law - A rate law is a mathematical expression that describes the relationship between the rate of a chemical reaction and the concentration of reactants. The rate law for a reaction is usually determined experimentally by measuring the rate of the reaction at different concentrations of reactants and fitting the data to a mathematical model. The general form of a rate law is:
    $$ rate = k[A]^m[B]^n $$
    where k is the rate constant, [A] and [B] are the concentrations of reactants A and B, and m and n are the orders of the reaction with respect to reactants A and B, respectively. The order of a reaction with respect to a reactant is the power to which the concentration of the reactant is raised in the rate law.
  • Thermodynamics - Thermodynamics is the study of the relationships between heat, work, and energy. It involves the development of laws that describe the conversion of energy from one form to another, and the prediction of the feasibility and direction of energy conversions. Thermodynamics is based on the concepts of energy, entropy, and temperature, and is used to understand and predict the behavior of systems that exchange energy with their surroundings.
  • Electrochemistry - Electrochemistry is the study of the relationships between electricity and chemical reactions. It involves the measurement of electrical potentials and currents in chemical systems, and the use of these measurements to understand and predict the behavior of electrochemical cells. Electrochemistry is used in a variety of applications, including the production of electricity from chemical reactions (e.g. in batteries), the electroplating of metals, and the analysis of chemical compounds.
  • Electrolyte - An electrolyte is a substance that conducts electricity when it is dissolved in water or melted. Electrolytes are composed of ions, which are atoms or molecules that have gained or lost electrons and have a net positive or negative charge. Electrolytes are important in electrochemistry, as they allow the flow of electric current through a solution. Common examples of electrolytes include salts (e.g. sodium chloride, NaCl) and acids (e.g. hydrochloric acid, HCl).
  • Electrode - An electrode is a conductor through which electric current flows into or out of an electrochemical cell. Electrodes are classified as either anode or cathode, depending on whether they are the site of oxidation or reduction, respectively. The anode is the electrode where oxidation occurs, and the cathode is the electrode where reduction occurs. The voltage of an electrochemical cell is determined by the difference in potential between the anode and cathode.
  • Oxidation - Oxidation is a chemical reaction in which a molecule or atom gains oxygen or loses hydrogen. Oxidation can also involve the loss of electrons, and is often accompanied by an increase in the oxidation state of the molecule or atom. Oxidation is the opposite of reduction, which is the gain of hydrogen or the addition of electrons.
  • Reduction - Reduction is a chemical reaction in which a molecule or atom gains hydrogen or gains electrons. Reduction is the opposite of oxidation, which is the loss of hydrogen or the loss of electrons.
  • Oxidizing agent - An oxidising agent is a chemical species that is able to oxidise other substances by accepting electrons from them. Oxidising agents are often powerful electron acceptors, and they are commonly used in chemical reactions to transfer oxygen or remove hydrogen from other compounds. Examples of common oxidising agents include oxygen (O2), chlorine (Cl2), and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2).
  • Reducing agent - A reducing agent is a chemical species that is able to reduce other substances by donating electrons to them. Reducing agents are often powerful electron donors, and they are commonly used in chemical reactions to transfer hydrogen or remove oxygen from other compounds. Examples of common reducing agents include metals such as zinc (Zn) and aluminium (Al), and hydrides such as sodium hydride (NaH).
  • Stoichiometry - Stoichiometry is the study of the quantitative relationships between the reactants and products in chemical reactions. It involves the use of balanced chemical equations to calculate the amounts of reactants and products required or produced in a reaction, based on the known quantities of other reactants or products. Stoichiometry is a powerful tool for predicting and controlling the outcomes of chemical reactions, and is an important concept in the fields of chemistry and chemical engineering.
  • Chemical equilibrium - Chemical equilibrium is a state in which the rates of the forward and reverse reactions in a chemical system are equal, and the concentrations of the reactants and products remain constant over time. In a chemical equilibrium, the system is in a state of dynamic balance, with the forward and reverse reactions occurring at the same rate. The position of chemical equilibrium can be affected by changes in temperature, pressure, and the concentrations of reactants and products.
  • Proton - A subatomic particle with a charge of +1 and a mass slightly less than a neutron. Protons are found in the nucleus of an atom and are responsible for determining the element that the atom belongs to.

    The number of protons in an atom's nucleus is equal to its atomic number, which is a unique identifier for each element. For example, an atom with 6 protons is a carbon atom, while an atom with 92 protons is a uranium atom.

  • Neutron - A subatomic particle with no charge and a mass slightly greater than a proton. Neutrons are found in the nucleus of an atom and help hold the protons together.

    The number of neutrons in an atom's nucleus can vary, even for atoms of the same element. These variations are known as isotopes, and they are distinguished by their atomic mass, which is the number of protons plus neutrons in the nucleus.

  • Electron - A subatomic particle with a charge of -1 and a mass nearly equal to zero. Electrons are found outside the nucleus of an atom and are responsible for the chemical behaviour of elements.

    The arrangement of electrons in an atom determines its chemical properties. Atoms can gain or lose electrons to form ions, or they can share electrons in covalent bonds to form molecules.

    The energy levels of electrons in an atom are quantized, meaning they can only occupy certain energy levels and not others. The energy levels are represented by shells, and the number of electrons that can occupy a shell is limited by the quantum numbers of the shell.

  • Reagent - A substance used in a chemical reaction to determine the presence or quantity of another substance. Reagents are typically used in analytical chemistry to identify and quantify the presence of specific substances in a sample.

    Reagents can be either acidic or basic, depending on their ability to donate or accept protons. For example, hydrochloric acid (HCl) is a strong acid that donates protons, while sodium hydroxide (NaOH) is a strong base that accepts protons.

    Reagents can also be used in quantitative analysis to determine the concentration of a substance in a sample. This is often done using titration, in which a known concentration of a reagent is added to a sample until a reaction occurs. The point at which the reaction occurs is called the endpoint, and it can be used to calculate the concentration of the substance in the sample.

  • Reactant - A substance involved in a chemical reaction. Reactants are typically consumed during the course of the reaction and are transformed into products.

    The amount of reactants consumed in a chemical reaction is typically represented by the stoichiometric coefficients in the balanced chemical equation for the reaction. For example, the balanced chemical equation for the combustion of methane (CH4) is:

    $$ CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O $$

    In this equation, the reactants are methane (CH4) and oxygen (O2), while the products are carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O).

  • Molecule - A group of atoms held together by chemical bonds. Molecules can be made up of atoms of the same element (e.g. oxygen molecule, O2) or different elements (e.g. water molecule, H2O).

    The properties of a molecule are determined by the types and arrangement of atoms it is made up of, as well as the type of chemical bonds that hold the atoms together. Covalent bonds, for example, are formed when atoms share electrons, while ionic bonds are formed when atoms transfer electrons.

    Molecules can be represented by Lewis structures, which show the atoms and their bonds using dots and lines. For example, the Lewis structure for a water molecule is shown below:

    $$ H_2O = O-H $$

    In this Lewis structure, the oxygen atom (O) is surrounded by two lone pairs of electrons, while the hydrogen atoms (H) are each bonded to the oxygen atom by a single covalent bond.

  • Ion - An atom or molecule that has gained or lost one or more electrons, resulting in a net electrical charge.

    Atoms or molecules that have gained electrons become negatively charged ions, known as anions. Atoms or molecules that have lost electrons become positively charged ions, known as cations.

    The charge of an ion is determined by the number of electrons it has gained or lost. For example, a sodium atom (Na) has 11 protons and 11 electrons, giving it a neutral charge. However, if it loses an electron, it becomes a sodium ion (Na+) with 11 protons and 10 electrons, giving it a positive charge.

    Ions can be formed by chemical reactions, such as the transfer of an electron from one atom to another. They can also be formed by the loss or gain of protons, as in the case of acids and bases.

  • Metal - An element characterized by its ability to conduct electricity and heat, as well as its tendency to lose electrons and form positive ions.

    Metals are found on the left side of the periodic table, and they tend to have relatively low ionization energies and electron affinities. This means that they are relatively easy to remove electrons from, which makes them good conductors of electricity.

    Metals are also known for their metallic bonds, which are formed by the electrostatic attraction between the positive ions in the metal lattice and the delocalized electrons that move freely throughout the lattice. This gives metals their characteristic properties, such as their high melting and boiling points, and their ductility and malleability.

  • Concentration - A measure of the amount of a substance present in a solution, expressed as the ratio of the amount of the substance to the total volume of the solution.

    Concentration is often expressed in terms of molarity, which is the number of moles of a substance per litre of solution. For example, a solution with a concentration of 1 mol/L contains 1 mole of solute (the substance being dissolved) in 1 litre of solvent (the liquid being dissolved in).

    The concentration of a solution can also be expressed in terms of mass, using units such as grams per litre (g/L) or parts per million (ppm). For example, a solution with a concentration of 1 g/L contains 1 gram of solute in 1 litre of solvent.

  • Pressure - A measure of the force per unit area exerted on a surface. Pressure is typically expressed in units of pascals (Pa) or atmospheres (atm).

    Pressure is a scalar quantity, meaning it has only magnitude and no direction. It is the result of the force exerted by a fluid (such as a gas or liquid) on the walls of its container.

    The pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its temperature and inversely proportional to its volume. This relationship is known as the ideal gas law, which is expressed as:

    $$ PV = nRT $$

    In this equation, P is the pressure of the gas, V is its volume, n is the number of moles of gas present, R is the ideal gas constant, and T is the temperature in kelvins.

  • Temperature - A measure of the heat energy of a system. Temperature is typically expressed in units of kelvins (K), degrees Celsius (°C), or degrees Fahrenheit (°F).

    Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a system, and it is directly proportional to the average speed of those particles. As the temperature of a system increases, the average kinetic energy of the particles increases, and their average speed increases.

    The relationship between temperature and kinetic energy is described by the equipartition theorem, which states that the average kinetic energy of a particle is equal to 1/2 kT, where k is the Boltzmann constant and T is the temperature in kelvins. This can be written as:

    $$ \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}kT $$

    In this equation, m is the mass of the particle and v is its velocity.
